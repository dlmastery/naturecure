> **For you, in plain words.** This is a safe, OTC, at-home plan for **waking through the night** — the 3 AM eyes-open hour, the four-times-a-night bathroom run, the wake-up-soaked-in-sweat hour, the snore-gasp-jolt-awake cycle. **Sister dossier** to sleep-onset: sleep-onset is "I can't fall asleep"; night-waking is "I fall asleep fine but I'm wide awake at 03:15." Mechanisms partly overlap (hyperarousal, circadian, environment) and partly diverge (OSA hypoxic micro-arousals, nocturnal hypoglycemia adrenaline wakes, RLS/PLMD, nocturia, GERD, perimenopausal vasomotor). ## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What night-waking actually is

> **For you, in plain words.** Night-waking (maintenance insomnia + WASO — wake-after-sleep-onset >30 min, or ≥3 awakenings per night, or final wake ≥60 min before alarm) is a measurable pattern with **eight distinguishable root causes** that need different fixes. Most people are treating it as one thing and missing the cause. *(Bastien 2001 ISI; Carney 2012 sleep diary; Riemann 2010 hyperarousal)*

### 1.2 The eight drivers — at a glance

> **Quick start.** Tonight: bedroom 18–20°C + screens off 21:00 + magnesium glycinate 400 mg + glycine 3 g + no caffeine after 14:00 + no alcohol within 3 h of bed + a snack with protein + slow-carb at 21:30 if your 03:00 wake feels adrenaline-rich. This week: start a 14-day consensus core sleep diary (Carney 2012), borrow a Wellue O2Ring or Lookee overnight pulse-oximeter for two nights to screen for obstructive sleep apnea, score STOP-BANG, score ISI + PSQI, and decide which of the eight sub-types in §2.6 fits you. Match the sub-type before you pick the lever. *(Bastien 2001; Buysse 1989; Carney 2012; Chung STOP-BANG 2008)*

### 1.3 The deeper layer

The mainstream story prescribes a Z-drug or benzodiazepine and calls it done — both carry dependence, fall-risk and complex-sleep-behaviour profiles, fragment architecture further, and don't address root cause. A more honest reframe: chronic night-waking is a stack of interacting drivers — conditioned cognitive hyperarousal returning at micro-arousals, undiagnosed OSA hypoxic events, RLS/PLMD often iron-driven, nocturia from BPH/OAB/late-fluid/apnea-via-ANP, nocturnal hypoglycemia adrenaline wakes, alcohol REM-rebound, perimenopausal vasomotor, GERD reflux. A home protocol pairing CBT-i adapted for fragmentation (A-grade) with sub-type fixes (OSA screen + ferritin + alcohol + nocturia audit + 21:30 protein snack) addresses all eight without dependence. **Synthesis.** Maintenance insomnia ≠ sleep-onset insomnia — match sub-type, fix cause.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (8-node)

### 2.1 Eight causes — the picture

> **For you, in plain words.** Eight causes drive fragmented night-waking — cognitive hyperarousal returning mid-night, OSA hypoxic micro-arousals, RLS/PLMD limb movements, nocturia, nocturnal hypoglycemia, alcohol REM-rebound, perimenopausal vasomotor surges, and GERD reflux. Pick the one or two that fit you before you pick the lever. *(Riemann 2010; AASM 2014; Allen 2014; ICSD-3; Ebrahim 2013)*

### 2.2 Cognitive hyperarousal returning

> **For you, in plain words.** You wake briefly at 03:15 (normal — everyone does at every REM-to-NREM transition), and your brain instantly switches on — "what time is it, will I sleep, will tomorrow be ruined." The cognitive engine that wouldn't switch off at bedtime now won't switch off at 03:15. CBT-i cognitive restructuring + paradoxical intention + body-scan return-to-sleep technique address this directly. *(Espie 2002; Harvey 2002 cognitive model)*

#### Open the science

Harvey 2002 cognitive model: the brief micro-arousals that punctuate normal sleep architecture (every 90–120 min at REM-NREM transitions) become problematic in fragmented insomnia because the awareness threshold drops + the cognitive-arousal cascade fires. Monitoring for the clock, catastrophising about the next day, sleep-effort, and "I have to get back to sleep" pressure all extend the brief wake into a prolonged one. Riemann 2010 hyperarousal model: insomnia patients show elevated EEG beta-band activity, elevated cortisol, elevated brain glucose metabolism at night. Targeted by **CBT-i cognitive restructuring** (Espie 2002; Harvey 2002), **paradoxical intention** ("try to stay awake" — Espie 1989), **body-scan return-to-sleep technique** (Yoga Nidra; Kumar 2008), **the 4-7-8 breath at the wake** (parasympathetic re-engagement), and the **clock-cover rule** (cover or turn the clock; never check time during wake). **Synthesis.** This is the most common single mechanism in chronic fragmented insomnia; the same CBT-i toolkit from sleep-onset works here.

### 2.3 OSA hypoxic micro-arousals

> **For you, in plain words.** Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is when your throat collapses repeatedly during sleep, you stop breathing for 10–60 seconds, oxygen drops, the brain emergency-wakes you. You may not consciously remember the wakes — but you wake unrefreshed, your partner hears the snore-gasp pattern, you feel exhausted at 14:00. Screen first with STOP-BANG + a $150 Wellue O2Ring two-night oximetry trial. If positive, the home protocol pauses and clinic-route is genuinely appropriate. *(Chung STOP-BANG 2008; AASM 2014; Berry 2017 oximetry)*

#### Open the science

OSA prevalence is dramatically under-diagnosed: AASM estimates 80%+ of moderate-severe OSA cases are unrecognised. Mechanism: pharyngeal muscle tone falls in NREM/REM, airway collapses, no airflow despite respiratory effort, SpO2 falls, CO2 rises, sympathetic surge, brief cortical arousal restores tone. Repeated cycle: 5–60+ events/hour. Apnea-hypopnea index (AHI): 5–14 mild, 15–29 moderate, ≥30 severe. **Home screening:** STOP-BANG questionnaire (Snoring, Tired, Observed-apnea, BP, BMI, Age, Neck circ, Gender) ≥3 = moderate risk, ≥5 = high risk. Overnight oximetry with consumer ring pulse-oximeter (Wellue O2Ring, Lookee, Viatom) — look at oxygen desaturation index (ODI 4%) and SpO2 nadir + minutes-below-90% (T90). ODI >5/hr or SpO2 nadir <88% strongly suggests OSA — escalate to home polysomnography via Lofta/WatchPAT in §10.7.3. **Home positional therapy** (Night Shift, Philips NightBalance, tennis-ball-in-T-shirt for supine-dominant OSA), **consumer mandibular advancement devices** (SnoreRx Plus, Zyppah, VitalSleep — B-grade for mild positional OSA but C-grade for moderate-severe), **weight loss 5–10%** (single highest-leverage non-CPAP intervention if BMI elevated). **Clinic route §10.7.3** for confirmed moderate-severe OSA: formal in-lab or home polysomnography, CPAP/APAP titration (A-grade), Inspire hypoglossal nerve stimulation implant for select non-CPAP-tolerant patients. **Synthesis.** OSA is the single most-missed driver of fragmented night sleep — screen first.

### 2.4 RLS / PLMD limb movements

> **For you, in plain words.** Restless legs syndrome (RLS) — crawling, jittery, irresistible urge to move legs at rest in the evening, relieved by movement, worse in the second half of the night. Periodic limb movement disorder (PLMD) — leg twitches every 20–40 s during sleep that you may not notice but your partner does, fragmenting sleep architecture. Both respond strongly to **ferritin correction** (get >75 ng/mL) + magnesium + reviewing dopamine-antagonist medications. *(Allen 2014 IRLSSG guidelines; Trotti 2017 iron for RLS)*

#### Open the science

RLS pathophysiology: central iron deficiency in substantia nigra dopaminergic neurons + impaired iron transport across blood-brain barrier even when serum ferritin appears "normal." Allen 2014 IRLSSG guidelines: target serum ferritin >75 ng/mL (some sources >100) for RLS. PLMD — repetitive 0.5–10 s limb movements every 5–90 s during sleep; often comorbid with RLS but can occur alone. **Home approach:** DTC ferritin via LetsGetChecked / Walk-In Lab / Thriva UK first; if <75, **oral iron bisglycinate 25–50 mg + vitamin C 500 mg every other day** (Stoffel 2017 — alternate-day dosing improves absorption) — re-test ferritin at week 12. Magnesium glycinate 400 mg pre-bed (Hornyak 1998 small RCT signal for RLS). Audit medications that worsen RLS: SSRIs, antihistamines like diphenhydramine, dopamine antagonists like metoclopramide, some antipsychotics. Audit caffeine + alcohol + tobacco (all worsen RLS). Compression sleeves at night (Lettieri 2009). Pneumatic compression devices. **Clinic route §10.7.3** for ferritin-corrected non-responsive severe RLS: dopamine agonists (ropinirole, pramipexole — augmentation risk after months-years), gabapentin enacarbil (Horizant), IV iron infusion (Allen 2011) if oral iron fails. **Synthesis.** Measure ferritin before treating RLS as anything else — most resolves on iron correction.

### 2.5 Nocturia waking

> **For you, in plain words.** Waking ≥2× per night to urinate (nocturia) is its own driver of fragmented sleep — and OSA itself causes nocturia through atrial natriuretic peptide release. Bladder-diary 3 days, audit late fluids + diuretics + caffeine + alcohol, screen for BPH (men >50) or overactive bladder, screen for OSA. *(Asplund 2002; Umlauf 2004 OSA→nocturia)*

#### Open the science

Nocturia mechanism — multifactorial. Causes: (1) global polyuria (diabetes, primary polydipsia), (2) nocturnal polyuria (CHF, OSA via ANP, late fluid load, evening alcohol diuresis), (3) reduced bladder capacity (BPH, overactive bladder, interstitial cystitis), (4) sleep disorders (OSA, insomnia making the user *more aware* of the bladder fullness signal). **Home approach:** 3-day bladder diary (intake volumes + timing; voided volumes + timing; wakes-to-void). Audit: cut fluids 3 h pre-bed (especially diuretic fluids — alcohol, coffee, herbal teas with horsetail/dandelion). For men >50 with weak stream, hesitancy, post-void dribble + nocturia: International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) self-quiz, see prostate-wellness dossier (saw palmetto, beta-sitosterol, pumpkin seed oil, lifestyle audit). For overactive bladder: pelvic-floor PT (Kegel + biofeedback apps like Squeezy, Elvie Trainer), bladder retraining, reduce bladder irritants (caffeine, alcohol, acidic juices, artificial sweeteners). For confirmed OSA contribution: treat OSA first — nocturia often resolves on CPAP. **Synthesis.** Bladder diary first; OSA second; lifestyle audit third; clinic route only if persistent.

### 2.6 Nocturnal hypoglycemia + alcohol rebound

> **For you, in plain words.** Two specific patterns wake people around 03:00 with adrenaline. (a) **Nocturnal hypoglycemia** — blood sugar dips low at 02:00–04:00, the body releases cortisol + adrenaline to mobilise glucose, you wake heart-racing. (b) **Alcohol REM-rebound** — alcohol suppresses REM in the first half of the night; as it metabolises out, REM rebounds intensely in the second half with vivid-anxious dreams + wakes. CGM (Stelo, FreeStyle Libre) reveals nocturnal hypoglycemia in non-diabetics. Audit alcohol. *(Hardy 2013 nocturnal hypoglycemia; Ebrahim 2013 alcohol-REM)*

#### Open the science

Nocturnal hypoglycemia outside diabetes — under-recognised cause of 03:00–04:00 adrenaline wakes. Pattern: high-glycemic dinner or dessert → insulin spike → glucose drops below ~70 mg/dL by 03:00 → counter-regulatory adrenaline + cortisol → tachycardia + sweat + sudden alert wake. **Home screening:** consumer CGM 14-day patch (Stelo OTC US, FreeStyle Libre 3 prescription in most markets, Dexcom Stelo, Sibio) — look for overnight glucose nadirs and correlate with wake-times in a sleep diary. **Fix:** **protein + slow-carb snack at 21:30** if 03:00 adrenaline wakes are reproducible (Greek yoghurt + walnuts; cottage cheese + berries; nut butter on apple). Cut dessert. Move dinner earlier. Address insulin resistance if present (see insulin-resistance-support dossier). **Alcohol-REM rebound** — alcohol's H1-antihistamine sedation suppresses REM in the first half of night; metabolism (~1 unit/hour) clears the suppression by 02:00–04:00, REM rebounds intensely, vivid dreams + frequent micro-arousals + HRV crashes 15–25% for 12–24h (Ebrahim 2013). **Fix:** 30-day alcohol pause is the highest-leverage single change for fragmented sleep. If not pausing fully, stop ≥3 h before bed. **Synthesis.** Adrenaline wake at 03:00 = check sugar + alcohol first.

### 2.7 Perimenopausal vasomotor wakes

> **For you, in plain words.** Hot flushes + night sweats wake women in perimenopause and menopause repeatedly. Sleep architecture is disrupted by the temperature surge itself + the wake reaction to the discomfort. Cooling mattress pad + Sheetali pranayama + black cohosh + Pycnogenol + saffron + magnolia bark all have signal. *(Modabbernia 2012 saffron; Geller 2009 black cohosh; see menopause-perimenopause dossier)*

#### Open the science

Vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes + night sweats) are the most common cause of fragmented sleep in women 45–55. Mechanism: oestrogen withdrawal → narrowed thermoneutral zone in hypothalamic preoptic area → minor temperature shifts trigger massive vasodilation + sweating + heart-rate increase + brief awakening. **Home approach:** active cooling — Chilipad/Eight Sleep Pod/BedJet (cools mattress surface to 12–18°C), wicking bedding (TENCEL, bamboo, performance linen), keep bedroom 18°C, Sheetali pranayama (cooling tongue-curl breath, 5 min × 2 daily). **Supplements:** saffron 30 mg/day (Modabbernia 2012 — anti-anxiety + mood + vasomotor signal), Pycnogenol 100 mg/day (Yang 2007), magnolia bark 250 mg pre-bed (Mucci 2006), black cohosh 40 mg (Geller 2009 — modest signal; some hepatic-safety caveats). Phytoestrogens (soy isoflavones, red clover) signal mixed. Cross-reference the menopause-perimenopause dossier in this library for the full protocol. **Synthesis.** Cool the bedroom; cool the body; address the menopause transition holistically.

### 2.8 GERD reflux + cervicogenic positional wakes

> **For you, in plain words.** Reflux that you don't consciously notice can still wake you — acid hits the larynx, you cough or aspirate briefly, you wake. Similarly, a pillow too high or too low + a cervicogenic headache pattern can wake you with neck pain you didn't have at lights-out. Audit dinner timing + bed-head elevation + pillow geometry. *(Fass 2009 GERD-sleep; see reflux-support dossier)*

#### Open the science

GERD-sleep bidirectional relationship: nocturnal reflux fragments sleep; fragmented sleep increases acid sensitivity. **Home approach:** last meal ≥3 h before bed, no late-evening alcohol or chocolate or spicy/acidic food, elevate head of bed 6–8 inches (NOT just extra pillows — that bends the neck and worsens reflux; use bed-risers under the head-of-bed legs or a wedge pillow), left-side-down sleeping (right-side-down worsens reflux), 5–10% weight loss if BMI elevated. See reflux-support dossier for the full protocol (DGL licorice, ginger, slippery elm, marshmallow root, alginate raft formulas like Gaviscon Advance). **Cervicogenic positional wakes** — pillow audit: cervical-support pillow (loft 4–6 inches for back-sleepers, 5–7 inches for side-sleepers), avoid stomach sleeping (extreme cervical rotation), water-based adjustable pillow (Mediflow, Chiroflow). **Synthesis.** Two cheap audits — dinner timing + pillow geometry — fix a surprising number of "unexplained" 02:00–04:00 wakes.

## 3. OTC Phytochemicals — Pharmacological Validation

### 3.1 What to take, what to skip

> **For you, in plain words.** Ten over-the-counter molecules support fragmented-night sleep — but no supplement replaces CBT-i, OSA screening, or ferritin correction. Read the plain-English column first. Notice the honest Cochrane caveats on melatonin (works circadian, not primary adult insomnia) and valerian (mixed). *(Trauer 2015; Cochrane Buscemi 2005; Bent 2006)*

### 3.2 Magnesium + glycine — the cornerstone pair

> **For you, in plain words.** Magnesium glycinate 400 mg 60 min before bed (Abbasi 2012 — Iranian RCT in elderly insomnia improved both onset AND fragmentation indices). Glycine 3 g 30–60 min before bed (Yamadera 2007 — Japan; improved subjective sleep quality + reduced daytime sleepiness). Both cheap, safe, B-grade. *(Abbasi 2012; Yamadera 2007)*

#### Open the science

Magnesium: cofactor for >300 enzymes; modulates NMDA receptors; relaxes smooth + skeletal muscle; precursor for melatonin synthesis; deficient state strongly correlates with PLMD signal (Hornyak 1998). Abbasi 2012 (Iranian RCT, n=46 elderly insomnia, 500 mg/day for 8 weeks, improved ISI + serum cortisol + sleep onset latency + WASO). Boyle 2017 review — B-grade signal especially when serum magnesium is low. Form matters: glycinate (gentle on gut, neuroactive amino-acid co-component), threonate (crosses blood-brain barrier — Slutsky 2010), citrate (laxative effect — useful if constipated). Glycine: NMDA glycine-site modulator + thermoregulatory drop in core body temperature (same physiological signal that initiates sleep + the signal that re-initiates sleep at the wake). Yamadera 2007 (Japan, n=15, 3 g 30 min pre-bed, improved subjective sleep quality + reduced daytime sleepiness). Bannai 2012 (n=19 — improved next-day fatigue + cognition). **Grade B** for both, working for both onset AND fragmentation.

## 4. Traditional Medicine Integration

### 4.1 Ayurveda — Anidra & home Shirodhara

> **For you, in plain words.** Ayurveda calls fragmented sleep *Anidra* (specifically the *vata*-prakopa pattern with broken sleep) and treats it with nourishing herbs, warm-oil head + foot massage, and home approximations of Shirodhara (oil-pour to forehead). ## 5. Biophysical Interventions — home-administered only

### 5.2 Sleep tracking + OSA screening

> **For you, in plain words.** Oura Ring, Whoop, Eight Sleep Pod, Apple Watch Sleep, RingConn, Fitbit, Garmin — all give awakenings + WASO + sleep-stage estimates. ## 6. Dietary Protocol

### 6.1 Mandatory foods + timing

> **For you, in plain words.** Eight foods that physically support fragmented-sleep recovery — protein + slow-carb at dinner, tart cherry, kiwi, magnesium-rich greens, chamomile tea, fatty fish, walnuts, AND a small protein-slow-carb snack at 21:30 if you have 03:00 adrenaline wakes. Timing matters more than amount. *(Lin 2011 kiwi sleep; Howatson 2012 tart cherry; Hardy 2013 nocturnal hypoglycemia)*

- **Tryptophan-rich protein at dinner (60–90 g protein)** — turkey, chicken, eggs, salmon, pumpkin seeds, tofu
- **Tart cherry (Montmorency) 30 ml concentrate** — endogenous melatonin source *(Howatson 2012)*
- **2 kiwi fruit 1h pre-bed** — Lin 2011 Taiwan RCT improved both onset AND total sleep
- **Magnesium-rich greens at dinner** — spinach, kale, chard, pumpkin seeds
- **Slow carb at dinner (40–60 g)** — sweet potato, brown rice, quinoa (raises tryptophan-to-LNAA ratio + sustains overnight glucose)
- **Chamomile tea 60 min pre-bed** — gentle apigenin GABAergic *(Zick 2011)*
- **Fatty fish 120 g × 3–4/week** — omega-3 EPA/DHA membrane support
- **21:30 protein + slow-carb mini-snack (only if 03:00 adrenaline wakes)** — Greek yoghurt + walnuts, cottage cheese + berries, or nut butter on apple

### 10.2 Supplement × supplement

- Magnesium + calcium at same dose: ≥2 h gap (divalent competition)
- Iron + tea/coffee polyphenols: ≥1 h gap (tannin chelation)
- Iron + zinc + calcium + magnesium: separate by 2 h (divalent cation competition)
- Glycine + L-theanine: complementary, safe to combine pre-bed
- Multiple sedating herbs together: start single, add one at a time
- Ashwagandha + magnolia bark: gentle synergy; both fine evening

### 10.3 Food × supplement

- Magnesium with food OK or pre-bed empty stomach OK (form-dependent)
- Iron + vit C boost; iron + tea/coffee/dairy block; alternate-day dosing optimises absorption (Stoffel 2017)
- Vitamin D + fat for absorption
- Tryptophan-LNAA ratio improved by carb at dinner
- Ashwagandha + alcohol → avoid same evening (CNS depression)

### 10.4 Caffeine × fragmentation

> **For you, in plain words.** Caffeine half-life is 5–6 h, longer with CYP1A2 slow-metabolizer genotype. A 14:00 espresso has 50% strength at 20:00 — and the metabolites disrupt sleep architecture (specifically slow-wave + REM) even when you "fall asleep fine." Cut by 14:00 minimum; some genotypes need 12:00. Tolerance is a myth for objective sleep — Drake 2013 showed disrupted PSG even in self-described "caffeine doesn't affect me" subjects. *(Drake 2013; Cornelis 2006 CYP1A2)*

### 10.5 Alcohol × fragmentation

> **For you, in plain words.** One drink drops HRV 15–25% for 12–24 h and triggers REM-rebound wakes at 02:00–04:00 in the second half of the night. The single highest-leverage change for fragmented sleep is a 30-day alcohol pause + measure ISI + tracker WASO before and after. *(Ebrahim 2013)*

### 10.6 Exercise × fragmentation

> **For you, in plain words.** Daily movement supports sleep depth (Kredlow 2015 meta). HIIT before 19:00 OK; later HIIT raises core temp + cortisol and pushes wakes later in the night. *(Kredlow 2015)*

### 10.8 Red-flag escalation

> **For you, in plain words.** **Stop the home protocol and seek immediate clinical care** if: (1) bed-partner has witnessed you stop breathing repeatedly + you are falling asleep at the wheel (treat as safety emergency — stop driving, get a home sleep test this week, see §10.7.3); (2) acute sleeplessness >5 days in someone with bipolar history (mania risk); (3) psychotic features; (4) active suicidal ideation (crisis lines at top of dossier); (5) severe restless legs unresponsive to iron correction; (6) parasomnia with risk of self-harm (sleepwalking with injury); (7) sudden-onset narcolepsy with cataplexy; (8) suspected cardiac arrhythmia driving wakes (palpitations + chest pain + dyspnoea); (9) depression with insomnia + suicidal ideation (depression + insomnia is a high-risk combination — Pigeon 2017). Driving safety is non-negotiable.

## 11. Self-Administered Clinical Scoring

### 11.1 The five-instrument home assessment

> **For you, in plain words.** Five tools — ISI every 4 weeks, PSQI monthly, 14-day consensus core sleep diary at baseline + week 8, ACTH cognitive-arousal scale, and tracker WASO/awakenings daily. STOP-BANG once at start to triage OSA. *(Bastien 2001; Buysse 1989; Carney 2012; Nicassio 1985 ACTH; Chung 2008)*

### 11.2 Insomnia Severity Index (ISI)

> **For you, in plain words.** 7-item self-quiz; total 0–28. 0–7 no clinically significant insomnia; 8–14 subthreshold; 15–21 moderate; 22–28 severe. Re-score every 4 weeks. *(Bastien 2001)*

### 11.3 Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)

> **For you, in plain words.** 19-item global sleep quality assessment; total 0–21; ≥5 poor sleep quality. Components include sleep disturbance which is the fragmentation marker. Re-score monthly. *(Buysse 1989)*

### 11.4 Two-week consensus core sleep diary

> **For you, in plain words.** Daily entries: bedtime, sleep onset latency, AWAKENINGS COUNT, WAKE-AFTER-SLEEP-ONSET MINUTES, final wake time, total sleep, subjective quality 1–5, caffeine, alcohol, naps, exercise. Sleep efficiency = total sleep / time-in-bed × 100. Under 85% triggers sleep restriction therapy. *(Carney 2012)*

### 11.5 ACTH (Adapted Cognitive Activity in Sleep at Night)

> **For you, in plain words.** Tracks racing-mind content at wakes; helps map sub-type and CBT-i target. *(Nicassio 1985)*

### 11.6 STOP-BANG (one-time OSA triage)

> **For you, in plain words.** 8-item OSA screen at start of dossier work. ≥3 = moderate risk → home oximetry; ≥5 = high risk → home polysomnography. *(Chung 2008)*

> **For you, in plain words.** Repeat ISI + sleep diary + tracker WASO. If DSPS confirmed, add bright-light morning + low-dose melatonin 0.3–0.5 mg timed (4–6 h before desired sleep). Add tart cherry + kiwi food layer + Yoga Nidra 3×/week. Re-check O2Ring if borderline. *(Lack 2007)*

> **For you, in plain words.** Hold the wins. Re-test ISI quarterly. Maintain CBT-i behaviours as identity, not protocol. Most users no longer need pre-bed supplements after 6 months of established sleep efficiency >85% and WASO <30 min.
