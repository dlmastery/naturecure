## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What kidney stones actually are

**For you, in plain words.** Kidney stones are crystals that grow in the urine when one of three things goes wrong: too much of the stone-forming chemicals (calcium, oxalate, uric acid, cystine, phosphate), not enough of the stone-blockers (citrate, magnesium), or not enough water to dilute everything. The most common stone is **calcium oxalate** — about 75-80% of all stones. The protocol works upstream of the crystal.

**Quick start.** Drink to make 2-2.5 L of pale urine per day. Eat normal calcium WITH every meal (300-400 mg/meal — Greek yogurt, paneer, sardines). Cut sodium to under 2300 mg/day. Squeeze half a lemon into 2-3 glasses of water daily. Catch the next stone you pass and send it for analysis.

#### Open the science

Lifetime prevalence ~10% industrialised; rising. Pathogenesis: urinary supersaturation → nucleation → crystal growth on Randall's plaque → stone retention. **CaOx** (whewellite + weddellite) 75-80%; **CaP** (apatite/brushite — alkaline urine, distal RTA, PHPT) 10-15%; **uric acid** 5-10% (acidic pH <5.5 + insulin-resistance pattern); **struvite** 5-10% (urea-splitting bacteria → alkaline urine → staghorn); **cystine** <1% (SLC3A1/SLC7A9 genetic — lifelong nephrology). Each type has its own urinary signature — hence 24-h urine collection is the most important diagnostic.

### 1.2 The five drivers — at a glance

**For you, in plain words.** Five engines drive recurrence — dehydration, urine chemistry (high calcium, oxalate, uric acid; low citrate, magnesium), diet (too much sodium, animal protein, sugar; not enough plant alkalinity), gut absorption (Crohn's, bariatric, fat-malabsorption raises absorbed oxalate), and systemic disease (parathyroid, RTA, gout, obesity, T2D).

**Quick start.** Hit all five. Hydrate. Get a 24-hour urine collection (Litholink DTC) to read your own chemistry. DASH-Mediterranean diet with calcium-from-food. Treat the gut if IBD or post-bariatric. Screen comorbidities once.

#### Open the science

The five-driver framework: (i) **dehydration** (Borghi 1996); (ii) **urine chemistry** (hypercalciuria, hyperoxaluria, hyperuricosuria, hypocitraturia, hypomagnesuria, pH out of range); (iii) **diet** (high Na, high animal protein, high fructose, low-Ca-from-food trap); (iv) **gut absorption** (fat-malabsorption → enteric hyperoxaluria); (v) **systemic disease** (PHPT, distal RTA, sarcoidosis, gout).

**Synthesis.** A home protocol that pairs **2.5-3 L/d fluid + DASH-Mediterranean with Ca-from-food + low-Na + lemon-water + Mg-citrate + 24-h urine + stone-type analysis + comorbidity screen** hits all five drivers. Genetic disease (cystinuria, PH1, Dent's) requires lifelong nephrology.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (5-node)

### 2.1 Five drivers — the picture

**For you, in plain words.** Naming each driver lets every food, supplement, and habit aim at the right one. *(Worcester & Coe 2010 NEJM nephrolithiasis review; Pearle 2014 EAU/AUA stone guideline framework)*

**Quick start.** Don't memorise. Just remember: every intervention maps to one of these five, and the master 24-hour plan in §8.5 weaves them together.

#### Open the science

The five-node framework is the working model in non-US integrative nephrology (EAU urolithiasis guideline, TGA/NHMRC, AYUSH CCRAS urinary monographs, China NMPA, Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020). Each node has its own primary literature, its own intervention class, and its own measurable urinary chemistry — making it the most quantifiable preventive medicine domain in the entire NicheCore atlas.

### 2.2 Dehydration & low urine volume

**For you, in plain words.** If your urine volume is below 2 L/day, every chemical in it is more concentrated, and crystals form. Borghi 1996 randomised stone-formers to extra fluid vs ad-lib drinking — the extra-fluid arm halved their recurrence over 5 years. This is the single most important home move. *(Borghi 1996; Hooton 2018)*

**Quick start.** Drink to keep urine pale. Aim for ~3 L of total fluid intake, ~2.5 L of urine output. Drink steadily (250 mL every hour you are awake). Add an extra glass before bed (only if not waking to urinate excessively) — overnight urine concentration is highest.

#### Open the science

Borghi 1996 (Italian RCT n=199 first-time idiopathic stone-formers, 5-y): high-fluid arm (≥2 L urine/d) ~12% recurrence vs ~27% control = ~55% relative reduction. Hooton 2018 JAMA Intern Med (n=140 UTI analog) confirmed ~1.5 L/d extra water cut recurrence ~50% (mechanism: urinary-flush + dilution). Saturation index (SS-CaOx, SS-CaP, SS-UA) is a direct function of concentration — doubling volume halves SS. Targeted by **plain water + citrus-infused water + herbal teas (hibiscus, nettle, dandelion mutrala) + coconut water + diluted vegetable broths**, and avoidance of dehydration drivers (high Na, sauna without rehydration, hot occupational, intense exercise without replacement).

### 2.3 Urine chemistry & supersaturation

**For you, in plain words.** Stones form when one of six urinary chemicals is too high (calcium, oxalate, uric acid, cystine, phosphate) or two are too low (citrate, magnesium), and the pH is in the wrong window. A 24-hour urine collection (Litholink DTC mail-kit) measures all of these on one report. *(Pearle 2014 EAU; Litholink methodology)*

**Quick start.** Order a Litholink 24-hour urine collection kit (DTC, $200-400 depending on country). Collect for 24 hours, mail back. The report shows you which chemical is high or low. Then the protocol becomes specific.

#### Open the science

The 24-h urine panel reports: volume (target ≥2 L/d), Ca (<250 mg/d women, <300 men), oxalate (<40), uric acid (<750-800), citrate (≥320 — the major stone-blocker), Mg (≥50), Na (<2300 — diet proxy), sulfate (animal-protein proxy), phosphate, pH (CaOx 6.0-6.5; uric-acid 6.5-7.0; CaP 6.0 or below — opposite direction; cystine 7.0-7.5). Supersaturation indices (SS-CaOx, SS-CaP, SS-UA) integrate concentration + pH into a single risk number. Targeted by **fluid volume** (denominator), **Ca-from-food** (binds gut oxalate), **low-Na** (reduces calciuresis), **low animal protein** (reduces uric acid + sulfate + acid load), **DASH-style citrate-rich diet** (raises citrate — the most powerful endogenous inhibitor), **lemon water** (Penniston 2007), **Mg citrate/glycinate** (complexes oxalate + raises Mg), **B6** for PH1.

### 2.4 Diet — sodium, animal protein, fructose, calcium-paradox

**For you, in plain words.** Four diet levers move urine chemistry the most. **Cut sodium hard** (high sodium spills calcium into urine — Massey 2005). **Reduce animal protein** (high animal protein raises uric acid + sulfate + acid load that pulls calcium out of bone into urine). **Cut fructose** (sugary drinks raise uric acid and stone risk per Taylor 2008). **Keep normal calcium-from-food** — counter-intuitively, low-calcium diets RAISE stone risk because dietary calcium binds oxalate in the gut. *(Massey 2005; Borghi 2002 DASH; Curhan 2002; Taylor 2008)*

**Quick start.** Read sodium labels — target <2300 mg/day, ideally <1500 mg/day. Replace some red meat with legumes + fish. Stop soda + fruit juice. Take 300-400 mg calcium WITH each meal (Greek yogurt, paneer/cottage cheese, sardines with bones, fortified plant milk).

#### Open the science

Massey 2005 (review): high-Na intake increases urinary Ca 1:1 via Na-Ca co-transport — every additional 100 mmol Na (2.3 g) raises urinary Ca ~20-40 mg. Borghi 2002 (Italian RCT n=120 idiopathic hypercalciuria recurrent stone-formers, 5-y): low-Na + normal-Ca + reduced-animal-protein DASH-style diet vs traditional low-Ca diet — DASH arm halved recurrence (~20% vs ~40%). Curhan 2002 (HPFS + NHS pooled n>200,000): high dietary Ca-from-FOOD INVERSELY associated with stone risk; Ca supplements between meals slightly increase risk. Mechanism: dietary Ca binds gut oxalate → insoluble CaOx stool excretion → less oxalate reaches kidneys; low-Ca → oxalate hyper-absorbed. Taylor 2008 (NHS II + HPFS): sugar-sweetened beverages + high-fructose → higher stone risk (uric-acid + insulin-resistance mechanism). Knight 2010 confirmed fructose signal. Targeted by **DASH-Mediterranean diet**, **lemon-water** (Penniston 2007), **fruit-vegetable expansion** (K-citrate-equivalent alkalinisation), **avoidance of sugary drinks**, **moderate animal protein 0.8-1.0 g/kg**, **reduction of high-oxalate foods ONLY for known CaOx + hyperoxaluria**.

### 2.5 Gut absorption & enteric hyperoxaluria

**For you, in plain words.** If you have Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis with bowel resection, short-bowel syndrome, or post-Roux-en-Y bariatric surgery, fat doesn't absorb well — fatty acids bind calcium in the gut, leaving oxalate free to be hyper-absorbed. The result: enteric hyperoxaluria. Calcium citrate WITH meals (binds oxalate again) plus low-oxalate diet is the home fix. *(Witting 2021 enteric hyperoxaluria review)*

**Quick start.** If you have IBD, short bowel, or post-bariatric — talk to a nephrologist (§10.7.3 honest referral). Take calcium citrate 300-500 mg WITH each meal. Strictly avoid spinach, rhubarb, beets, almonds, peanut butter, cashews, dark chocolate, soy, dark tea. Hydrate aggressively.

#### Open the science

Enteric-hyperoxaluria mechanism: fat malabsorption → luminal FFA saponify with dietary Ca → Ca not free to bind oxalate → oxalate hyper-absorbed across colon (bile-salt-mediated permeability ↑). Urinary oxalate >80 mg/d (vs normal <40). Witting 2021 CJASN: Crohn's + ileal resection, modern RYGB +25-30% lifetime stone risk vs sleeve (lower), cystic fibrosis, chronic pancreatitis. Targeted by **Ca citrate 300-500 mg WITH meals** (re-binds gut oxalate + alkalinises urine), **strict low-oxalate diet**, **aggressive hydration**, **probiotic + *Oxalobacter formigenes* repletion** (Oxabact EU trials), **B6 50-100 mg**, **MCT-oil substitution** in severe malabsorption (does not bind Ca).

### 2.6 Systemic disease

**For you, in plain words.** A small but important fraction of stone-formers have an underlying systemic disease — primary hyperparathyroidism (PTH gland tumour), distal renal tubular acidosis, sarcoidosis, gout, uncontrolled diabetes, cystinuria (genetic), primary hyperoxaluria (genetic), Dent's disease (genetic). Screen once with a basic blood panel + 24-hour urine. *(Worcester & Coe 2010)*

**Quick start.** Order a DTC blood panel: calcium, phosphate, intact PTH, uric acid, creatinine, eGFR, vitamin D, HbA1c, bicarbonate. If anything is abnormal — see §10.7.3 specialist referral.

#### Open the science

Screen once, then as indicated:
- **Primary hyperparathyroidism** — high serum Ca + high/inappropriately normal PTH → endocrinology; parathyroidectomy curative.
- **Distal RTA** — urine pH >5.5 + metabolic acidosis; high CaP + low citrate; nephrology + K-citrate (§10.7.3).
- **Sarcoidosis** — granuloma 1,25-D → hypercalcemia + hypercalciuria.
- **Gout** — high uric acid + uric-acid stones; allopurinol (§10.7.3).
- **Cystinuria** — autosomal-recessive SLC3A1/SLC7A9; urinary cystine >250 mg/g Cr; lifelong nephrology + tiopronin/D-penicillamine (§10.7.3).
- **Primary hyperoxaluria I-III** — AGXT/GRHPR/HOGA1 mutations; childhood severe stones; B6-responsive PH1 G170R subset (Hoyer-Kuhn 2014) + emerging RNAi.
- **Dent's disease** — X-linked CLCN5; tubular proteinuria + hypercalciuria; paediatric nephrology.
- **Uncontrolled T2D + metabolic syndrome** — acidic urine → uric-acid stones; cross-link `type-2-diabetes-active.md`, `insulin-resistance-support.md`.

**For you, in plain words.** Three things deliberately excluded — high-dose vitamin C (RAISES oxalate stones), cranberry (great for UTI, slightly bad for stones), and calcium supplements taken between meals.

## 5. Biophysical Interventions — home-administered only

## 6. Dietary Protocol

### 6.1 Mandatory foods daily

**For you, in plain words.** Hydration-first eating. DASH-Mediterranean pattern: abundant vegetables and fruits (citrate-rich), normal calcium from food WITH meals, fish more than red meat, low sodium, plant-protein anchoring. Lemon every day. *(Borghi 2002 DASH; Pearle 2014 EAU; AYUSH CCRAS Mutrashmari pathya)*

**Quick start.** Water 2.5-3 L/day. Calcium 1000-1200 mg/day FROM FOOD spread across meals. Sodium <2300 mg/day. Lemon ½-1 fruit/day. Vegetables 400-500 g/day. Fruit 200-300 g/day. Animal protein 0.8-1.0 g/kg total, mostly fish + poultry + legumes.

#### Open the science

Daily targets:
- **Water + fluids** — 2.5-3 L/d; urine pale yellow; spread across day + extra glass before bed.
- **Calcium-from-food** — 1000-1200 mg/d spread across meals (binds dietary oxalate). Greek yogurt 200 g = 250 mg; paneer 100 g = 100-200 mg; sardines-with-bones 100 g = 380 mg; calcium-set tofu 150 g = 350 mg; fortified plant milk 200 mL = 240 mg.
- **Lemon / citrus** — ½-1 lemon in 250 mL water with main meals, OR 1 orange, OR 1 grapefruit (avoid grapefruit with CYP3A4-substrate Rx).
- **Vegetables** — 400-500 g/d varied; boil-and-drain greens if known hyperoxaluria.
- **Fruit** — 200-300 g/d (citrus, apples, melon, berries).
- **Legumes** — 100-150 g cooked/d.
- **Whole grains** — 100-150 g cooked/d (millet, brown rice, barley, oats).
- **Fish + poultry + lean meat** — 100-150 g/d, fish ≥3×/week; total animal protein 0.8-1.0 g/kg.
- **Olive oil** — 30-45 mL/d (Mediterranean anchor).
- **Barley-coriander-fennel water (Ayurvedic mutrala)** — 500-750 mL/d in fluid total.
- **Hibiscus tea (cooled) 1-2 cups/d** — avoid if uric-acid stones (would acidify further).
- **Cucumber, watermelon, ash gourd, lauki** — natural mutrala; summer-favouritise.
- **Banana 1/d** + **coconut water 200-300 mL/d** — potassium-citrate-equivalent.

### 6.2 Absolutely avoid

**For you, in plain words.** Five things drive stones — high sodium (processed food + restaurant food + cured meat), sugary drinks + fructose, megadose vitamin C, very-low-calcium "stone-prevention" diets (paradoxically WORSE), and for known calcium-oxalate stone-formers: the top-eight high-oxalate foods only when oxalate is the documented driver. *(Massey 2005; Taylor 2008; Curhan 1996; Curhan 2002)*

#### Open the science

- **Sodium >2300 mg/d (ideally <1500)** — 100 mmol Na = 20-40 mg urinary calcium. Sources: processed + restaurant + cured-meat + salty snacks + soup + soy sauce + bread.
- **Sugary drinks + high fructose** — Taylor 2008 positive cohort; ditch soda + sweetened juice/tea.
- **Vitamin C >1000 mg/d supplement** — Curhan 1996 + Thomas 2013: 25-100% increased CaOx stones. **Cap supplemental C at RDA (~90 mg/d).** Dietary vit C from fruit/veg is fine.
- **Low-calcium diets** — Curhan 2002 + Borghi 2002 confirm LOW-Ca INCREASES stones (unbound oxalate hyper-absorbed). Aim 1000-1200 mg/d from food.
- **Top-8 high-oxalate foods (ONLY for known CaOx + hyperoxaluria)** — spinach (750 mg/100 g), rhubarb (500), almonds (470), peanut butter (95), beets (100), sweet potato (70), dark chocolate (70), soy (56), dark tea (50). For everyone else, nutritious and not banned.
- **Excess animal protein >1.2 g/kg/d** — raises uric acid + sulfate + acid-load mobilising bone Ca.
- **Cola + dark sodas** — phosphoric acid + fructose.
- **Excessive caffeine without rehydration**; moderate coffee is NEUTRAL (Ferraro 2014 — slightly protective).
- **Alcohol excess**; **aggressive sauna/hot-yoga without rehydration**; **holding urine**; **megadose Ca-only supplements without meals**.

### 6.3 Preparation methodology

**For you, in plain words.** Cooking and draining leafy greens reduces oxalate by 30-90%. Soaking grains and legumes overnight reduces phytate (improves mineral absorption). Pair calcium foods with oxalate-containing foods at the same meal to bind oxalate in the gut.

#### Open the science

- **Boil-and-drain green leafy vegetables** (spinach, beet greens, Swiss chard) — reduces oxalate 30-90% by leaching into water (which is discarded). For known oxalate-stone-formers who still want some greens, this is the kitchen lever.
- **Soak legumes overnight** — reduces phytate, improves mineral absorption.
- **Pair calcium with oxalate** — yogurt + berries; paneer + spinach (saag paneer is naturally well-engineered for stone-formers); milk + tea (rather than tea alone); cheese + nuts.
- **Light cooking preserves polyphenols** — citrus juiced fresh, lemon at end of cooking.
- **Use whole lemons rather than bottled juice** when possible — fresh contains more citrate.

### 6.5 Environmental & occupational triggers

**For you, in plain words.** Three big environmental drivers: hot occupational environments (construction, kitchen, foundry, military, military-deployment-to-desert = stone hotspot), chronic dehydration, and exposure to lead-paint / heavy metals (rare but documented stone driver).

**Quick start.** If you work hot — measure intake, force fluid every 30 min, electrolyte drinks (low-sugar). If pregnant — extra fluid, avoid sauna. If on stone-forming medications (indinavir, triamterene, topiramate, sulphadiazine, ceftriaxone) — discuss substitutes via prescribing GP.

#### Open the science

- **Hot occupational** — Borghi 1993 (steel-mills); military desert deployment 2-3× stone incidence.
- **Chronic dehydration occupations** — pilots, long-haul drivers, surgeons, teachers (limited bathroom).
- **Hard vs soft water** — epidemiology mixed; hard water does NOT raise stones (Schwartz 2002).
- **Heavy-metal exposure (lead)** — saturnine gout + stones (historical).
- **Drug-induced stones** — indinavir, triamterene, topiramate, sulphadiazine, ceftriaxone — substitute via prescribing GP.
- **High-sodium processed-food environment** — slowest-to-fix structural driver.

### 10.6 Strictly Avoid — specific to kidney-stone prevention

**For you, in plain words.** Some things directly aggravate stone pathology.

- **High-dose vitamin C supplements (>1 g/day)** — Curhan 1996 + Thomas 2013: 25-100% increase in calcium-oxalate stones. Cap at RDA.
- **Aggressive low-calcium diets** — Curhan 2002 + Borghi 2002: paradoxically increase stones; aim 1000-1200 mg from food.
- **Sugary drinks + high-fructose** — Taylor 2008.
- **Heavy oxalate-loading for known oxalate-stone formers** — top-8 high-oxalate foods listed in §6.2.
- **Chronic dehydration** — single biggest modifiable risk.
- **Holding urine for long stretches** — stasis.
- **Megadose calcium supplements without meals** — Curhan 2002.
- **Cranberry juice as preventive in calcium-oxalate stone-formers** — slight oxalate signal; for UTI use D-mannose instead.
- **"Kidney cleanses" with olive oil + Epsom salt + apple juice** — pseudo-medicine; the "stones" are saponified material.
- **Pregnancy: most herbs untested** — Pashanabheda, Gokshura, Phyllanthus, hibiscus high-dose: use only with obstetric clearance.
- **Lactation**: most OTC herbs caution; consult Hale's Medications & Mothers' Milk.
- **Renal failure / advanced CKD**: do NOT supplement potassium or magnesium without nephrologist; risk hyperkalemia or hypermagnesemia.
