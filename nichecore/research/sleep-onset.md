## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What sleep-onset insomnia actually is

> **For you, in plain words.** Sleep-onset insomnia is a measurable conditioned arousal pattern — your brain has learned that bed = wake. It is fixable behaviourally. Most "insomnia" is really 30-90 minutes of cognitive hyperarousal. *(Spielman 1987; Bootzin 1972)*

### 1.2 The four drivers — at a glance

> **Quick start.** Tonight: bedroom 18-20°C + screens off 21:00 + magnesium glycinate 400 mg + glycine 3 g + no caffeine after 14:00 + bed only when sleepy + out of bed if awake >20 min. This week: download Sleepio or start Bootzin stimulus control + Spielman sleep restriction; book a 2-week sleep diary. *(Trauer 2015 CBT-i meta; Abbasi 2012 Mg; Yamadera 2007 glycine)*

### 1.3 The deeper layer

The mainstream story prescribes a Z-drug (zolpidem) or benzodiazepine and calls it done — both have real dependence, fall-risk, and complex-sleep-behaviour profiles. A more honest reframe (Spielman 3-P model): chronic sleep-onset insomnia emerges from (i) **predisposing** factors (high arousal trait), (ii) **precipitating** event (a stressor that triggered the first sleepless nights), and (iii) **perpetuating** behaviours (lying in bed awake, naps, irregular schedule, sleep-effort, blue light, evening caffeine, evening alcohol). The four mechanistic drivers are: **cognitive + somatic hyperarousal**, **circadian misalignment** (chronotype mismatch, DSPS, shift work), **conditioned bed-wake association** (the bed is now an alarm cue), and **environmental + behavioural perpetuators** (heat, light, caffeine, alcohol, screens, irregular wake time). **Synthesis.** A home protocol that pairs CBT-i (A-grade) with environmental + circadian fixes + targeted supplements addresses all four drivers without dependence risk.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (4-node)

### 2.1 Four causes — the picture

> **For you, in plain words.** Four causes drive sleep-onset insomnia — cognitive/somatic hyperarousal, circadian misalignment, conditioned bed-wake association, and environmental/behavioural perpetuators. CBT-i hits the first three; environmental fixes hit the fourth. *(Spielman 1987; Bootzin 1972; Borbély two-process)*

### 2.2 Cognitive + somatic hyperarousal

> **For you, in plain words.** Your brain stays in alert mode at bedtime — racing thoughts, body tense, can't switch off. Both cognitive (worry, planning) and somatic (muscle tension, fast HR) limbs need targeting. Slow paced breathing + body scan address the somatic limb; CBT-i cognitive restructuring addresses the racing-mind limb. *(Riemann 2010 hyperarousal model; Espie 2002)*

#### Open the science

Hyperarousal model (Riemann 2010): insomnia patients show elevated sympathetic tone, elevated cortisol, elevated EEG beta-band activity, and elevated brain glucose metabolism at sleep onset and through the night. Cognitive hyperarousal manifests as racing thoughts + worry; somatic hyperarousal as muscle tension + elevated HR + low HRV. Targeted by **CBT-i cognitive restructuring** (Espie 2002, Sleepio), **slow paced breathing 5.5-6 bpm** (Lehrer 2014), **Yoga Nidra body-scan** (Khalsa 2014), **progressive muscle relaxation** (Jacobson; McCallie 2006), **mindfulness for insomnia** (Britton 2010), **autogenic training** (Schultz; Stetter 2002 meta), **L-theanine + magnesium + glycine + lavender**. **Synthesis.** This is the biggest single mechanistic lever for primary insomnia.

### 2.3 Circadian misalignment

> **For you, in plain words.** If your biology wants to fall asleep at 02:00 (delayed sleep phase syndrome — DSPS) but you need to wake at 06:30, you're chronically jet-lagged. Bright morning light + timed evening melatonin can shift the curve. *(Czeisler 1999; Burgess melatonin phase response curve)*

#### Open the science

Two-process model (Borbély): sleep is driven by Process S (homeostatic sleep pressure) and Process C (circadian drive). When the circadian drive is delayed — DSPS, shift work, frequent travel, late-evening blue light — Process C tells the brain to be awake when bedtime arrives. Targeted by **bright morning light box** 10,000 lux for 20-30 min within 30 min of desired wake time (cornerstone for DSPS — Lack & Wright 2007), **timed evening melatonin** 0.3-0.5 mg at appropriate phase 4-6h before desired sleep (NOT the 5-10 mg doses sold OTC — Burgess 2010 phase-response curve), **strict wake-time anchor** even on weekends (Eastman 1995), **blue-light blockers after 19:00** (Swannies, RaOptics), **dim red-bulb evening lighting**, **morning sunlight as natural light box**. **Synthesis.** Melatonin works here even though it fails the primary-insomnia Cochrane review — use it for the right indication.

### 2.4 Conditioned bed-wake association

> **For you, in plain words.** Bootzin's classical insight (1972): if you spend hours lying in bed awake, your brain learns "bed = awake". Stimulus control therapy retrains this. The rule is brutal: bed only when sleepy + out of bed if awake >20 min + same wake time daily + no naps. *(Bootzin 1972; Morin 2006)*

#### Open the science

Bootzin's stimulus control therapy and Spielman's sleep restriction therapy are the two behavioural cornerstones of CBT-i. Stimulus control: (1) go to bed only when sleepy, (2) use bed only for sleep + sex, (3) if not asleep within 20 minutes, get up and do something boring in low light, (4) return to bed only when sleepy again, (5) repeat as needed, (6) anchor wake time every day including weekends, (7) no daytime napping. Sleep restriction: temporarily compress time-in-bed to match estimated sleep time (~5-6 hours), build up sleep pressure, then gradually extend as sleep efficiency rises >85%. Both are A-grade interventions, hard but effective. **Synthesis.** Stimulus control + sleep restriction together are the highest-yield behavioural levers in the dossier.

### 2.5 Environmental + behavioural perpetuators

> **For you, in plain words.** Heat, light, noise, caffeine after 14:00, evening alcohol, screens, irregular wake time, late-day exercise — each is removable. Most people can fix their sleep onset by 20-30 minutes just on environment + behaviour. *(Walker 2017; Ebrahim 2013)*

#### Open the science

Bedroom environment determines a measurable proportion of sleep latency and efficiency. Ideal: 18-20°C ambient temperature (Okamoto-Mizuno 2012); blackout-dark (Burgess 2010 light effects on melatonin); low noise (white noise machine masks intermittent disruption); supportive mattress (firmness depends on body composition); cool pillow + cooling mattress pad (Chilipad, Eight Sleep Pod, BedJet). Behavioural: caffeine cut-off by 14:00 (half-life 5-6h — Drake 2013); alcohol cut-off >3h before bed (Ebrahim 2013 — alcohol fragments REM and drops HRV); screen blue-light cut-off 90 min before bed (Hattar 2002 ipRGC); regular bedtime + wake-time within ±30 min (Czeisler 1999); evening exercise no later than 19:00 for most. **Synthesis.** This is the cheapest highest-yield layer — fix it first.

## 3. OTC Phytochemicals — Pharmacological Validation

### 3.1 What to take, what to skip

> **For you, in plain words.** Ten over-the-counter molecules support sleep onset — but no supplement replaces CBT-i + environmental fixes. Read the plain-English column first. Notice the honest Cochrane caveats on melatonin (works for circadian, not primary adult insomnia) and valerian (mixed). *(Trauer 2015; Cochrane Buscemi 2005; Bent 2006)*

### 3.2 Magnesium + glycine — the cornerstone pair

> **For you, in plain words.** Magnesium glycinate 400 mg 60 min before bed (Abbasi 2012 Iranian RCT — improved sleep onset in elderly insomnia). Glycine 3 g 30-60 min before bed (Yamadera 2007 Japan — improved subjective sleep onset + quality + reduced daytime sleepiness). Cheap, safe, B-grade evidence. *(Abbasi 2012; Yamadera 2007)*

#### Open the science

Magnesium: cofactor for >300 enzymes; modulates NMDA receptors; relaxes smooth + skeletal muscle; precursor for melatonin synthesis. Abbasi 2012 (Iranian RCT, n=46 elderly insomnia, 500 mg/day for 8 weeks, improved ISI + serum cortisol + sleep onset latency). Boyle 2017 review — B-grade signal especially when serum Mg is low. Form matters: glycinate (gentle on gut, neuroactive amino-acid co-component), threonate (crosses blood-brain barrier — Slutsky 2010), citrate (laxative effect — useful if constipated). Glycine: NMDA glycine-site modulator + thermoregulatory drop in core body temperature (the same physiological signal that initiates sleep). Yamadera 2007 (Japan, n=15, 3 g 30 min pre-bed, improved subjective sleep onset + quality + reduced daytime sleepiness). Bannai 2012 (n=19 — improved next-day fatigue + cognition). **Grade B** for both.

## 5. Biophysical Interventions — home-administered only

### 5.1 What you'll use this for

> **For you, in plain words.** Six home modalities physically support sleep onset — bedroom temperature engineering, cooling mattress pad, weighted blanket, bright-light box (for circadian shift), blue-light blockers, white noise machine, and home CES (Alpha-Stim consumer device). *(Okamoto-Mizuno 2012; Burgess 2010; Ekholm 2020)*

### 5.2 Sleep tracking — make the problem visible

> **For you, in plain words.** Oura Ring, Whoop, Eight Sleep Pod, Apple Watch Sleep, RingConn, Fitbit Sense, Garmin — all give sleep onset latency + total sleep + sleep stage estimates. Use the trend over weeks, not single nights. *(Henriksen 2020; Chinoy 2021)*

#### Open the science

RingConn — emerging contender with subscription-free model. **Use the trend over 30 days**, not any single night. Avoid orthosomnia (the paradoxical anxiety from over-tracking that disturbs sleep further) — if tracking is making things worse, pause it.

## 11. Self-Administered Clinical Scoring

### 11.1 The four-instrument home assessment

> **For you, in plain words.** Four tools — ISI every 4 weeks, PSQI monthly, 2-week sleep diary at baseline + week 8, Oura/Whoop daily. *(Bastien 2001 ISI; Buysse 1989 PSQI; Carney 2012 sleep diary)*

### 11.2 Insomnia Severity Index (ISI)

> **For you, in plain words.** 7-item self-quiz; total 0-28. 0-7 no clinically significant insomnia; 8-14 subthreshold; 15-21 moderate; 22-28 severe. Re-score every 4 weeks. *(Bastien 2001)*

### 11.3 Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)

> **For you, in plain words.** 19-item global sleep quality assessment; total 0-21; ≥5 poor sleep quality. Re-score monthly. *(Buysse 1989)*

### 11.4 Two-week sleep diary

> **For you, in plain words.** Daily entries: bedtime, wake time, sleep onset latency, awakenings, total sleep, subjective quality 1-5, caffeine, alcohol, naps, exercise. Calculate sleep efficiency = total sleep / time-in-bed × 100. <85% triggers sleep restriction. *(Carney 2012)*

### 11.5 Munich Chronotype Questionnaire (MCTQ) for DSPS

> **For you, in plain words.** Free academic PDF; identifies DSPS / chronotype mismatch. Mid-sleep time on free days (MSFsc) >05:30 = late chronotype; >06:30 = DSPS suspected. *(Roenneberg 2003)*
