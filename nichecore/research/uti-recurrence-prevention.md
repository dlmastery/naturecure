## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What recurrent UTI actually is

**For you, in plain words.** Recurrent UTI means 3+ infections in 12 months or 2+ in 6 months. It affects roughly 20-30% of women who get one UTI. Most are uncomplicated cystitis caused by *E. coli* travelling from the gut to the bladder via the perineum. The cycle is: infection → antibiotics → vaginal microbiome disruption → re-colonisation → next infection. Breaking the cycle is the whole protocol.

**Quick start.** Take D-mannose 2 g/day in 200 mL water (cornerstone). Add cranberry-PAC 36 mg/day. Drink 2-2.5 L water per day. Void within 15 min after sex. Wipe front-to-back. If postmenopausal — discuss topical vaginal hyaluronic-acid (OTC) or estradiol (clinic-route §10.7.3).

#### Open the science

UTI epidemiology: ~50% of women experience at least one UTI in their lifetime; 20-30% of those have recurrence within 6-12 months. *E. coli* causes 75-95% of uncomplicated cystitis. Pathogenesis: gut → perineum → urethra → bladder; uropathogenic *E. coli* (UPEC) adhere via type-1 fimbriae (FimH adhesin) to mannosylated uroplakin on bladder epithelium → intracellular bacterial communities (IBCs) → biofilm → recurrence. D-mannose blocks FimH directly. Cranberry proanthocyanidins (A-type linkages, specifically) inhibit type-1 fimbrial adhesion. The mechanism rationale is strong; the trial evidence depends on standardisation.

### 1.2 The four drivers — at a glance

**For you, in plain words.** Four engines drive recurrence — bacterial adhesion to bladder wall, vaginal microbiome dysbiosis (low *Lactobacillus*), post-menopausal estrogen drop changing tissue, and behavioural/anatomic factors (voiding, wiping, intercourse, holding urine).

**Quick start.** Hit all four. D-mannose for adhesion. *L. crispatus* probiotic for microbiome. Vaginal hyaluronic acid (or §10.7.3 estradiol) if postmenopausal. Postcoital void + front-to-back wiping + no-holding.

#### Open the science

The four-driver framework: (i) **bacterial adhesion** (FimH-mannose pathway — Kranjčec 2014, Lenger 2020; PAC inhibition — Williams 2023 Cochrane positive update); (ii) **vaginal microbiome dysbiosis** — loss of *L. crispatus* dominance allows *E. coli* peri-urethral colonisation (Stapleton 2011 RCT vaginal *L. crispatus* positive); (iii) **postmenopausal GSM** — estrogen drop → vaginal atrophy → pH rise → microbiome shift → UTI risk; topical vaginal estradiol is A-grade for postmenopausal UTI prevention; (iv) **behavioural + anatomic** — postcoital voiding, wiping technique, hydration, urinary retention (pelvic-floor dysfunction, prolapse, BPH in men).

**Synthesis.** A home protocol that pairs **D-mannose + cranberry-PAC + L. crispatus probiotic + hydration + postcoital voiding + (postmenopausal) hyaluronic acid suppository** (the highest-grade frontline) with optional methenamine (clinic-route §10.7.3), behavioural habits, and pelvic-floor PT for those with retention — hits all four drivers in parallel. For the small minority that plateaus, §10.7.3 documents Uromune (EU), continuous antibiotic prophylaxis, self-start, and urology workup honestly.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (4-node)

### 2.1 Four drivers — the picture

**For you, in plain words.** Naming each driver lets every supplement and habit aim at the right one. *(Foxman 2014 UTI epidemiology)*

**Quick start.** Don't memorise. Just remember: every herb and habit maps to one of these four, and the master 24-hour plan in §8.5 weaves them together.

#### Open the science

The four-node framework is the working model in non-US integrative urology (TGA/NHMRC, EMA HMPC, AYUSH CCRAS urinary monographs, China NMPA). Each node has its own primary literature, its own intervention class, and its own measurable behaviour. The skill of building a real protocol is sequencing them.

### 2.2 Bacterial adhesion (FimH pathway)

**For you, in plain words.** *E. coli* sticks to the bladder wall with tiny grippers called type-1 fimbriae. D-mannose looks like the bladder's surface sugar — so the *E. coli* grip mannose instead and get flushed out in urine. Cranberry's PACs block the same grippers a different way. *(Kranjčec 2014; Williams 2023)*

**Quick start.** D-mannose 2 g dissolved in 200 mL water in the morning. Cranberry-PAC 36 mg standardised. Drink to keep urine pale.

#### Open the science

Uropathogenic *E. coli* (UPEC) express type-1 fimbriae with the FimH adhesin at the tip. FimH binds mannosylated uroplakin on bladder umbrella cells, enabling colonisation, intracellular bacterial community (IBC) formation, and biofilm. **D-mannose competitively blocks FimH** — Kranjčec 2014 (n=308, head-to-head non-inferior to nitrofurantoin 50 mg/day prophylaxis, both ~15% recurrence vs ~60% no-prophylaxis); Lenger 2020 meta-analysis positive across 8 studies. **Cranberry A-type proanthocyanidins** (PACs, specifically A-type linkages) inhibit type-1 fimbrial adhesion by conformational change — Williams 2023 Cochrane update found **moderate-quality evidence for cranberry products in preventing recurrent UTI in susceptible women**, a meaningful change from the 2012 null update because it incorporated newer trials with standardised PAC content (typically 36 mg/day cranberry-PAC isolate, often using the BL-DMAC analytical method). **Older cranberry juice trials (Jepson 2012)** were Cochrane-null because doses were tiny and inconsistent — the standardisation matters. Targeted by **D-mannose**, **cranberry-PAC**, **hibiscus** (some studies show urinary acidification + antimicrobial signal — Allaert 2018 RCT signal), **forskolin** (mechanistic data on IBC release).

### 2.3 Vaginal microbiome dysbiosis

**For you, in plain words.** The vagina is dominated by *Lactobacillus* in healthy women — they keep the pH acidic and out-compete *E. coli* on the perineum. Antibiotics, douching, spermicide all knock them down, and *E. coli* takes the empty seat. Re-seeding with *L. crispatus* directly into the vagina works. *(Stapleton 2011)*

**Quick start.** Lactin-V *L. crispatus* vaginal probiotic or oral multi-strain probiotic with *L. rhamnosus GR-1 + L. reuteri RC-14* (Jorgensen protocol). Stop douching, spermicide-coated condoms, diaphragms.

#### Open the science

The vaginal microbiome in healthy reproductive-age women is dominated by *Lactobacillus crispatus*, *L. iners*, *L. gasseri*, *L. jensenii* — producing lactic acid (pH ~4) and H₂O₂, suppressing pathogens. Disruption (antibiotics, douching, spermicide N-9, diaphragms) shifts pH up and allows *E. coli* peri-urethral colonisation, the immediate precursor of cystitis. **Stapleton 2011 RCT (n=100, Lactin-V *L. crispatus* CTV-05 vaginal capsules)** — significant reduction in recurrent UTI. **Oral multi-strain probiotics** (Jorgensen 2013) — mixed evidence; vaginal route stronger than oral. Targeted by **L. crispatus vaginal probiotics**, **multi-strain oral probiotics** (Lactobacillus GR-1 + RC-14), **avoidance of spermicide-coated condoms / diaphragms / douching**, **fermented foods** (kefir, sauerkraut for systemic Lactobacillus support).

### 2.4 Postmenopausal GSM (genitourinary syndrome of menopause)

**For you, in plain words.** After menopause, low estrogen makes vaginal and bladder tissues thinner, drier, less elastic. The vaginal pH rises, the lactobacilli die back, *E. coli* moves in. The fix is restoring local estrogen-like effects — hyaluronic-acid suppository OTC (good evidence), or topical vaginal estradiol (clinic-route §10.7.3, A-grade for postmenopausal UTI prevention). *(Cody 2012 Cochrane vaginal estrogen postmenopausal UTI)*

**Quick start.** Hyaluronic-acid vaginal suppository 2-3×/week (Hyalofemme, Hyalo Gyn, Multi-Gyn). If symptoms severe, discuss topical vaginal estradiol (Vagifem, Estring) in §10.7.3 — A-grade evidence for postmenopausal UTI prevention.

#### Open the science

GSM (genitourinary syndrome of menopause, renamed from vulvovaginal atrophy + atrophic urethritis 2014) affects 40-60% of postmenopausal women. Mechanism: estrogen deficiency → thin vaginal/urethral epithelium → glycogen depletion → loss of *Lactobacillus* substrate → pH rise → *E. coli* dominance. **Topical vaginal estradiol** (cream / tablet / ring) — A-grade for postmenopausal recurrent UTI prevention (Cody 2012 Cochrane). **Hyaluronic-acid vaginal suppositories** — emerging evidence for symptom relief and microbiome support without estrogen exposure (Costantino 2008; Origoni 2016 — non-inferior to estradiol for symptom relief in some endpoints). **DHEA (Intrarosa)** — clinic-route alternative. Targeted by **hyaluronic-acid suppositories** (OTC), **topical vaginal estradiol** (§10.7.3), **DHEA** (§10.7.3), **systemic phytoestrogens** (red clover, soy — weak evidence for GSM specifically), **vaginal moisturisers** (water-based, non-estrogenic).

### 2.5 Behavioural + anatomic

**For you, in plain words.** Holding urine too long, wiping back-to-front, not voiding after sex, dehydration — all of these load the dice toward UTI. So can pelvic-floor dysfunction (incomplete emptying), bladder prolapse, kidney stones, and (rarely) diabetes. *(Hooton 2013 NEJM UTI prevention review)*

**Quick start.** Void every 3-4 hours. Void within 15 min after sex. Front-to-back wiping. Cotton breathable underwear. Drink to keep urine pale (2-2.5 L/day). Avoid douching.

#### Open the science

Mechanical and behavioural drivers: incomplete bladder emptying (residual urine = bacterial growth medium) — pelvic-floor PT helps; postcoital voiding mechanically flushes peri-urethral organisms; front-to-back wiping reduces gut→urethra transit; spermicide N-9 directly kills *Lactobacillus*; diaphragms cause urinary retention; high-frequency intercourse + new partners increase exposure; dehydration reduces flushing; constipation can mechanically obstruct emptying. Targeted by **postcoital voiding within 15 min**, **front-to-back wiping**, **breathable cotton underwear**, **adequate hydration ≥2 L/day**, **regular voiding every 3-4 h (no holding)**, **pelvic-floor PT for incomplete emptying**, **avoidance of spermicide-coated condoms and diaphragms**, **constipation correction**.

### 3.2 What's NOT in the table

**For you, in plain words.** Things deliberately left out — high-dose vitamin C as primary, goldenseal, plain cranberry juice (un-standardised). *(Jepson 2012 Cochrane juice null)*

#### Open the science

- **Cranberry juice (un-standardised)** — Jepson 2012 Cochrane null. The juices tested had inconsistent or very low PAC content. Standardised PAC supplements only.

- **Goldenseal (berberine)** — traditional antimicrobial; limited prevention evidence; berberine doesn't reach high urinary concentrations.
- **Garlic capsules** — antimicrobial mechanism but no UTI-prevention RCT.

## 6. Dietary Protocol

### 6.1 Mandatory foods daily

**For you, in plain words.** Hydration-first eating. Water-rich foods. Fermented foods for vaginal microbiome support. Polyphenol-rich plants for systemic anti-inflammation.

#### Open the science

- **Water** — 2-2.5 L/day; urine should be pale yellow.
- **Fermented foods** — 1 serving/day (kefir 200 mL, yoghurt 200 g, sauerkraut 50 g, kimchi 50 g, idli/dosa, lassi).
- **Berries** — 100 g/day (blueberries, blackberries, cranberries fresh; standardised PAC supplements are the main mechanism).

- **Hibiscus tea** — 1-2 cups/day.

### 6.2 Absolutely avoid

**For you, in plain words.** Things that aggravate recurrence — chronic dehydration, holding urine, sugary drinks, excessive caffeine + alcohol (bladder irritants).

#### Open the science

- **Dehydration** — primary modifiable risk; ensure 2-2.5 L/day water.
- **Sugary drinks (soda, sweetened juice)** → glycosuria + bladder irritation; zero in diabetic recurrence.
- **Excessive caffeine** (>3 cups coffee/day) — bladder irritant; can worsen urgency.
- **Alcohol** — bladder irritant + dehydrating; reduce, particularly during flares.
- **Spicy foods + acidic drinks** during flare — bladder irritant for some.
- **Artificial sweeteners (aspartame)** — bladder irritant for sensitive individuals.
- **Spermicide (N-9)** condoms / diaphragms — knock down vaginal *Lactobacillus*.
- **Douching** — disrupts vaginal microbiome.
- **Holding urine** — bacterial proliferation in retained urine.

**For you, in plain words.** Soak, sprout, ferment, cook gently. Same four traditional preparations as elsewhere.

**Quick start.** Soak legumes overnight. Ferment 1 food daily. Don't over-cook polyphenol-rich foods.

### 6.5 Environmental & occupational triggers

**For you, in plain words.** Several environmental factors raise recurrence risk. Cleaning up the environment is part of the protocol. *(CCRAS gynecology dietetic monograph)*

**Quick start.** Switch from spermicide-coated to plain latex/non-latex condoms. Stop douching. Avoid scented feminine products. Drink filtered water (chlorinated tap water signal for vaginal microbiome disruption). Cotton underwear.

#### Open the science

- **Spermicide (N-9) condoms / diaphragms** — directly kill vaginal *Lactobacillus*; switch to plain condoms, non-spermicidal contraception.
- **Douching, scented feminine washes, fragranced pads/tampons** — disrupt vaginal microbiome.
- **Synthetic underwear / tight clothing** — moisture retention favours *Candida* + *E. coli*; switch to breathable cotton.
- **Bath products with sulphates / fragrance** — vulvar irritation.
- **Chlorinated swimming pools** — for very recurrent users, post-swim shower + change of clothing.
- **Public toilet seats** — myth-level risk (UTI is from gut→perineum, not toilet seats); but hygiene of own toilet seat matters with shared bathrooms.
- **Sexual lubricants** — water-based, no glycerin (feeds yeast); silicone-based OK for most.
- **Constipation** — fecal loading near perineum; treat with fibre + hydration (read `constipation-regularity.md`).
