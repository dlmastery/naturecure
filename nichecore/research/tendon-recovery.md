## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What tendinopathy actually is

> **For you, in plain words.** "Tendonitis" is the wrong word — the inflammation is largely gone after the first 1-2 weeks. What lingers is **tendinopathy** — disorganised collagen, neovascularisation (abnormal new blood vessels and nerves growing into the tendon), and a tendon that can no longer tolerate load. The cure isn't rest — it's **graded loading**. *(Cook & Purdam 2009 continuum; Khan 2002 BJSM tendinopathy reframe)*

### 1.2 The four drivers — at a glance

> **Quick start.** Tonight: start a single-leg heel-raise from a step (slant-board / step edge), 3 sets of 15, slow 3-second lowering (eccentric) — for Achilles. Or single-leg decline squat 25° board 3 × 15 — for patellar. Pain up to 5/10 during is allowed (Silbernagel pain-monitoring model). Add 15 g hydrolysed collagen + 50 mg vitamin C 15-60 minutes before loading (Shaw 2017 — doubled collagen synthesis). Avoid full immobilisation. *(Alfredson 1998; Shaw 2017 collagen; Silbernagel pain-monitoring)*

### 1.3 The deeper layer — for those who want it

Tendinopathy is conventionally siloed as "tendon inflammation" treated with rest, NSAIDs, cortisone injections, and surgery. A more honest reframe: persistent tendon pain is the load-tolerance failure of a tendon stuck in a **degenerative + maladaptive remodelling state** — driven by **four convergent root drivers** — (i) **mechanical overload** (training error, sudden load increase, work load mismatch); (ii) **disordered collagen remodelling** (insufficient mechanical stimulus to drive normal Type I collagen organisation; MMP-2/9 imbalance; reduced tendon stiffness); (iii) **neovascularisation + neuro-nerve in-growth** (the source of pain — abnormal vessels and accompanying nociceptive C-fibres infiltrating an avascular tendon); and (iv) **systemic factors** (metabolic syndrome, T2D, statins, fluoroquinolones, hypothyroidism, low vitamin D, low dietary protein, perimenopause oestrogen decline, smoking). **Synthesis.** A protocol that loads the tendon at the right intensity and tempo (eccentric and slow-heavy-resistance), supplies the building blocks for collagen synthesis (hydrolysed collagen + vitamin C; gelatin), quiets the inflammation enough to allow loading (curcumin, omega-3), and fixes the systemic drivers, addresses all four — without injections or surgery.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (4-node)

### 2.1 Four causes — the picture

> **For you, in plain words.** Four causes drive tendon pain — overload that exceeds capacity, the collagen scaffold getting messy, abnormal vessels and pain nerves growing in, and systemic factors that block normal healing. Naming them lets every exercise, supplement and lifestyle change aim at the right one. *(Cook & Purdam 2009; Khan 2002; Riley 2008; Magnusson 2010)*

### 2.2 Mechanical overload

> **For you, in plain words.** Tendons fail when load outpaces capacity — usually a sudden jump in training volume or intensity, a change in work, or wearing the wrong shoes. The fix is to lower load briefly, then progressively rebuild it. *(Cook & Purdam 2009 continuum; Gabbett 2016 ACWR)*

**The deeper layer.** The Cook-Purdam continuum (BJSM 2009) describes three reactive states: **reactive tendinopathy** (acute overload response — short-term cell + matrix change; reversible with load reduction); **tendon dysrepair** (matrix breakdown begins; cell phenotype shifts); **degenerative tendinopathy** (areas of cell death + matrix disorder; poor reversibility but still loadable). Mechanical overload typically follows the Gabbett acute-to-chronic-workload-ratio (ACWR) framework: ratios above 1.3-1.5 in a 7:28 day rolling window predict injury. Targeted by **load reduction (NOT full rest)**, **isometric loading first if pain is high (Cook & Purdam continuum)**, **eccentric loading 12-week protocol (Alfredson 1998 Sweden)**, **slow-heavy-resistance training (Beyer 2015 Denmark)**, **graded return-to-running using pain-monitoring model**, **footwear + bracing audit**, **strength of the kinetic chain (calf/hamstring/glute for lower extremity; rotator cuff/scapula for upper)**. **Synthesis.** The tendon doesn't need rest. It needs the right load.

### 2.3 Disordered collagen remodelling

> **For you, in plain words.** A normal tendon is densely packed Type I collagen. In tendinopathy, the collagen gets disorganised — Type III "messy" collagen replaces Type I "strong" collagen. The fix is the right kind of load (slow, heavy, repeated) and the building blocks (collagen + vitamin C + glycine). *(Riley 2008 collagen; Shaw 2017 collagen + Vit C; Magnusson 2010)*

**The deeper layer.** Type I collagen is the dominant tendon protein; in pathology, Type III increases (weaker, more elastic, less ordered). MMP-2 + MMP-9 (matrix metalloproteinases) increase. TGF-β + IGF-1 signalling shifts. Tendon stiffness drops. Shaw 2017 (Harvard, Australian Institute of Sport collaboration) demonstrated 15 g hydrolysed collagen + 50 mg vitamin C consumed 30-60 minutes before 6 min of skipping doubled collagen synthesis markers (PINP). Targeted by **15 g hydrolysed collagen peptides + 50 mg vitamin C 15-60 min pre-load**, **dietary gelatin (bone broth, traditional gummies)**, **adequate protein (1.6-2.0 g/kg/day, leucine-rich, distributed across meals)**, **glycine + proline-rich foods**, **vitamin C 500-1000 mg daily**, **lysine 1-2 g daily**. **Synthesis.** Don't load without the building blocks.

### 2.4 Neovascularisation + nerve in-growth

> **For you, in plain words.** Normal tendon is mostly avascular and aneural — it has very few blood vessels or pain nerves. In chronic tendinopathy, abnormal vessels grow in and bring pain nerves with them. That's the source of the burning, aching pain at rest. The fix is the right kind of load plus shockwave or sclerosing if home protocol plateaus. *(Alfredson 2003 neovascularisation; Öhberg 2002 neovessels + Alfredson eccentric reduces them)*

**The deeper layer.** Doppler ultrasound studies (Öhberg 2002) showed neovessels accompanied by sensory nerve fibres in Achilles tendinopathy. Alfredson's heavy-load eccentric protocol reduced neovessel density and pain in parallel. Substance-P and CGRP nerve markers increased. Targeted by **eccentric or slow-heavy-resistance loading (reduces neovessels — Alfredson 2003)**, **extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT — Speed 2014 A-grade)** in §10.7.3 as clinic-route, **home red-light PBM** as a partial home alternative, **high-volume injection** clinic-only §10.7.3, **sclerosing injection** clinic-only §10.7.3. **Synthesis.** Loading is the home lever; clinic procedures are honestly noted but never folded in.

### 2.5 Systemic drivers

> **For you, in plain words.** Type-2 diabetes, statins, fluoroquinolones (the Cipro/Levaquin antibiotic class), hypothyroidism, low vitamin D, low dietary protein, perimenopause oestrogen decline, and smoking all wreck tendon healing. Audit and fix these in parallel with the loading protocol. *(Abate 2014 metabolic tendinopathy; Wise 2012 fluoroquinolone tendon rupture; Magnusson 2007 estrogen)*

**The deeper layer.** T2D increases tendinopathy and rupture risk via AGE cross-linking + microvascular damage. Statins (especially atorvastatin, simvastatin) are associated with tendinopathy + rupture in observational data. **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) carry boxed warnings for tendon rupture (Wise 2012; FDA / EMA / TGA boxed warnings — used here as regulatory-path context, not as evidence anchor on whether something works). Hypothyroidism, hypercholesterolemia (familial), vitamin D deficiency, low dietary protein, perimenopause oestrogen decline (Magnusson 2007 — tendon stiffness drops with oestrogen drop), and smoking all impair tendon healing. Targeted by **HbA1c + lipid + thyroid + Vit D DTC labs**, **statin review on the user's own initiative**, **fluoroquinolone avoidance where possible**, **dietary protein 1.6-2.0 g/kg/day**, **vitamin D 4000-5000 IU**, **smoking cessation**. **Synthesis.** No load protocol works while a systemic driver is unfixed.

### 3.5 Honest skip-list — popular-but-weak or harmful in this context

> **For you, in plain words.** A few popular interventions actively harm tendon adaptation or carry honest evidence gaps. We list them for transparency. *(Schoenfeld 2012 NSAID + adaptation; Coombes 2013 cortisone)*

- **High-dose chronic oral NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600 mg+ TID, naproxen 500 mg BID) for >2 weeks.** Schoenfeld 2012 meta showed they blunt muscle protein synthesis and tendon adaptation. **Short-term burst for acute pain is fine; chronic = harmful to recovery.** Use topical diclofenac instead.
- **Repeated cortisone injections.** Coombes 2013 *Lancet* found short-term relief but HIGHER recurrence at 12 months and worse outcomes than wait-and-see for lateral epicondyle. Clinic-route only — never recommended in home protocol; honestly noted in §10.7.3.
- **PRP (platelet-rich plasma).** PEAK Achilles trial null; elbow signal. Cost $500-1500/injection. Clinic-route only — §10.7.3.
- **Full immobilisation rest.** "Rest" was the old paradigm — the current evidence (Cook & Purdam 2009; Alfredson 1998) is that the tendon needs graded loading, not rest. The tendon adapts to load; without load it atrophies further.
- **Glucosamine-only OA stacks (no chondroitin, no MSM).** Cochrane mixed; pair with chondroitin and MSM for the highest-evidence stack (ESCEO Bruyère 2016).

## 5. Biophysical Interventions — home-administered only

### 5.1 Six home levers

> **For you, in plain words.** Six home physical levers — eccentric/slow-heavy loading (the cornerstone), isometric pain-relief loading, home red-light photobiomodulation, compression sleeves, night splints (plantar fascia), and home foam rolling + lacrosse ball release. No clinic shockwave, no clinic injection, no surgery recommended in the home regime. *(Alfredson 1998; Beyer 2015; Bjordal 2008 PBM)*

### 5.2 Eccentric + slow-heavy-resistance loading

> **For you, in plain words.** The single highest-evidence intervention in this dossier — Alfredson 1998 (Sweden) showed 90% of Achilles tendinopathy patients improved on a 12-week eccentric program; Beyer 2015 confirmed slow-heavy-resistance is equivalent. Pain up to 5/10 during loading is allowed (Silbernagel pain-monitoring model). 12 weeks minimum. *(Alfredson 1998; Beyer 2015; Silbernagel 2007)*

**Quick start.** Achilles: single-leg heel-raise from a step (toes on step, heel drops below), 3 × 15 BID, slow 3-second lowering with bent knee + 3 × 15 with straight knee. Patellar: single-leg decline squat on a 25° board, 3 × 15 BID, slow 3-second descent. Lateral epicondyle: Tyler twist with Theraband FlexBar, 3 × 15 BID. Plantar: heel-raise with towel rolled under toes, 3 × 12 BID (Rathleff 2015).

#### Open the science

Alfredson 1998 *AJSM* — n=15 Achilles tendinopathy — 12-week eccentric heel-drop program (3 × 15 BID with both bent + straight knee, 7 days/week) — 100% returned to sport with full pain relief vs 0% in surgery-waiting control. Subsequent meta-analyses (Cochrane Kingma 2007; Magnussen 2009; Habets 2015) confirm A-grade evidence. Beyer 2015 *AJSM* — slow-heavy-resistance training (3 sets × 15-6 reps at 70-90% 1RM, twice a week) is equivalent to eccentric loading at 12 weeks. Kongsgaard 2009 confirmed for patellar tendinopathy. Cook & Purdam 2009 isometric-first if pain is high (relief during contraction; 5 × 45-sec holds at 70% MVIC). Silbernagel 2007 — pain-monitoring model: VAS 0-5/10 during exercise is acceptable; 5-10 is too much; pain settles within 24 h post-load is acceptable. References: [Alfredson 1998 AJSM] · [Beyer 2015 AJSM] · [Cook & Purdam 2009 BJSM] · [Silbernagel 2007 AJSM].

### 5.3 Isometric pain-relief loading

> **For you, in plain words.** When tendon pain is high (>5/10) and eccentric exercise hurts too much, start with isometric holds — they reduce pain immediately and let you load without flare. 5 sets × 45 seconds at 70% maximum voluntary contraction, 3-4×/day. *(Rio 2015 isometric tendinopathy)*

**Quick start.** Patellar isometric: single-leg wall-sit at 60° knee flexion, 5 × 45 sec, 2-min rest, 3-4×/day. Achilles isometric: heel-raise hold on a flat floor (or single-leg) at the highest point, 5 × 45 sec. Lateral epicondyle: wrist-extension isometric hold against Theraband resistance, 5 × 45 sec.

#### Open the science

Rio 2015 (Cook lab, La Trobe University Australia) showed isometric contractions (5 × 45 sec at 70% MVIC) reduced patellar tendon pain by 50% immediately and the effect lasted 45 minutes. Mechanism: descending corticospinal pain inhibition + reduced motor cortex excitability. Use isometric-first protocol when pain is >5/10; transition to eccentric / SHR once pain is <5/10 during loading.

### 5.4 Home red-light PBM (photobiomodulation)

> **For you, in plain words.** Home red-light therapy (660 nm + 810-850 nm) supports tendon healing and pain relief with moderate evidence. Bjordal 2008 meta-analysis supports it for lateral epicondyle. 5-10 minutes per area, 3-5×/week. *(Bjordal 2008 meta; Tumilty 2010 review)*

**Quick start.** Mito Red Light MitoMIN/MID/MAX, Joov Original/Solo/Elite, Hooga, Red Light Rising. Position panel 6 inches from skin. 5-10 min over affected tendon, 3-5×/week. Caution if photosensitiser drug, retinal disease, pregnancy.

#### Open the science

Bjordal 2008 meta-analysis (n=18 RCTs across multiple musculoskeletal sites) — LLLT (low-level laser therapy) at 660-905 nm reduced pain in lateral epicondyle. Tumilty 2010 systematic review — mixed but signal positive at correct doses (4-8 J/cm² per point). Home panels deliver lower irradiance but longer treatment time achieves comparable cumulative dose. Devices: Mito Red Light, Joov, Hooga, Red Light Rising, BlockBlueLight, BioLight Shield. Wavelengths: 630-680 nm + 810-850 nm.

### 5.5 Compression + bracing

> **For you, in plain words.** Compression sleeves (CEP, 2XU, Bauerfeind) provide comfort + proprioception during loading and recovery. Achilles night splint or boot for severe insertional + plantar fasciopathy at night (Strassburg Sock for plantar fascia). *(DiGiovanni 2003 plantar fascia night stretch)*

- **Compression sleeves**: CEP Achilles support, 2XU compression calf sleeve, Bauerfeind AchilloTrain — daily wear during work + light activity.
- **Counterforce brace (tennis-elbow strap)**: for lateral epicondyle activity — straps below the elbow muscle belly (Bauerfeind EpiTrain, Mueller HG80).
- **Patellar tendon strap** (Cho-Pat, Bauerfeind GenuTrain): for patellar tendinopathy during running/jumping.
- **Plantar fascia night splint (Strassburg Sock, dorsiflexion night brace)**: DiGiovanni 2003 showed dorsiflexion stretching overnight reduced first-step pain in plantar fasciopathy.
- **Counterforce brace for golf elbow**: same principle medial side.

### 5.6 Home soft-tissue tools

> **For you, in plain words.** Foam roller and lacrosse ball for the muscle that pulls on the tendon (calf for Achilles, quadriceps for patellar, forearm for elbow, plantar foot ball for plantar fascia). 2-3 min per area, daily. *(Cheatham 2015 foam roll review)*

## 6. Dietary Protocol

### 6.1 Foods to eat daily

> **For you, in plain words.** Eat for collagen synthesis — bone broth, gelatin, oily fish, leafy greens, bell peppers, kiwi (vitamin C), beef liver (copper + iron), and 1.6-2.0 g/kg/day of protein distributed across 4 meals. The Mediterranean and Indian Triphala-rich diets both protect the tendon. *(Shaw 2017; Lis 2022; Phillips 2016 protein meta)*

**Quick start.** Today: 30 g hydrolysed collagen + 1 kiwi 30-60 min before loading; 4 protein meals with 30 g protein each across the day; 200 g bone broth daily (sip warm); 150 g oily fish 3×/week; 1 small avocado daily for fat; 1 Brazil nut daily for selenium.

#### Open the science

Mandatory daily anchors (gram quantities):
- **Protein 1.6-2.0 g/kg/day** distributed across 4 meals × 25-40 g (leucine threshold ~2.5 g per meal — eggs, whey/casein, beef, chicken, fish, lentil + rice combo).
- **Hydrolysed collagen peptides 15-25 g daily**, half pre-loading day.
- **Bone broth 200-300 ml daily** — gelatin + glycine + proline.
- **Oily fish 150 g 3×/week** (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring) — omega-3 anchor.
- **Vitamin C anchor**: 1 kiwi (75 mg) OR ½ red bell pepper (95 mg) OR 100 g amla (Indian gooseberry).
- **150-200 g leafy greens daily** (kale, spinach, rocket) — magnesium, folate, lutein.
- **30 g walnuts OR 2 tbsp ground flaxseed daily** — plant omega-3.
- **1 small avocado 3-4×/week** OR **30 g olives daily** — monounsaturated fat.
- **1 Brazil nut daily** — selenium.
- **2-3 cups green tea daily** — polyphenols (avoid same hour as iron-rich foods).
- **30-35 ml/kg/day water** + electrolytes if sweating heavily.

### 6.2 Foods to cut

> **For you, in plain words.** Industrial seed oils, refined sugar, ultraprocessed snacks, alcohol, and excessive caffeine all worsen tendon healing. Smoking is a major systemic driver — cessation is non-negotiable.

- **Industrial seed oils** (soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower) → omega-6 dominance → pro-inflammatory eicosanoids.
- **Refined sugar + HFCS** → AGEs cross-link tendon collagen → stiffness.
- **Alcohol >1 drink/day** → tendon repair impairment + zinc depletion.
- **Smoking + vaping** → vascular insult to already-poorly-vascular tendon (cessation is critical).
- **Excess caffeine after 14:00** → sleep disruption (sleep is the main collagen synthesis window).
- **Trans fats** (margarine, hydrogenated fats) → anti-resolvin.
- **High-dose chronic NSAIDs** (see §3.5; not a food but a critical avoid).

### 6.3 Preparation methods

> **For you, in plain words.** Slow-cook and bone-broth — both extract gelatin from bones and connective tissue. Sprout legumes for protein digestibility. Use ghee or cold-pressed olive oil for tempering.

Slow-cook bone-in cuts (osso buco, chicken thighs with skin, bone broth) extracts collagen + gelatin. Sprouted legumes (mung, chickpea) improve protein digestibility. Ghee for high-heat. - **High-dose chronic oral NSAIDs >2 weeks** — Schoenfeld 2012 adaptation block.

- **Fluoroquinolone antibiotics + heavy loading concurrent** — rupture risk (use alternative antibiotic class where possible, on user's own initiative with prescriber).
- **Full immobilisation rest** — outdated paradigm; tendon needs graded loading.
- **Stretching cold tendon (especially Achilles AM)** — pre-warm first.
- **Sudden volume jumps in training (ACWR >1.3)**.
- **Smoking + vaping** — vascular insult.
- **Alcohol >1 drink/day during recovery** — impairs healing + zinc.
