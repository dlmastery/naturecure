## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What Bell's palsy actually is

> **For you, in plain words.** Bell's palsy is sudden, usually one-sided, idiopathic peripheral facial-nerve (cranial nerve VII) weakness — the nerve that runs the muscles of facial expression. It is NOT a stroke (forehead is spared in stroke; in Bell's, forehead also drops on the affected side — that's the classic peripheral pattern). Most people recover most of their function over weeks to months. *(Sir Charles Bell 1821; Sullivan 2007 NEJM; Peitersen 2002 natural history)*

### 1.2 The five levers — at a glance

> **Quick start.** TONIGHT: (1) call your clinician or go to ER for prednisolone within 72 hours of onset (A-grade); (2) start eye protection — preservative-free artificial tears (Refresh Optive, Hylo-Forte, Theratears, Systane Ultra) every hour daytime, ophthalmic ointment (Lacri-lube, Lubrithal, Refresh PM) at bedtime, medical tape (3M Micropore) or a custom moisture chamber to keep the lid closed during sleep, sunglasses outdoors. (3) Photograph your face from the front in neutral, smile, eyebrow-raise, eye-closure, lip-pucker — this is your day-0 House-Brackmann baseline. THIS WEEK: start gentle mirror-feedback facial exercises (5 min × 2/day, NO forcing — mild engagement only; Beurskens 2003 Mime Therapy A-grade adapted home version); B-complex (with methylcobalamin / methylfolate); 2 g EPA+DHA omega-3; 400 mg magnesium glycinate; vitamin D 5000 IU if deficient. THIS MONTH: weekly House-Brackmann + Sunnybrook self-grading; CBT-app for distress (Woebot, MoodGym); stress + sleep optimisation; smoking + alcohol moderation. *(Sullivan 2007 NEJM; Madhok 2016 Cochrane; Beurskens 2003 BMJ)*

### 1.3 The deeper layer — for those who want it

Bell's palsy is usually framed as "viral inflammation of the facial nerve — wait it out." A more honest reframe: Bell's palsy = the convergence of **five intersecting systems** that the home protocol can support around a single time-critical Rx intervention — (i) **the 72-hour neuroprotective window** (corticosteroids reduce nerve oedema in the bony Fallopian canal; this is the dominant outcome lever); (ii) **mechanical eye protection** (cornea exposed to evaporation + foreign body without normal blink — preventable damage if drops + tape + ointment start tonight); (iii) **neuromuscular re-education** (gentle, low-effort, mirror-feedback movement during the recovery window prevents synkinesis — aberrant nerve regrowth that causes involuntary co-contractions, e.g., eye-closes-when-smiling — and Mime Therapy / Kabat / PNF is A-grade for this); (iv) **nerve-regeneration cofactors** (B12, B-complex, omega-3, magnesium, vitamin D — Schwann cell myelination + axonal growth substrate); (v) **psychological distress + facial-disfigurement adjustment** (Bell's hits identity and social function hard; CBT + MBSR + body-image work has small but real evidence). **Synthesis.** Steroid window + eye protection + gentle Mime Therapy + B-vit / omega-3 cofactors + CBT for distress + classical layers (Ashwagandha for nerve-tonic, Qian Zheng San for facial-deviation, Causticum keynote homeopathy) — five systems, one face, twelve weeks until most of the work is done.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (5-node)

### 2.1 Five systems — the picture

> **For you, in plain words.** Five systems govern Bell's palsy recovery — nerve oedema in the bony canal, corneal exposure, neuromuscular re-education, nerve regeneration substrate, and psychological adjustment. Naming them lets every Rx, drop, exercise and supplement aim at the right system. *(Adour 1972; Peitersen 2002; Sullivan 2007 NEJM)*

### 2.2 Nerve oedema in the Fallopian canal

> **For you, in plain words.** The facial nerve runs through a tight bony canal in the skull (the Fallopian canal). When inflammation swells the nerve, the canal acts like a tourniquet — the nerve compresses against bone, axons stop firing, the face drops. High-dose oral corticosteroids in the first 72 hours reduce that oedema and let the nerve recover with less long-term damage. *(Sullivan 2007 NEJM Scottish Bell's Palsy Study)*

**The deeper layer.** Sullivan 2007 NEJM (n=551, four-arm RCT: prednisolone vs aciclovir vs both vs placebo) showed **prednisolone 50 mg/d × 10 days started <72 hr of onset** raised the proportion with complete recovery at 9 months from 64% (placebo) to 83% (prednisolone). Aciclovir alone offered no benefit; prednisolone + aciclovir was not superior to prednisolone alone. de Almeida 2009 BMJ meta-analysis (n=1,569) confirmed steroids ↑ recovery odds 1.6×; antivirals NO added benefit. Madhok 2016 Cochrane review locked in A-grade for steroids. The mechanism is straightforward anti-inflammatory: reduce nerve oedema before it compresses axons into wallerian degeneration. **Synthesis.** The single largest outcome lever is whether the reader picks up the phone in the first 72 hours and gets prednisolone — every home layer below depends on this happening.

### 2.3 Corneal exposure and lagophthalmos

> **For you, in plain words.** If your eye won't close fully (lagophthalmos), the cornea dries out within hours and corneal ulcers + scarring can form within days. Eye protection — drops every hour day, ointment + tape night, sunglasses outdoors — is your second non-negotiable, and it's fully OTC + home. *(Lacri-lube + Refresh OTC monographs; Joseph 2003 corneal protection in facial palsy)*

**The deeper layer.** Normal blink rate ~15-20/min spreads tear film and prevents corneal desiccation. In Bell's palsy with paralytic lagophthalmos, blink rate on the affected side drops to near-zero; tear film evaporates; corneal epithelium breaks down; exposure keratopathy → corneal ulceration → permanent visual impairment if neglected. The eye-protection cascade is dose-stepped to severity: mild → preservative-free drops every 1-2 hr; moderate → drops every hour + bedtime ointment + medical eye tape; severe → moisture chamber goggles + bedtime patch + urgent ophthalmology for lid weight (small gold or platinum weight implanted in upper lid — gravity helps closure) or tarsorrhaphy (temporary surgical lid-narrowing). Bell's phenomenon (eye rolls up when attempting closure) is protective when intact; absence increases risk. **Synthesis.** Every reader with lagophthalmos needs the eye-protection stack starting tonight — non-substitutable, fully OTC, fully sovereign.

### 2.4 Neuromuscular re-education and synkinesis prevention

> **For you, in plain words.** As the nerve regrows over weeks to months, the regrowing axons sometimes "wire to the wrong muscle" — so when you smile, your eye closes; when you blink, the corner of your mouth twitches. This is **synkinesis**, and gentle, mindful, low-effort facial exercises during the recovery window reduce its severity. The mistake to avoid: forcing movement, grimacing, or doing high-effort facial workouts — that drives more synkinesis, not less. *(Beurskens 2003 BMJ Mime Therapy RCT; Cardoso 2008; Barbara 2010)*

**The deeper layer.** Mime Therapy (Beurskens 2003 BMJ RCT, n=50 chronic Bell's) is mirror-feedback neuromuscular re-education focusing on isolated, low-effort, symmetrical movements with conscious inhibition of mass-action co-contractions — A-grade for both functional outcome and synkinesis reduction. Kabat / proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF) — facial-PT variant adapted from Margaret Knott's body PNF — uses passive stretch + resistance + diagonal patterns; Cardoso 2008 systematic review B+ signal; Barbara 2010 RCT supports. The home version is a daily 10-minute mirror practice: gentle smile, lip-pucker, eyebrow-raise, eye-closure, brow-furrow — each ×5-10 reps with conscious release between, low effort, no grimacing. The principle: the brain re-wires the motor program through accurate, repeated, low-noise sensory feedback. **Synthesis.** Mirror-feedback facial-PT is the second-most-evidenced lever after the steroid window — A-grade, home-usable, free.

### 2.5 Nerve-regeneration substrate

> **For you, in plain words.** The nerve needs raw materials to rebuild its myelin sheath and regrow its axons — B-vitamins (especially B12 methylcobalamin), folate, omega-3 fatty acids, magnesium, vitamin D. These are the construction materials; without them the rebuild slows. The evidence here is supportive (not the dominant lever) but the cost is low and the rationale is clean. *(Diaz 2018 methylcobalamin facial-nerve recovery small RCT; classical neurology B12 + nerve regeneration)*

**The deeper layer.** Methylcobalamin (B12 methylated form) — small RCTs (Diaz 2018 et al.) suggest faster recovery vs placebo in Bell's palsy; mechanism is Schwann cell myelination + axonal growth. Folate / methylfolate pairs with B12 in one-carbon metabolism critical to nerve repair. Omega-3 (EPA + DHA) — anti-inflammatory + membrane fluidity + nerve growth factor support. Magnesium — NMDA modulation + neuromuscular junction support + nerve sheath repair cofactor. Vitamin D — neurotrophic + immunomodulatory; deficiency is overrepresented in idiopathic facial palsy cohorts. Alpha-lipoic acid — small signal for diabetic facial neuropathy; weaker for classic Bell's. **Synthesis.** The nerve-cofactor stack is a low-cost, low-risk supportive layer — B-complex with methylcobalamin + omega-3 + magnesium + D — wrapped around the dominant Rx and PT layers.

### 2.6 Psychological adjustment + facial-disfigurement distress

> **For you, in plain words.** Bell's palsy is psychologically heavy — your face is your identity in every social moment, and watching it not work is destabilising. Depression, anxiety, social withdrawal, body-image distress are common and under-discussed. CBT, MBSR, body-image work, and supportive community (Facial Palsy UK forum, local groups) all help, with small but real evidence. *(Bogart 2010; Bradbury 2006 facial-disfigurement adjustment)*

**The deeper layer.** Bogart 2010 (and subsequent work) — facial-paralysis patients score significantly higher on depression and social-anxiety inventories than matched controls; severity of distress does NOT correlate tightly with severity of paralysis (i.e., the psychological response is a separable layer that responds to its own treatment). CBT for facial-disfigurement-related distress (Bradbury 2006 framework, Ling 2008 small trials) — addresses appearance-related cognitions, social-avoidance behaviours, identity-rigidity. MBSR for chronic-condition adjustment generally — moderate evidence for facial-paralysis cohorts. Self-help: Facial Palsy UK community + Bell's palsy social forums + curated stories of recovery + workbooks (Bradbury, "Living with Facial Difference"). **Synthesis.** Treating the psychological layer is the fifth non-substitutable lever — not optional decoration, but a load-bearing part of recovery.

### 3.2 The honest caveat — antivirals do NOT add to steroids

> **For you, in plain words.** For decades, the HSV-1 reactivation theory of Bell's palsy made adding aciclovir or valaciclovir to prednisolone the "obvious" combination. **Sullivan 2007 NEJM directly tested this and found no added benefit; de Almeida 2009 meta confirmed.** Despite this, antivirals are still sometimes prescribed — partly habit, partly the (uncertain but possible) value in Ramsay-Hunt overlap. **For classic idiopathic Bell's, the steroid alone is what works; the antiviral does not measurably help.** Don't push your clinician for antivirals on the basis of older literature; the right ask is the steroid within 72 hours.

### 3.4 The diet base layer

> **For you, in plain words.** No diet replaces the steroid window. But the food pattern that supports nerve regeneration is the same as the food pattern for chronic inflammation: Mediterranean / anti-inflammatory + adequate protein (1.2 g/kg) + omega-3-rich fish 3×/week + leafy greens for folate + eggs / B12 sources + colourful vegetables + nuts + olive oil. Soft foods + straw drinks if facial muscle weakness makes chewing/sipping awkward. *(PREDIMED 2013 vascular; classical neurology nutrition)*

**Quick start.** Week 1: soft-food rotation — soups, smoothies, mashed cooked vegetables, scrambled eggs, oatmeal — to reduce facial fatigue + drooling distress while eating. Add 3 servings/week oily fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, anchovies). Daily leafy greens + nuts + olive oil + colourful vegetables. Adequate hydration; alcohol moderation (alcohol has vascular contribution to neuropathy and impairs nerve repair). Coffee fine but watch jitters during acute-stress phase.

## 6. Master 7-Day Meal Plan (soft-food adaptive)

### 6.1 Why a soft-food rotation

> **For you, in plain words.** Acute Bell's makes chewing, swallowing, and lip-sealing awkward — food drools, liquids leak past the corner of the mouth, and one-sided chewing fatigues fast. A soft-food rotation reduces meal-time distress + supports recovery without sacrificing nutrition. Once muscle tone returns, you naturally re-introduce harder textures. *(classical neurology nutrition; speech-language pathology dysphagia guidelines)*

### 6.2 Soft-food principles

> **Quick start.** (1) Smoothies + soups + porridges + scrambled eggs + soft cooked vegetables + soft fish + soft cooked grains = staples. (2) Use a straw on the unaffected side to bypass the weak lip-seal. (3) Lean forward slightly when swallowing to use gravity. (4) Wipe the affected corner of the mouth often. (5) Eat slowly. (6) Avoid: crusty bread, raw vegetables, popcorn, sticky candy, dry foods that scatter, very hot liquids (sensation can be altered).

## 12. Clinical Scoring Instrument (House-Brackmann + Sunnybrook + FDI)

### 12.1 House-Brackmann I-VI

> **For you, in plain words.** The House-Brackmann grading is the classical 6-level facial-nerve function scale. Score yourself weekly with a phone photo of your face in neutral, smile, eyebrow-raise, eye-closure, lip-pucker. *(House-Brackmann 1985)*

| Grade | What it means | Recovery target |
|---|---|---|
| I | Normal symmetry + function | Target endpoint |
| II | Mild dysfunction — slight weakness on close inspection, complete eye closure with minimal effort | Most reach by 3-6 months with steroids |
| III | Moderate dysfunction — obvious but not disfiguring difference, complete eye closure with effort | Common 1-3 month grade |
| IV | Moderately severe — obvious weakness or disfiguring asymmetry, incomplete eye closure | Early/acute grade |
| V | Severe — only barely perceptible motion, no eye closure | Worst-grade early presentation |
| VI | Total paralysis — no movement | Acute presentation |

### 12.2 Sunnybrook Facial Grading System

> **For you, in plain words.** More granular than House-Brackmann — scores resting symmetry, voluntary movement, and synkinesis separately. Useful for tracking subtle improvement weeks 4-12. *(Ross 1996 Sunnybrook)*

### 12.3 Facial Disability Index FDI

> **For you, in plain words.** 10-item self-rated questionnaire — physical function (5 items: eating, drinking, lip-seal, mouth-rinse, brushing teeth) + social/wellbeing function (5 items). Score monthly. *(VanSwearingen 1996 FDI)*
