> **For you, in plain words.** This is a safe, over-the-counter, at-home plan for chronic venous insufficiency (CVI), varicose veins, and the spectrum of leg-vein problems clinicians stage with the CEAP system from C0 (no visible signs) through C6 (active venous ulcer). CVI affects an estimated 25-40% of adults in Western populations; women > men; pregnancy + occupation involving prolonged standing/sitting + age + obesity + family history are the dominant risk factors. The A-grade levers we will use: **graduated compression therapy** (Cochrane Nelson 2014 A-grade for symptom relief; O'Meara 2012 Cochrane A-grade for venous-ulcer healing with multilayer compression), **horse chestnut seed extract (HCSE) standardised to escin/aescin** (Cochrane Pittler 2012 — A-grade for CVI symptoms — the gold-standard non-prescription oral CVI agent), **structured walking** (Padberg 2004 + Klonizakis 2018 — A-grade for calf-pump activation), and **leg elevation + weight management + ergonomic standing/sitting habits**. The supportive layer adds gotu kola Centella asiatica (Cochrane Chong 2013 B+), Pycnogenol (Belcaro 2006 B+), butcher's broom Ruscus aculeatus (B+), and micronised purified flavonoid fraction MPFF/Daflon where available OTC (Cochrane Martinez-Zapata 2020 B+ for chronic venous symptoms — note: prescription-only in some jurisdictions). *(Pittler 2012 Cochrane HCSE; Nelson 2014 Cochrane compression; O'Meara 2012 Cochrane multilayer; Padberg 2004 walking RCT)*
>
> **The frame.** Grounded in the WHO Traditional & Complementary Medicine Strategy 2025-2034, India AYUSH (CCRAS, CCRH, CCRYN, CCRUM, CCRS, PCIMH, ICMR), EMA herbal monographs (HMPC) including the EMA Aesculus monograph, Australia TGA + NHMRC, China NMPA + Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020, Health Canada NHP Directorate, Men-Tsee-Khang Tibetan formulary, Hamdard Dawakhana Pharmacopoeia, IMPCOPS Chennai, the European Society for Vascular Surgery (ESVS) clinical practice guidelines, the International Union of Phlebology (UIP), and classical Ayurvedic, TCM, Unani, Siddha, Tibetan and homeopathic literature. **No US regulator and no US disease association (American Heart Association, American Venous Forum, AAFP) is cited as authority.**
>
> **Sovereignty.** Every supplement, compression garment, and home device here is something you can buy and use yourself. Medical-grade graduated compression stockings 15-20 mmHg through 30-40 mmHg are consumer-purchasable from Sigvaris, Jobst, Mediven, Therafirm, Bauerfeind. Pneumatic compression sleeves for home (Normatec, Therabody RecoveryAir, Hyperice) are consumer-purchasable. **No prescription, no clinic visit, and no hospital admission is recommended in the home regime.** Clinic-route options (sclerotherapy, endovenous laser ablation EVLA, radiofrequency ablation RFA, mechanochemical ablation MOCA ClariVein, cyanoacrylate adhesive closure VenaSeal, ambulatory phlebectomy, deep-venous reconstructive surgery, venous stenting for May-Thurner, prescription MPFF/Daflon where Rx-only, sulodexide, pentoxifylline, wound-care nurse for active C6 venous ulcers, and skin grafting for refractory ulcers) sit in a labelled §10.7.3 referral addendum — never inside the home protocol.
>
> **CRITICAL RED-FLAG ROUTING — read this before starting compression.** Compression therapy is contraindicated in significant peripheral arterial disease (PAD). Measure your ankle/brachial index (ABI) — at home with a Bluetooth BP cuff and a stethoscope is achievable but imperfect; if you have any pulse-absent foot, cold-pale foot, claudication (calf pain on walking that resolves on rest), or known diabetes with vascular complications, do not apply compression until ABI is confirmed >0.8 by a clinician. **Acute red-warm-painful unilateral leg swelling — STOP, this could be deep vein thrombosis (DVT), seek same-day medical care (Doppler ultrasound + D-dimer + anticoagulation if confirmed).** Sudden chest pain or shortness of breath after leg pain — this could be pulmonary embolism (PE) — call emergency services immediately. Severe venous ulcer with cellulitis (red, hot, spreading, fever) — urgent antibiotics + wound care. Pregnancy + new-onset varicose with localised pain — rule out DVT urgently. Post-thrombotic syndrome with severe edema or May-Thurner left-leg disparity — vascular workup. Severe lipodermatosclerosis + skin changes risk-ulceration — early phlebology referral.

## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 What CVI actually is

> **For you, in plain words.** CVI is the long-term failure of leg veins to return blood efficiently to the heart. Veins have one-way valves that, when they leak or weaken, allow blood to pool against gravity — causing visible varicose veins, swelling, heaviness, aching, skin changes, eczema, lipodermatosclerosis (hard brown skin) and eventually open venous ulcers. It is staged CEAP C0 (no visible signs) through C6 (active venous ulcer). It is common, progressive, and very treatable with home measures + compression — but underdiagnosed and undertreated. *(CEAP classification Eklöf 2004; ESVS guidelines)*

### 1.2 The five drivers — at a glance

> **Quick start.** Tonight: order medical-grade graduated compression stockings 20-30 mmHg knee-high (Sigvaris, Jobst, Mediven, Therafirm — consumer-purchasable; size by ankle + calf circumference measured first thing in the morning). Tomorrow: walk 30 minutes (the calf-pump is your secondary heart) + elevate legs 15-30 minutes × 3-4 times daily. This week: start horse chestnut seed extract (HCSE) standardised to escin 50 mg twice daily (the Cochrane A-grade non-Rx oral CVI agent) + photograph your worst leg from three angles for baseline. *(Pittler 2012 Cochrane; Nelson 2014 Cochrane; Padberg 2004)*

### 1.3 The deeper layer

The mainstream venous story treats CVI as a cosmetic varicose-vein issue, defers it to vascular surgery for ablation, and ignores the C0-C3 spectrum until it progresses to C4-C6 skin damage and ulceration. A more honest reframe (Cochrane meta + ESVS guidelines + classical phlebology): CVI is the predictable progressive output of **(i) venous valve incompetence** (genetic + acquired — pregnancy + previous DVT damage), **(ii) calf-pump failure** (the calf muscle is the secondary heart for venous return — when calves don't pump, blood pools), **(iii) chronic venous hypertension** (sustained high venous pressure damages capillaries, causes edema, drives skin changes), **(iv) inflammatory cascade** (leukocyte trapping in venous wall + matrix metalloproteinase activation + skin damage cascade), and **(v) lifestyle + occupational + obesity contributors** (prolonged standing + sitting + obesity all amplify venous load). **Synthesis.** A home protocol combining graduated compression + HCSE oral + structured walking + leg elevation + weight management + venoactive Cochrane B+ herbs (gotu kola + Pycnogenol + butcher's broom + MPFF where OTC) addresses all five drivers without surgery for C0-C3 and most C4. Surgery — endovenous ablation EVLA/RFA, MOCA, cyanoacrylate VenaSeal — is appropriate for selected C2-C6 patients, but is NOT a first-line home protocol; it is honestly described in §10.7.3 only.

## 2. Mechanistic Network (5-node)

### 2.1 Five causes — the picture

> **For you, in plain words.** Five causes drive CVI — valve incompetence, calf-pump failure, venous hypertension, inflammatory cascade, lifestyle contributors. Naming each lets every intervention aim at the right one. *(CEAP Eklöf 2004; Bergan 2006 NEJM CVI review)*

### 2.2 Venous valve incompetence

> **For you, in plain words.** Veins have one-way valves. When they leak (reflux), blood flows backwards under gravity and pools in the lower legs. The great saphenous vein (GSV) is the most commonly affected. Valve incompetence is genetic (60-70% have family history), and acquired (pregnancy, previous DVT, ageing). *(Bergan 2006 NEJM)*

#### Open the science

Venous valves are bicuspid structures in superficial and deep veins; they prevent reflux. Reflux is measured by Doppler ultrasound (reflux >0.5s on Valsalva or augmentation). The great saphenous vein (GSV) is incompetent in the majority of varicose-vein cases; small saphenous vein (SSV) in a minority; perforator-vein incompetence (linking superficial to deep) in another subset. Genetic factors are strong — family history is the most consistent risk factor across cohorts. Acquired factors: pregnancy (multiple pregnancies cumulatively worsen risk; the gravid uterus compresses iliac veins + progesterone weakens vein walls), previous DVT (post-thrombotic syndrome PTS produces deep-venous-insufficiency), age. Targeted by **compression therapy externally** (compresses the leg from outside, narrowing veins, restoring valve coaptation), **horse chestnut HCSE orally** (Pittler 2012 Cochrane — A-grade — venoactive, supports vein-wall integrity), **MPFF/Daflon micronised flavonoid fraction** (where OTC; Rx in some jurisdictions — Cochrane Martinez-Zapata 2020 B+), **gotu kola Centella asiatica** (Cochrane Chong 2013 B+ — venous-wall + connective-tissue support), **endovenous ablation** for selected cases (§10.7.3 clinic route — A-grade per CLASS 2014, Rasmussen 2010, Carradice 2011, EVRA 2018). **Synthesis.** Once a valve is anatomically incompetent, you cannot regenerate it with herbs — but you can manage the haemodynamic consequences indefinitely with compression + venoactives. Ablation removes the incompetent vein.

### 2.3 Calf-pump failure

> **For you, in plain words.** The calf muscle is the secondary heart — when you walk, calf contraction squeezes deep veins and pushes blood upward against gravity. Sedentary lifestyle + prolonged sitting/standing without walking = calf-pump failure. Padberg 2004 RCT showed structured walking improves haemodynamics. *(Padberg 2004; Klonizakis 2018; Bergan 2006)*

#### Open the science

The musculo-venous calf pump generates an estimated 200 mmHg pressure during contraction, propelling blood toward the heart against gravity. Calf-pump failure occurs from prolonged immobility (sitting, standing, hospitalisation), age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia), neurological disease (stroke, neuropathy), and disuse. Padberg 2004 RCT — supervised exercise + compression vs compression alone showed improved ejection fraction + reduced reflux on plethysmography. Klonizakis 2018 — structured walking program improved CVI haemodynamics. Hamdan 2014 — exercise improves microcirculatory function. Targeted by **structured walking 30 minutes daily** (the cheapest, highest-leverage intervention — A-grade), **calf raises + tip-toe exercises daily** (10-15 reps × 3 sets), **standing-desk + sit-stand alternation timer** (every 30-60 minutes change posture), **dorsiflexion-plantarflexion in seated position** (airline-passenger ankle circles — for long sitting), **structured cycling + swimming** (low-impact + calf pump activation), **structured yoga viparita karani inverted-pose** (combines elevation + relaxation), **tai chi for balance + circulation**, **avoid prolonged standing/sitting without movement** (occupational ergonomics conversation). **Synthesis.** Walking is the foundational behavioural intervention. If you cannot walk, calf raises + dorsiflexion + pneumatic compression substitute partially.

### 2.4 Chronic venous hypertension

> **For you, in plain words.** When blood pools, venous pressure stays chronically high. This pressure pushes fluid out of capillaries (edema), damages capillary walls, deposits iron + haemoglobin in skin (haemosiderin = brown staining), and eventually destroys subcutaneous tissue (lipodermatosclerosis) and skin (venous ulcer). Compression reverses the haemodynamics from outside. *(Coleridge-Smith 1988; Browse 1982)*

#### Open the science

Chronic venous hypertension is the pathophysiological hub of CVI. The Coleridge-Smith 1988 white-cell-trapping hypothesis posits that chronic venous hypertension causes leukocyte adherence to endothelium, activation, release of matrix metalloproteinases, capillary damage, fluid + protein + RBC extravasation, haemosiderin deposition, lipodermatosclerosis, and ultimately venous ulceration. Browse 1982 fibrin-cuff hypothesis added the role of pericapillary fibrin. The "ankle blow-out" of severe CVI shows haemosiderin staining + lipodermatosclerosis. Targeted by **graduated compression** (the gold-standard externally-applied haemodynamic reverser — Cochrane Nelson 2014 A-grade), **leg elevation 15-30 minutes × 3-4 daily** (gravity-reversal — passive venous drainage), **weight management** (every kg of weight increases venous load), **pneumatic compression sleeves** (Normatec, Therabody RecoveryAir, Hyperice — mechanical augmentation for severe edema or when stockings insufficient), **multilayer compression bandaging** for active venous ulcers (O'Meara 2012 Cochrane A-grade — note: home application requires proper instruction; active venous ulcers C6 require professional dressing — §10.7.3). **Synthesis.** Compression is the single most evidence-supported intervention in CVI history. Wear stockings during waking hours; elevate legs at rest. Both are non-negotiable.

### 2.5 Inflammatory cascade

> **For you, in plain words.** Chronic venous hypertension triggers a low-grade inflammatory cascade in the vein wall and skin — matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), leukocyte trapping, oxidative stress. Anti-inflammatory venoactives (HCSE escin, Pycnogenol, gotu kola, MPFF) target this cascade. *(Belcaro 2003; Bergan 2006)*

#### Open the science

The CVI inflammatory cascade involves leukocyte adherence to venous endothelium, ICAM/VCAM upregulation, MMP-2 and MMP-9 activation, oxidative stress, and TGF-β-mediated tissue remodelling. This is the molecular substrate of skin damage in C4-C6. Targeted by **horse chestnut HCSE escin** (anti-inflammatory + capillary-sealing + diuretic — Pittler 2012 Cochrane A-grade — gold-standard non-Rx CVI oral), **gotu kola Centella asiatica** (TECA — titrated extract of Centella asiatica — Belcaro 1990 + Pointel 1987 + Cochrane Chong 2013 — B+ — venous-wall + connective-tissue support, capillary stabilisation), **Pycnogenol French maritime pine bark** (Belcaro 2006 RCT B+ — anti-inflammatory + microcirculation), **grape seed extract OPC oligomeric proanthocyanidins** (moderate signal — venoactive flavonoid family), **butcher's broom Ruscus aculeatus** (B+ traditional — α-adrenergic agonist + anti-inflammatory venoactive), **MPFF/Daflon micronised purified flavonoid fraction** (diosmin 90% + hesperidin 10% — Cochrane Martinez-Zapata 2020 B+ — note: Rx-only in some jurisdictions including US — OTC in EU, India, Asia, Latin America), **bromelain** (modest signal — anti-inflammatory + fibrinolytic), **omega-3 EPA/DHA** (general anti-inflammatory). **Synthesis.** Venoactives reduce symptoms (heaviness, aching, edema, cramps) by 30-50% across trials; they do not reverse anatomical valve damage but durably improve quality of life.

## 3. OTC Phytochemicals — Pharmacological Validation

### 3.1 What to take, what to skip

> **For you, in plain words.** Eight over-the-counter venoactives are supported. The gold-standard is horse chestnut seed extract (HCSE) standardised to 50 mg escin/aescin twice daily — A-grade per Cochrane Pittler 2012, comparable to compression stockings for symptom relief in some trials. Read the plain-English column first. *(Pittler 2012 Cochrane; Belcaro 2006; Chong 2013 Cochrane)*

### 3.2 Horse chestnut HCSE + MPFF — the cornerstone pair

> **For you, in plain words.** Horse chestnut HCSE standardised to escin 50 mg twice daily is the Cochrane A-grade non-Rx oral CVI agent — reduces leg pain, heaviness, itching, and ankle/calf swelling comparably to compression stockings in some trials (Pittler 2012). MPFF (micronised purified flavonoid fraction — Daflon 500 mg twice daily; diosmin 90% + hesperidin 10%) is Cochrane B+ — Rx-only in some jurisdictions (US), but OTC in EU, India, Asia, Latin America. *(Pittler 2012 Cochrane HCSE; Martinez-Zapata 2020 Cochrane MPFF)*

#### Open the science

Aesculus hippocastanum (horse chestnut) seed extract standardised to 16-20% triterpene saponins (the escin/aescin family). Mechanism: escin closes the venous endothelial gaps that produce capillary leakage, reduces lysosomal enzyme activity in vein wall, increases venous tone via stimulation of prostaglandin F2α, has mild anti-edema diuretic effect. Pittler 2012 Cochrane meta — 17 RCTs, ~1,500 patients — consistent symptom reduction (leg pain, heaviness, itching, paraesthesia, swelling, ankle/calf circumference) — A-grade. Importantly, head-to-head with compression stockings, HCSE was non-inferior in symptom relief in some trials (Diehm 1996). Dose: HCSE standardised to 50 mg escin twice daily (typical preparation: 300 mg HCSE standardised to 50 mg escin). **Cautions:** raw horse chestnut is TOXIC — only use standardised pharmaceutical-grade extract; avoid in renal/hepatic failure; pregnancy use limited data — consult prescriber. **Brands:** Venostat (Switzerland), Venoplant (Germany), Aescusan (EU), Solgar (international), NOW Foods, Nature's Way Standardised. **Grade A.**

MPFF (micronised purified flavonoid fraction — Daflon, also marketed as Venitan, Detralex, Alvenor) — diosmin 90% + hesperidin 10%, particle-size-reduced for bioavailability. Mechanism: protects venous tone, reduces capillary hyperpermeability, anti-inflammatory + leukocyte-trapping reducer. Cochrane Martinez-Zapata 2020 — symptom improvement (heaviness, pain, cramps, paraesthesia) and reduction in functional impairment — B+. Belcaro 2002 + Bogachev 2014 — moderate-to-good signal for chronic venous symptoms. Particularly used in adjunctive role for venous-ulcer healing (Coleridge-Smith 2005). Dose: Daflon 500 mg twice daily; or 1000 mg once daily new formulation. **OTC in EU, India, Asia, Latin America, Russia. Prescription-only in US.** **Grade B+.**

## 4. Traditional Medicine Integration

### 4.1 Ayurveda — varicose veins as siragata kapha-vata

> **For you, in plain words.** Ayurveda views varicose veins as *siragata vata* (vata dosha in the vessel) combined with *kapha-stasis* — sluggish circulation in cold-stagnant pattern. The fix is herbs that move stagnation + tonify vessels + reduce edema. Triphala + Manjishtha + Punarnava + Ashwagandha + Arjuna + Guggul + Mahanarayan taila external. *(Charaka Samhita; Sushruta Samhita; AYUSH CCRAS)*

#### Open the science

**Triphala** (Haritaki + Bibhitaki + Amalaki) — daily systemic-circulation rasayana, mild astringent + bowel-regulating (constipation worsens venous load — straining raises intra-abdominal pressure). 3-5 g night. **Manjishtha (Rubia cordifolia)** — classical blood + vascular-cleanser; raktashodhana. 500 mg-1 g twice daily. **Punarnava (Boerhavia diffusa)** — diuretic + anti-edema for venous-edema pattern; classical mutrala. 500 mg-1 g twice daily. **Ashwagandha** — sthana-balya tissue-strengthening rasayana — supportive vein-wall tone. 300-600 mg twice daily. **Brahmi (Bacopa monnieri)** — endothelial + general circulation support. 500 mg twice daily. **Arjuna (Terminalia arjuna)** — cardiovascular-venous tonic; bark-decoction classical. 500 mg-1 g twice daily. **Guggul (Commiphora mukul)** — for inflammatory venous pattern, lipid-modulating. 500 mg-1 g twice daily (avoid hyperthyroidism). **Pippali (Piper longum)** — kapha-stagnation-circulation enhancer; small dose 100-250 mg. **Mahanarayan taila** external for leg-massage — daily padabhyanga along varicose veins, against gravity (toward heart), morning before stockings; cinnamon + sesame base. **Classical formulas:** Manjishtadi kashayam (decoction), Sariva pana (Hemidesmus indicus tonic), Triphala guggulu. **Padabhyanga + leg-elevation + walking** as classical regimen.

### 4.2 TCM — blood stasis pattern in the lower extremity

> **For you, in plain words.** TCM views varicose veins and CVI as *blood stasis* (Xue Yu) in the lower extremity, often with qi-deficiency-blood-stasis pattern in elderly + post-DVT. ## 5. Biophysical Interventions — home-administered only

### 5.1 What you'll use this for

> **For you, in plain words.** Six home modalities physically support CVI management — medical-grade graduated compression stockings (A-grade), pneumatic compression sleeves, leg-elevation pillows, calf-pump exercise tracking, smart-compression Bauerfeind, structured sit-stand alternation timer. *(Nelson 2014 Cochrane; Mosti 2018)*

### 5.5 CIVIQ-20 self-administered quality of life

> **For you, in plain words.** The CIVIQ-20 (Chronic Venous Insufficiency Quality of life Questionnaire) — 20 items, self-administered, validated globally. Score baseline + every 8 weeks. Domains: pain, physical, psychological, social. *(Launois 1996 CIVIQ-20)*

### 5.6 Home compression-bandaging training

> **For you, in plain words.** For active venous ulcers (C6), multilayer compression bandaging is the A-grade healing intervention (O'Meara 2012 Cochrane). After training, daily/twice-weekly home self-application becomes feasible. Active venous ulcers are §10.7.3 wound-care nurse co-managed. *(O'Meara 2012; Mosti 2018)*

## 6. Dietary Protocol

### 6.1 Mandatory daily foods

> **For you, in plain words.** CVI nutrition prioritises anti-inflammatory + circulation-supporting + weight-management + constipation-prevention (straining raises intra-abdominal pressure + worsens CVI). Daily essentials — leafy greens, berries (flavonoids + OPC), fatty fish (omega-3), high-fibre legumes + millets (constipation prevention), fermented food, water hydration, gotu kola tea. *(Cesarone 2006 Pycnogenol diet adjunct; Mori 2009 omega-3)*

- **Leafy greens 200-300 g** — vitamin K + folate + magnesium + antioxidants
- **Berries 100-150 g** — natural flavonoids + OPC (the same family as MPFF)
- **Fatty fish 120 g × 3-4/week** — omega-3 EPA/DHA anti-inflammatory
- **Legumes + slow carbs (sprouted millets, dal, lentils)** — fibre + constipation prevention
- **Fermented food 1 portion/day** — kefir, kanji, sauerkraut (microbiome)
- **Citrus 1-2 daily** — natural diosmin + hesperidin precursors
- **Water 2-3 L daily** — hydration (paradoxically reduces edema by preventing antidiuretic response)
- **Gotu kola tea 1-2 cups/day** — venoactive Cochrane B+ daily adjunct

> **For you, in plain words.** Padberg 2004 + Klonizakis 2018 + Hamdan 2014 — A-grade for calf-pump activation. Aim 30 minutes daily; can be split into 3 × 10-min walks if 30-minute sustained walks not tolerable. Add 5-minute warm-up + cool-down. *(Padberg 2004; Klonizakis 2018; Hamdan 2014)*

> **For you, in plain words.** 10-15 reps × 3 sets daily; can do while brushing teeth, on phone, at sink. Builds gastrocnemius + soleus pump capacity. Combined with walking, this is the calf-pump foundation.

### 7.5 Leg elevation 15-30 min × 3-4 daily

> **For you, in plain words.** Elevate legs higher than heart for 15-30 minutes, 3-4 times per day — gravity-reversal for venous drainage. Combine with reading, watching TV, work calls. Lounge Doctor + Mediflow + Brentwood Home pillows make it ergonomic.

### 7.6 Weight management

> **For you, in plain words.** Every kg of weight increases venous load + intra-abdominal pressure. BMI-CVI relationship is linear-progressive (Vlajinac 2003). Weight loss in obese reduces CVI symptoms and prevents progression. *(Vlajinac 2003)*

### 7.7 CBT for chronic-wound depression

> **For you, in plain words.** Chronic venous ulcers (C6) carry significant depression burden (Renner 2009 — depression in ~30% of venous-ulcer patients). CBT for chronic-wound depression is supportive. *(Renner 2009)*

### 7.8 Pregnancy + post-partum compression

> **For you, in plain words.** Robertson 2014 + Tarique 2015 — A-grade preventive — maternity compression stockings during pregnancy prevent + reduce varicose progression. Continue 6-12 weeks post-partum. *(Robertson 2014; Tarique 2015)*

### 10.8 Red-flag escalation

> **For you, in plain words.** Stop the home protocol and **seek immediate clinical care** if any of: **acute red-warm-painful unilateral leg swelling** (rule out DVT — URGENT Doppler ultrasound + D-dimer + anticoagulation if confirmed — call same-day for emergency assessment); **sudden chest pain + shortness of breath after leg pain** (rule out pulmonary embolism PE — call emergency services immediately); **severe venous ulcer with cellulitis** (red, hot, spreading, fever — urgent antibiotics + wound care); **pregnancy + new-onset varicose with localised pain** (rule out DVT urgent); **arterial-overlap with absent pulses + cold-pale foot** (ABI <0.5 = arterial — compression CONTRAINDICATED; vascular surgery referral); **post-thrombotic syndrome with severe edema** (haematology + vascular workup); **May-Thurner left-leg disparity** (vascular workup); **severe lipodermatosclerosis + skin changes** risk-ulceration (early phlebology referral); **hypercoagulability workup for unprovoked DVT + recurrent** (haematology); **suspected septic skin lesion** (urgent abx + wound care).

## 11. Self-Administered Clinical Scoring

### 11.1 The six-instrument home assessment

> **For you, in plain words.** Six tools — CEAP staging photos monthly, CIVIQ-20 every 8 weeks, AVVQ every 8 weeks, VCSS monthly, ankle + calf circumference weekly first-thing-AM, home ABI proxy with BP cuff every 8 weeks. *(Eklöf 2004; Launois 1996; Garratt 1993; Vasquez 2010)*

### 11.2 CEAP self-staging

> **For you, in plain words.** Photograph both legs from three angles (anterior, lateral, posterior) monthly. Match to CEAP descriptions — C0 (no visible signs) → C1 (telangiectasias) → C2 (varicose veins ≥3 mm) → C3 (edema) → C4a (eczema/pigmentation) → C4b (lipodermatosclerosis) → C5 (healed ulcer) → C6 (active ulcer). Track stage progression or regression over months. *(Eklöf 2004)*

### 11.3 CIVIQ-20 + AVVQ quality-of-life

> **For you, in plain words.** CIVIQ-20 (20 items, 4 domains — pain, physical, psychological, social) and AVVQ (Aberdeen Varicose Vein Questionnaire — varicose-specific) are validated self-administered tools. Score baseline + every 8 weeks. *(Launois 1996; Garratt 1993)*

### 11.4 Ankle + calf circumference

> **For you, in plain words.** Measure ankle (figure-of-8 around malleolus) + maximum calf circumference weekly, first thing AM before stockings + before edema develops. Decreases over weeks confirm intervention working. Same measurer + same tape + same time of day.

### 11.5 Home ABI proxy

> **For you, in plain words.** Bluetooth BP cuff (Withings, Omron) measure systolic at brachial (arm) + at posterior tibial (ankle) + dorsalis pedis (foot). ABI = ankle systolic / brachial systolic. Normal 0.9-1.4. <0.9 = peripheral arterial disease (compression contraindicated <0.5). Imperfect home measurement — use as trend; confirm clinically if abnormal.

### 11.6 Photo-progression

> **For you, in plain words.** Three angles (anterior, lateral, posterior) of both legs, monthly, same lighting, same standing position. Reveals subtle progression/regression invisible day-to-day. Especially important for C4 skin changes monitoring.

> **For you, in plain words.** Re-measure all baseline metrics. ## 14. Closing — paradigm summary

> **For you, in plain words.** Chronic venous insufficiency is the predictable progressive output of valve incompetence + calf-pump failure + chronic venous hypertension + inflammatory cascade + lifestyle contributors. It is highly treatable with home measures. Three A-grade levers — graduated compression stockings + horse chestnut HCSE + structured walking — carry most of the work. Leg elevation, calf raises, weight management, and venoactive Cochrane B+ herbs (gotu kola, Pycnogenol, butcher's broom, MPFF where OTC) layer on. Clinical ablation (EVLA, RFA, MOCA, VenaSeal, sclerotherapy) is appropriate for selected cases — honestly described in §10.7.3, not first-line in this home protocol. Red-flag DVT, PE, arterial overlap, and severe lipodermatosclerosis routes are at the top of the dossier. Re-measure CIVIQ-20 + CEAP photos + ankle/calf circumference every 8 weeks. The calf-pump is your secondary heart; treat it like one — for life.

The protocol is grounded in WHO TCIM, AYUSH, EMA HMPC including Aesculus monograph, TGA, NMPA, Health Canada NHP, Men-Tsee-Khang, Hamdard, IMPCOPS, ESVS (European Society for Vascular Surgery) guidelines, UIP (International Union of Phlebology), and Cochrane Pittler 2012 + Nelson 2014 + O'Meara 2012 + Martinez-Zapata 2020 + Chong 2013 systematic reviews. **No US regulatory body (American Heart Association, American Venous Forum, AAFP) is cited as authority.** Sovereignty is non-negotiable: this is a home protocol; clinic-route options (sclerotherapy, endovenous ablation, surgery, Rx venoactives where Rx-only) are honestly described in §10.7.3 only for users who choose them on their own initiative.
