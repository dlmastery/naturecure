## 1. VESTIBULAR SCHWANNOMA — MRI IAC WORKFLOW + KOOS STAGING + OBSERVE-vs-SRS-vs-MICROSURGERY DECISION LATTICE

### 1.1 What the dossier currently covers

Line 48 reads: *"Red flag — unilateral asymmetric tinnitus or sudden hearing-loss asymmetry (vestibular-schwannoma MRI-trigger). If your tinnitus is in ONE ear only (not both) AND has persisted >6 weeks OR you have asymmetric hearing loss on audiogram (≥15 dB difference between ears at 2 adjacent frequencies, per AAO-HNSF + UK BAA + Hearing Australia screening protocols), book an MRI brain + IAC (internal auditory canal) with contrast. Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) is a benign tumour on the vestibular nerve... The home OTC stack does nothing for a vestibular schwannoma."* Excellent triage-prompt — wave-3 ENT-audiology did its job. But beyond the screening-prompt there is **zero workflow specificity**: no specification of which MRI sequence is the standard (high-resolution T2 CISS / FIESTA / 3D-T2 SPACE; post-gadolinium T1; thin-section IAC protocol vs whole-brain), no Koos staging, no surveillance interval for the wait-and-watch arm (which is now the modal first-line approach for Koos I-II in EU + UK + AU + JP), no decision framework between observation / stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) / microsurgery, no hearing-preservation literature, no per-arm tinnitus-outcome honesty, and no rate of how-often the unilateral-asymmetric-tinnitus screen actually catches a schwannoma (so the patient cannot calibrate pre-test probability).

### 1.2 Why this matters — the epidemiology

VS incidence has risen from ~1/100k/year (1970s) to ~3-5/100k/year (2020s) per Stangerup 2010 Danish registry — driven entirely by **MRI access**. Of all newly-diagnosed VS, **~30-50% present with asymmetric tinnitus as chief complaint** before audiometric asymmetry is detectable (Selesnick 1992 + Sheppard 1996 + Lin 2005). Of the asymmetric-tinnitus + asymmetric-SNHL cohort meeting the dossier's ≥15 dB / 2-adjacent-frequency screen, **MRI-detected VS yield is ~1-5%** (Saliba 2011; Carrier 2024). The honest pre-test probability the dossier should surface: of ~100 readers meeting the screen, ~1-5 will have a schwannoma — high-sensitivity-low-specificity by design. Framing should reassure most readers (most MRIs come back negative) while preserving urgency for the small high-stakes minority.

### 1.3 The MRI protocol the dossier needs to name

"MRI brain + IAC with contrast" is not granular enough. The actual modern standard (per EAONO + EANS skull-base section + the Tokyo Consensus VS imaging protocol):

- **Primary screening sequence:** 3D high-resolution T2 (CISS on Siemens / FIESTA on GE / DRIVE on Philips / Balanced FFE) — sub-millimetre slices through the IAC + CPA. Detects schwannomas as small as 1-2 mm with sensitivity ~98%. **This sequence does not require gadolinium contrast.**
- **Confirmatory / characterisation:** Post-gadolinium T1 fat-saturated sequence — required to distinguish schwannoma from epidermoid, lipoma, meningioma, and to characterise extra-meatal extension.
- **Anatomic detail:** 3T scanner strongly preferred over 1.5T for IAC-region resolution.
- **What to ASK FOR at booking:** *"MRI internal auditory canals with high-resolution T2 (CISS / FIESTA / DRIVE) plus post-contrast T1 fat-saturation — to exclude vestibular schwannoma."* This phrasing routes correctly through radiology booking systems in UK NHS, NHS-private (Bupa / Spire / Nuffield), DE GKV (HNO-Überweisung), AU Medicare-rebated radiology, and IN tertiary-hospital radiology. **The patient-script load-bearing detail:** if the booking is left as "MRI brain with contrast" the radiology centre may default to a routine 5-mm-slice protocol that misses small intracanalicular schwannomas. The CISS / FIESTA specification is the non-negotiable.

### 1.4 Koos staging — the framework the patient needs for §11 / §15

The Koos grading system (Koos 1998; Hannover Skull Base Foundation) is the load-bearing decision-lattice anchor:

- **Koos I:** Intracanalicular only (confined to IAC). Tumour ≤10 mm. Modal first-line: **active surveillance** with serial MRI at 6 + 12 months, then annual. ~40-50% never grow (Stangerup natural-history series).
- **Koos II:** Extension into CPA cistern, ≤2 cm. Surveillance vs SRS vs microsurgery — decision-lattice opens.
- **Koos III:** CPA contact with brainstem, 2-3 cm. SRS or microsurgery typically indicated.
- **Koos IV:** Brainstem compression, >3 cm. Microsurgery typically primary; SRS limited by volume.

The dossier's failure to surface Koos staging means the unilateral-asymmetric-tinnitus patient who books an MRI per the screening prompt receives a radiology report with "Koos II schwannoma" and has no framework to interpret it. The dossier need not become a neurosurgical textbook — but a 3-line plain-English Koos summary in §11 + a §15.3-new "what your MRI report means" block belong on the page.

### 1.5 The observe / SRS / microsurgery decision lattice

For Koos I-II VS (the modal newly-diagnosed presentation in 2026 because MRI catches them earlier), three legitimate options exist:

- **Active surveillance ("watch and scan"):** Serial MRI at 6 + 12 months then annual; ~40-50% never grow; growth rate when present 1-3 mm/year. Intervention triggered by documented growth, hearing deterioration, or brainstem contact. Modal first-line in 2026 EU + UK + AU + JP.
- **Stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS):** Gamma Knife (Leksell), CyberKnife (Accuray), LINAC-based (Elekta Versa / Varian TrueBeam / BrainLab Novalis). Single-fraction 12-13 Gy to tumour margin. Tumour control ~93-97% at 10 years (Lunsford 2005; Hasegawa 2013). Useful-hearing preservation ~50-70% at 5 years declining over time. Facial-nerve preservation >95%. **Tinnitus outcome: ~30% improve, ~50% unchanged, ~15-20% worsen** (Lin 2005; Park 2011).
- **Microsurgery:** Three approaches per tumour size + hearing status:
  - **Retrosigmoid (suboccipital):** Most-used; any size; possible hearing preservation; cerebellar retraction.
  - **Middle-fossa:** Small intracanalicular tumours (Koos I); best hearing-preservation ~60-70%; temporal-lobe retraction.
  - **Translabyrinthine:** Any size; **sacrifices residual hearing in operated ear**; facial-nerve identification at fundus; no cerebellar retraction. *The dossier's §5.9 hearing-aid masker is irrelevant in the operated ear post-translabyrinthine — the cochlea is anatomically removed.*

**Tinnitus outcome by treatment arm — the honest framing the dossier owes the patient:** No arm reliably improves tinnitus. SRS, microsurgery (any approach), and observation all show ~30% improve / ~50% unchanged / ~20% worsen distributions. **The home OTC + CBT-T + sound-therapy stack remains relevant in all post-VS-treatment scenarios** — not as a substitute for treatment, but as the persistent-tinnitus management layer on top of whichever treatment arm the MDT chooses. This protects the dossier from the implicit framing that surgery / SRS "fixes" tinnitus.

## 2. SKULL-BASE TUMOURS BEYOND VS — MENINGIOMA + PARAGANGLIOMA + ENDOLYMPHATIC-SAC TUMOUR + NF2 / VHL ASSOCIATIONS

### 2.1 What the dossier currently covers

The non-VS skull-base tumour universe is **structurally invisible** in the current dossier.

### 2.2 The differential the MRI-IAC actually rules in

When the asymmetric-tinnitus patient books the §11 MRI and the report shows a CPA / IAC / petrous-temporal-bone lesion that is **not** VS, the realistic differential includes:

- **CPA meningioma (~10-15% of CPA masses):** Origin from petroclival / posterior surface of petrous bone. Dural-tail sign on post-contrast T1; broader-based bone contact than schwannoma; calcification ~20%. Tinnitus in ~30-40%. Management: observation small; SRS (Gamma Knife / CyberKnife / LINAC, 13-15 Gy margin) intermediate; microsurgery (retrosigmoid / petrosal / transpetrosal) for large or brainstem-compressing. Tinnitus outcome similar to VS.
- **Petroclival + foramen-magnum meningioma:** Distinct skull-base sub-classes; petroclival often involves cranial nerves V-VI-VII-VIII; modal first-line increasingly Gamma Knife SRS in EU + UK skull-base practice; far-lateral / suboccipital approaches for foramen magnum.
- **Jugulotympanic paraganglioma (glomus jugulare + glomus tympanicum):** **Single most-missed cause of pulsatile tinnitus with otoscopic-visible red mass behind tympanic membrane.** Glomus bodies of jugular bulb or middle-ear promontory. Female predominance 4:1. Pulsatile tinnitus + conductive hearing loss + (large) lower-cranial-nerve palsies (Fisch A-D). Imaging: HRCT temporal bone + post-contrast T1 + MRA / 4D-CTA shows "salt-and-pepper" flow voids. **Otoscopy shows red mass behind eardrum — pathognomonic when present.** Management: observation Fisch A; SRS intermediate; microsurgery (transtemporal / infratemporal Fisch / transcervical-transmastoid) for large; pre-operative embolisation usually required. **The pulsatile-tinnitus red flag must direct the patient to insist on otoscopic exam before any vascular imaging** — a red mass behind the eardrum completely re-routes the workup.
- **Glomus vagale + carotid-body paraganglioma:** Neck-mass + pulsatile-tinnitus; skull-base-adjacent; surgical or SRS with pre-op embolisation.
- **Endolymphatic-sac tumour (ELST):** Rare but high-yield. Sporadic ~60%; **VHL association ~40%** — bilateral ELST near-pathognomonic for VHL. Progressive SNHL + tinnitus + later vestibular symptoms; mimics Ménière's. Imaging: HRCT retrolabyrinthine destruction; MRI hypervascular lesion at posterior temporal bone. **If chronic-tinnitus patient has bilateral progressive SNHL + VHL family history (retinal haemangioblastomas, renal cysts / RCC, phaeochromocytoma) — VHL screen + bilateral ELST exclusion belongs on workup.**
- **NF2 (Neurofibromatosis type 2):** Bilateral VS is **pathognomonic for NF2**. Presents adolescence / early adulthood with bilateral progressive SNHL + tinnitus + balance symptoms. NF2 specialist centres (UK: Manchester + Cambridge; AU: Westmead; IN: AIIMS + NIMHANS). Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) off-label hearing-preservation signal (Plotkin 2009; UK NF2 Bevacizumab Programme). ABI relevant for bilateral anatomic deafness. **Patient under 30 with bilateral asymmetric tinnitus + bilateral progressive SNHL — NF2 screen belongs on workup.**

### 2.3 The otoscopic-examination gate the dossier omits

The wave-3 ENT-audiology reviewer flagged that audiologists cannot prescribe steroids; the analogous gate I am flagging here is that **only an ENT or otologist with otoscopy can rule in / rule out a jugulotympanic paraganglioma visible behind the eardrum**. The pulsatile-tinnitus patient who books straight to MRI brain + MRA + MRV without an otoscopic exam may receive a normal vascular study + miss the red mass behind the tympanic membrane that requires a different workflow (temporal-bone CT + 4D-CTA + interventional-neuroradiology pre-op embolisation + Fisch-classification skull-base surgery). **The pulsatile-tinnitus red-flag block needs an "ENT-otoscopy first, then imaging" routing line.**

## 3. PULSATILE TINNITUS ADVANCED WORKUP — DURAL AVF + AVM + VENOUS-SINUS STENOSIS + IIH

### 3.1 What the dossier currently covers

Line 46 reads: *"Realistic imaging ladder: MRI brain + MRA brain + neck + MRV (first-line, rules out vestibular schwannoma + venous sinus stenosis + dehiscence + most masses simultaneously); HRCT temporal bone (second-line if sigmoid sinus dehiscence or superior canal dehiscence suspected); digital subtraction angiography (third-line if AVM / dural fistula suspected). Pulse-synchronous (matches your heartbeat) vs non-pulse-synchronous (matches your breathing or jaw movement) distinction matters — non-pulse-synchronous palatal myoclonus, stapedial myoclonus, or patulous eustachian tube route to ENT for nasendoscopy, NOT vascular imaging."* This is a meaningful improvement on the wave-1 framing — but it compresses 9-12 named lesion classes into a 4-line ladder, conflates specialty routing, and omits the single highest-yield reversible pulsatile-tinnitus aetiology in 2026 (venous-sinus stenosis + IIH).

### 3.2 The differential the pulsatile-tinnitus patient actually has

Per Sismanis 2011 + Hofmann 2013 + Pegge 2017 + UK Pulse pulsatile-tinnitus pathway + Cleveland Pulsatile Tinnitus Center cumulative series:

- **Arterial:** atherosclerotic carotid stenosis (older, vascular risk profile, carotid duplex, vascular surgery); fibromuscular dysplasia (younger, female, "string of beads" on MRA, interventional neuroradiology); carotid artery dissection (neck pain + Horner's + stroke risk, urgent MRA / CTA, vascular neurology); aberrant ICA + persistent stapedial artery (congenital, HRCT diagnostic, otologic surgical risk).
- **Venous:**
  - **Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) + venous-sinus stenosis** — **the highest-yield reversible cause in 2026**; female + overweight + reproductive-age; pulsatile tinnitus + headache + visual obscurations + papilloedema; MRV + LP opening pressure; venous-sinus stenting (Patsalides + Levitt + Higgins) increasingly first-line over CSF-shunting. **The dossier's complete omission of IIH is the largest reversible-aetiology miss in §0.**
  - Sigmoid sinus dehiscence / diverticulum (HRCT diagnostic; sigmoid-wall reconstruction per Eisenman curative in selected patients).
  - High-riding / dehiscent jugular bulb (anatomic variant, conservative).
  - Transverse-sinus stenosis (with or without IIH; MRV + ICP measurement).
- **Arteriovenous shunting (high-stakes):**
  - **Dural arteriovenous fistula (dAVF):** Cognard I-V (Cognard 1995). Cognard I-II without cortical-venous reflux = low haemorrhage risk; **Cognard III-V with cortical-venous reflux = neurosurgical / endovascular emergency**. Transverse + sigmoid sinus = most common pulsatile-tinnitus location. MRA can miss; **DSA gold standard**. Endovascular embolisation (Onyx / coiling) curative ~70-85%.
  - **Cerebral AVM:** Spetzler-Martin I-V; combined microsurgery + endovascular + SRS.
- **Tumours (Dimension 2).** Paraganglioma, meningioma, vascular metastases.
- **Bone:** SSCD / Minor's (Tullio + Hennebert + pseudo-conductive audiogram + autophony; 0.5-mm CT reformats; surgical resurfacing or plugging); Paget's; otosclerosis with vascular component.

### 3.3 The IIH self-screen the dossier urgently needs

Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) — increasingly recognised as a leading reversible pulsatile-tinnitus aetiology in 2026 (Higgins 2002 + Bono 2008 + Mollan 2018 + UK IIH Trial 2024). Demographic skew: female (~90%), reproductive-age (15-44), overweight (BMI typically >30). The dossier's pulsatile-tinnitus block routes to MRA / MRV — which is correct — but does not give the patient a self-screen to enrich pre-test probability for IIH:

- *"Do you also have headache that is worse on waking, worse with bending forward, or worse with cough / strain?"*
- *"Have you noticed brief seconds-long episodes of blackout / greying-out of vision (visual obscurations), especially when you stand up?"*
- *"Have you been told you have papilloedema on a recent eye exam, or have you noticed peripheral-vision loss / blind spots?"*
- *"Are you female, of reproductive age, and BMI >30? (Demographic enrichment — does not rule in or out.)"*

**≥2 yes + pulsatile tinnitus = high pre-test probability of IIH-driven venous-sinus stenosis. Urgent ophthalmology fundoscopy + neurology + MRV + (if MRV abnormal) interventional-neuroradiology referral.** The visual-obscuration screen is critical because untreated IIH causes progressive optic-nerve damage and visual loss — the pulsatile tinnitus is the warning sign that gives the patient access to a reversible diagnosis before the vision goes.

### 3.4 The specialty-routing lattice

The dossier's "arrange vascular imaging on your own initiative" is workable but lossy. Realistic specialty routing:

- **Carotid duplex first** for cervical-bruit-clinical-pattern → vascular surgery routing.
- **MRI + MRA + MRV** for any pulsatile tinnitus without obvious cervical bruit → neuro-otology / neurology routing.
- **Temporal-bone CT** if MRI suggests bone-dehiscence pattern → otology / skull-base routing.
- **DSA** if dural-AVF or AVM suspected on cross-sectional imaging → interventional neuroradiology routing.
- **Otoscopy first** if any middle-ear visible mass → ENT / otology routing (jugulotympanic paraganglioma, see Dimension 2).

### 4.2 The vestibular paroxysmia / typewriter tinnitus phenotype

The Jannetta procedure — micro-vascular decompression at the brainstem root-entry zone — was developed for trigeminal neuralgia + hemifacial spasm and extended to vestibular paroxysmia + typewriter tinnitus. Pathophysiology: a vascular loop (typically AICA, sometimes PICA or vertebral artery) compresses cranial-nerve VIII at the root-entry zone, demyelinating the segment and causing ephaptic transmission.

**Clinical phenotype:**

- **Brief paroxysmal attacks** — seconds to 1-2 minutes — patients describe as "Morse code," "typewriter," "machine-gun," "telegraph." Distinct from continuous chronic tinnitus.
- **Dozens of attacks per day.**
- **Sometimes brief positional / motion-triggered vertigo** (Bárány 2016 vestibular paroxysmia criteria: ≥10 episodes; ≤1 minute; stereotyped within-patient).
- **Carbamazepine / oxcarbazepine response 50-90%** — diagnostic-and-therapeutic test.
- **MRI 3D-CISS / FIESTA shows the vascular loop** — but loops are present in ~30% of asymptomatic patients, so imaging alone is non-diagnostic.

**Treatment ladder:** carbamazepine 100-200 mg BID titrated (or oxcarbazepine 150-300 mg BID) as diagnostic-therapeutic trial; continued medical therapy if tolerated; **micro-vascular decompression (Jannetta, retrosigmoid craniotomy, Teflon felt between loop and nerve)** if medical therapy fails. Published MVD response rates ~75-85% (Brackmann 1993; Møller 1993; Sindou 2018; Pittsburgh + Hannover + AIIMS series).

**Why this matters:** the patient with paroxysmal "typewriter" / "Morse code" tinnitus following the OTC + sound-therapy + CBT-T stack gets nothing — none of these interventions address ephaptic transmission from a vascular loop. A carbamazepine trial via neurology has very high diagnostic-therapeutic yield in this sub-cohort.

## 5. POST-SURGICAL + POST-SRS TINNITUS MANAGEMENT

### 5.1 What the dossier currently covers

Nothing. The dossier treats vestibular schwannoma and skull-base lesions purely as red-flag-route-out items. It does not address the longitudinal post-VS-treatment population — a population that grows ~3-5/100k/year + cumulative — for whom **the home OTC + CBT-T + sound-therapy stack remains highly relevant.**

### 5.2 Why the home protocol matters post-VS-treatment

Multiple longitudinal series (Lin 2005 + Park 2011 + Wackym 2018 + Carlson 2018 Mayo + Sheffield Pulse VS registry) document that **persistent or new tinnitus occurs in ~30-60% of VS patients regardless of treatment arm**:

- ~30-40% of observation-arm patients carry tinnitus at 5 years.
- ~40-50% of SRS-arm patients carry tinnitus at 5 years.
- ~50-60% of microsurgery-arm patients carry tinnitus at 5 years.
- Tinnitus is independent of hearing-preservation status.

**The home protocol is therefore not less relevant after VS treatment — it is more relevant.** The patient who has navigated active surveillance + skull-base MDT + SRS or microsurgery still needs a chronic-tinnitus self-management framework, and the dossier's §5 sound-therapy + §5.5 CBT-T + §5.7 MBCT-T + §7 neck-jaw-vagus + §6 anti-inflammatory diet + the cofactor floor of §6.7 all retain doctrine-aligned relevance.

### 5.3 The post-translabyrinthine contraindication universe

Post-translabyrinthine patients are **anatomically deaf in the operated ear** — the cochlea is removed. This changes: §5.9 OTC hearing-aid masker — irrelevant in operated ear; contralateral CROS / BiCROS / BAHA (Cochlear) / Ponto (Oticon Medical) / ADHEAR (MED-EL) become the masker layer. §11.3 pitch/loudness matching — invalid in the deaf ear. CBT-T / MBCT-T (§5.5 / §5.7) — fully relevant (tinnitus arises centrally). Cochlear implantation post-translabyrinthine — emerging option with cochlear-nerve preservation surgery + simultaneous CI (Hassepass 2016; Carlson 2019; Manchester NF2; Cambridge skull-base). ABI relevant for NF2 bilateral-translabyrinthine.

### 5.4 The post-SRS delayed-changes window

Post-SRS (Gamma Knife / CyberKnife / LINAC): delayed hearing loss (preservation ~50-70% at 5 years drops further at 10 — advise annual audiometry surveillance 10+ years); delayed tinnitus changes (~20% experience worsening or new tinnitus in 12-24-month window even with well-controlled tumour — home CBT-T + sound-therapy + cofactor layer eases chronic course); hydrocephalus risk (~1-2% in first 12-24 months for larger lesions — neurosurgical review if headache / cognitive change / gait disturbance); cyst formation (~5-10% in years post-SRS, usually silent).

### 5.5 The pre-operative consent honesty the dossier can support

Patient-literacy script at the skull-base MDT: *"What is expected tinnitus outcome for my Koos stage on this arm?"* (honest: ~30 improve / ~50 unchanged / ~15-25 worsen, invariant across arms). *"Will I retain useful hearing?"* (middle-fossa best ~60-70% for small lesions; SRS ~50-70% at 5 years declining; retrosigmoid variable; translabyrinthine sacrifices residual hearing). *"Will I need vestibular rehabilitation?"* (most benefit — Cawthorne-Cooksey + gaze stabilisation per wave-8).
