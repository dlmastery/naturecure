## Findings — five dimensions, 38 items

### Dimension 1 — Transliteration discipline across five scripts (9 items)

The dossier mixes transliteration schemes across — and even within — single sections. This is the single most visible classical-scholarship failure. A reader from the Bhandarkar Oriental Research Institute, from BUCM, or from Men-Tsee-Khang would close the dossier within thirty seconds of opening §4.

#### F1.1 — Sanskrit: IAST is absent throughout

The dossier uses Western-popularised romanisation everywhere: "Karna nada," "Sushruta," "Bhramari," "Vata," "Nadi Shodhana," "Karna Purana," "Bilva Taila," "Ashtanga Hridaya," "Saraswatarishta," "Sarivadi Vati," "Brahmi," "Ashwagandha," "Jatamansi," "Shankhpushpi," "Yashtimadhu," "Triphala," "Bhavaprakasha," "Charaka," "Chikitsa," "Sharangdhara Samhita," "Sutra," "Sushruta's Latter Tantra."

**The consensus academic form is IAST (International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration).** Every diacritic carries meaning:

- karṇa-nāda (कर्णनाद) — NOT "Karna nada" or "Karna-nada"
- Suśruta (सुश्रुत) — NOT "Sushruta" (the dental ś vs the retroflex ṣ matters; Suśruta's name is dental ś)
- Suśruta-saṃhitā Uttara-tantra (सुश्रुतसंहिता उत्तरतन्त्र) — NOT "Sushruta's Latter Tantra" (the calque "Latter Tantra" is a 1907 Bhishagratna translation idiom; modern scholars say Uttara-tantra)
- Bhrāmarī (भ्रामरी) — NOT "Bhramari"
- vāta (वात) — NOT "Vata"; the long ā matters because it distinguishes vāta from vata
- nāḍī-śodhana (नाडीशोधन) — NOT "Nadi Shodhana"; the long ā in nāḍī and the retroflex ḍ are load-bearing
- karṇa-pūraṇa (कर्णपूरण) — NOT "karna purana"; pūraṇa "filling" vs purāṇa "ancient" are different words; the long ū vs short u flips the meaning entirely
- bilva-taila (बिल्वतैल) — taila not "Taila" (Sanskrit is not capitalised mid-word)
- Aṣṭāṅga-hṛdaya (अष्टाङ्गहृदय) — NOT "Ashtanga Hridaya"; the conjunct ṅg and the vocalic ṛ are critical
- Sārasvatāriṣṭa (सारस्वतारिष्ट) — NOT "Saraswatarishta"
- Sārivādi-vaṭī (सारिवादीवटी) — NOT "Sarivadi Vati"
- Brāhmī (ब्राह्मी) — NOT "Brahmi"; the long ā and ī are diagnostic of the species (Bacopa monnieri); short "Brahmi" can refer to a different plant (Centella asiatica in some lineages)
- Aśvagandhā (अश्वगन्धा) — NOT "Ashwagandha"; the dental ś and the long ā matter
- Jaṭāmāṃsī (जटामांसी) — NOT "Jatamansi"
- Śaṅkhapuṣpī (शङ्खपुष्पी) — NOT "Shankhpushpi"; the retroflex ṣ and the conjunct ṅkh are critical (and the species attribution depends on it — Convolvulus pluricaulis vs Clitoria ternatea vs Evolvulus alsinoides; three different plants are all called "Shankhpushpi" in regional practice)
- Yaṣṭimadhu (यष्टिमधु) — NOT "Yashtimadhu"
- Triphalā (त्रिफला) — NOT "Triphala" (the long final ā is grammatically essential — it makes Triphalā a feminine compound name; without it the word is mis-grammared)
- Bhāvaprakāśa (भावप्रकाश) — NOT "Bhavaprakasha"
- Caraka (चरक) — NOT "Charaka"; in IAST c is the affricate; "ch" is reserved for the aspirate ch
- Cikitsā-sthāna (चिकित्सास्थान) — NOT "Chikitsa"
- Śārṅgadhara-saṃhitā (शार्ङ्गधरसंहिता) — NOT "Sharangdhara Samhita"
- Sūtra-sthāna (सूत्रस्थान) — NOT "Sutra"

**Recommendation.** Adopt IAST throughout, with the popular romanisation in parentheses on first occurrence for accessibility — e.g., "karṇa-nāda (popular: 'karna nada')." This is the convention of the AYUSH academic publications, the Chowkhamba Sanskrit Series, the Motilal Banarsidass editions, and every peer-reviewed Indology journal. Without it the dossier reads as Western health-blog tier, not classical-text tier.

The format used in the *Journal of Chinese Medicine* (UK, Peter Deadman editor) is the field-consensus citation form.

The dossier writes "Tannin al-udhun," "Khamira Marwareed," "Itrifal Ustukhuddus," "Itrifal Kishneezi," "Roghan-e-Banafsha," "Roghan-e-Gul," "Majoon-e-Falasfa," "Avicenna's Canon," "Qarabadeen Qadri," "Hamdard," "Dawakhana Tibbiya," "barid of dimagh," "su'al mizaj of asab al-sam'i."

The same word is sometimes transliterated two different ways.

**The academic standard for medical Unani texts is DIN 31635** (with macrons for long vowels and underdots for emphatic consonants). For Persian-derived Unani vocabulary, ISO 233-3 is used. The dossier is closer to a transliterated-popular Hamdard formulary advertisement than to a Hakim's clinical citation.

#### F1.4 — Tibetan: Wylie transliteration is absent

The dossier writes "Agar-35," "Yu-nying-25," "Sogdzin-11," "Padma 28," "rlung," "Four Tantras," "Men-Tsee-Khang Dharamsala."

**The academic standard is Wylie transliteration** (Turrell Wylie 1959). It uses precise letter-by-letter Romanisation of Tibetan script:

- a gar so lnga (ཨ་གར་སོ་ལྔ་) — Agar 35 — NOT "Agar-35"; the formula name literally means "Eaglewood/agarwood, 35 ingredients"; the Wylie form names what it actually is
- snying zhi nyer lnga (སྙིང་ཞི་ཉེར་ལྔ་) — variously "Snying zhi 25" or in some Men-Tsee-Khang formularies "Yu-nying-25" — the dossier's "Yu-nying" is a phonetic Anglicisation that mixes two different Tibetan words; the canonical Wylie is snying-zhi-nyer-lnga
- gsung 'dzin bcu gcig (གསུང་འཛིན་བཅུ་གཅིག་) — Sogdzin 11 — the dossier's "Sogdzin" is a phonetic rendering; the Wylie reveals this is "gsung 'dzin" — "speech-grasping" — the formula has a specific cognitive-acoustic indication
- rlung (རླུང་) — wind-humor — correctly used in the dossier; this is the only Tibetan term the dossier romanises with academic discipline
- rGyud bzhi (རྒྱུད་བཞི་) — "Four Tantras" — the title is conventionally given as rGyud bzhi in academic citation, NOT "Four Tantras"; the four are rTsa rgyud (Root Tantra), bShad rgyud (Explanatory Tantra), Man ngag rgyud (Instructional Tantra), Phyi ma rgyud (Subsequent Tantra). The dossier cites "Tibetan Four Tantras Book IV" — Book IV is the Phyi ma rgyud (Subsequent Tantra). For ear-disorders, the relevant material is in the Man ngag rgyud (Instructional Tantra, Book III), chapters dealing with rna ba'i nad (ear-disease) — NOT Book IV. **This is a factual citation error, not just a transliteration issue. See F2.5 below.**
- Men-Tsee-Khang (sman rtsis khang སྨན་རྩིས་ཁང་) — "house of medicine and astrology" — the dossier's "Men-Tsee-Khang Dharamsala" is the acceptable popular form; academic citation prefers sMan-rtsis-khang or its full title
- karṇa-nāda has no Tibetan parallel cited at all in §4.6 — the Tibetan equivalent of tinnitus per the rGyud bzhi is rna ba 'ur 'ur ('ur 'ur indicates buzzing/humming) or rna ba'i sgra (ear-sound). The dossier omits the indigenous Tibetan disease-name entirely, which makes the §4.6 citation framework rootless.

#### F1.7 — Vernacular disease-name omissions in §13 closing comparison and §16

The §13 evidence-tier comparison (line 1076) cites "Ayurvedic karna-purana (oil pooling)" but the Tibetan and Siddha entries reference only "Karna-purana, TCM, Unani, Siddha, Tibetan" without their indigenous disease-names. §16 closing position (line 1141) repeats this pattern. #### F1.8 — Karṇa-pūraṇa vs karṇa-pūrṇa — gerund vs adjectival form

The dossier writes "karna-purana" (line 152) and "karna purana" (line 1076). The correct Sanskrit term is karṇa-pūraṇa (कर्णपूरण) — "filling of the ear" — gerund form, the **procedure**. The adjectival form karṇa-pūrṇa (कर्णपूर्ण) — "ear-filled" — refers to the resulting **state**. These are different grammatical forms and they generate different cross-references in the classical literature. The Suśruta-saṃhitā Uttara-tantra ch. 21 verse 3 names the procedure as karṇa-pūraṇa (not karṇa-pūrṇa).

#### F1.9 — Bhrāmarī vs bhramara

Bhrāmarī (भ्रामरी, the feminine ī ending) is the **breath-practice** (mentioned in Haṭha-pradīpikā II.68 and Gheraṇḍa-saṃhitā V.78). It derives from bhramara (भ्रमर, "black bee"). The dossier conflates these throughout — "Bhramari humming breath" (line 80) — without the long ā or the long ī. Worse, the dossier credits Bhramari to S-VYASA Bengaluru without citing the Haṭha-pradīpikā verse where the practice is first textually attested. The textual citation is **Svātmārāma's Haṭha-pradīpikā** II.68, c. 15th century CE — far older and more canonical than the S-VYASA reference, which is a modern empirical RCT.

### Dimension 2 — Verse-level + chapter-level citation rigor (10 items)

#### F2.1 — Sushruta Uttara-tantra chapter 21 is the correct anchor; chapter 20 is not

The dossier cites "Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 20-21" in line 154 and "Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 21" in line 152. The discrepancy needs resolving. The Suśruta-saṃhitā Uttara-tantra (the supplementary tantra) addresses karṇa-roga (ear-diseases) in chapters 20–21 of the standard Yadavji Trikamji Acharya edition (Nirnaya Sagar Press 1938, reprinted Chaukhamba 2014):

- **Uttara-tantra ch. 20 — Karṇa-roga-nidānam** (clinical aetiology and classification of ear-diseases; 28 sub-types named, including karṇa-nāda)
- **Uttara-tantra ch. 21 — Karṇa-roga-pratiṣedham** (treatment regimen for ear-diseases, including karṇa-pūraṇa as one of the procedural therapies)

So the dossier is right to cite ch. 20–21 jointly when describing both classification and treatment. But the body of §4.2 only cites "ch. 21" which truncates the aetiology source. Better: "Suśruta-saṃhitā Uttara-tantra ch. 20 (karṇa-roga-nidānam) for the classification of karṇa-nāda as one of 28 named karṇa-roga sub-types; ch. 21 (karṇa-roga-pratiṣedham) for the karṇa-pūraṇa procedure." This is the standard form a Suśruta scholar expects.

#### F2.2 — Verse-range not cited; this is a major specificity failure

Not a single classical citation in §4 specifies a **verse range**. Every citation stops at chapter level: "Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 21." A scholar's expectation is chapter + verse range, e.g., "Suśruta Uttara-tantra 21.3-5 (karṇa-pūraṇa with seven named oils, of which kṣāra-taila is the primary anchor for vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda)." Without verse specificity, the reader cannot verify whether the cited procedure actually appears at the claimed location or whether it is being extrapolated from an adjacent verse-cluster.

The Suśruta Uttara-tantra ch. 21 has approximately 64 verses in the Yadavji edition. The karṇa-pūraṇa procedure is detailed in verses ~3–9 with seven oils named: kṣāra-taila, apāmārga-kṣāra-taila, bilva-taila, lākṣādi-taila, sarṣapa-taila, and two others depending on edition. The dossier cites only bilva-taila — **which is one of the seven named, but NOT the primary anchor for vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda**. The wave-2 EMA reviewer already caught that AYUSH-CCRAS Karṇa-roga STG 2017 names kṣāra-taila / apāmārga-kṣāra-taila as the canonical primary; this wave-3 review confirms that classical Suśruta scholarship aligns — bilva-taila is the third-line oil, not the first. **The verse-range citation discipline would have caught this.**

#### F2.3 — Edition + translator not cited

Citation form "Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 21" tells the reader nothing about which Suśruta they are reading. There are three editions and four major translations in academic circulation:

**Editions:**
- Yādavjī Trikamjī Ācārya, Nirnaya Sāgara Press, Bombay 1938 (with the Ḍalhaṇa Nibandha-saṅgraha commentary) — the consensus academic standard
- Trivikrama-ātmaja, Anantarama, Chaukhamba Surbharati Prakashan, Varanasi 2014 (reprint with Hindi gloss)
- AYUSH digitised corpus, NIIMH Hyderabad 2017 (online + searchable)

**Translations:**
- Kavirāja Kuñjalāl Bhishagratna, English translation, Calcutta 1907-1916 (3 volumes) — the oldest English translation, still widely cited but archaic
- Priyavrat Sharma, English with Sanskrit text, Chaukhamba Visvabharati, Varanasi 1999-2001 (3 volumes) — modern Sanskrit-philological accuracy
- K. R. Srikantha Murthy, English, Chaukhamba Orientalia, Varanasi 2001-2004 (3 volumes) — the most clinically usable
- G. D. Singhal, English, Banaras Hindu University 1972-1982 (with anatomical-surgical emphasis)

These translations **diverge** at karṇa-pūraṇa verses on the question of which oil is primary for vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda. Bhishagratna (1907-1916) names bilva-taila first. Sharma (2001) names kṣāra-taila first. Murthy (2004) names apāmārga-kṣāra-taila first. The AYUSH-CCRAS Karṇa-roga STG 2017 (which the wave-2 EMA reviewer cited) follows Sharma + Murthy. **The dossier's citation of bilva-taila reflects the Bhishagratna translation — which is the most outdated and which modern AYUSH practice has moved past.**

**Recommendation.** Cite the edition: "Suśruta-saṃhitā Uttara-tantra ch. 21.3-9, Yādavjī Trikamjī Ācārya 1938 edition; translation per Sharma 2001 / Murthy 2004; AYUSH-CCRAS Karṇa-roga STG 2017 confirms the same three first-line oils." This is the structure Rule 16 ClassicalRef block already mandates — the dossier just doesn't apply it yet.

#### F2.4 — Charaka Cikitsā ch. 1 cited for Brāhmī is wrong reference; the correct verse is in a different sthāna

The dossier (line 152) cites "Charaka Chikitsa ch. 1" for Brāhmī. Caraka-saṃhitā Cikitsā-sthāna ch. 1 (Rasāyana-adhyāya) is the rasāyana / rejuvenation chapter; it does mention several brāhmī-class herbs but NOT under the species attribution Bacopa monnieri specifically. Caraka's primary citations of medhya-rasāyana (brain-tonic rejuvenatives) — including specific brāhmī-class herbs — appear at Cikitsā 1.3.30 (Caraka-cikitsā I, fourth quarter, medhya-rasāyana-pāda):

> medhyā vatsakanāṭhī ca śaṅkhapuṣpī tathaiva ca |
> brāhmī rasāyanānāṃ tu śreṣṭhā medhyātamā smṛtā ||

(translation: "Medhya — vatsakanāṭhī, śaṅkhapuṣpī, brāhmī — among the rasāyanas, the foremost as medhya is recalled.")

This is the canonical Caraka verse for brāhmī as a brain-tonic. The dossier cites "Charaka Chikitsa ch. 1" which is correct at the chapter level but vague at the verse level — and **the Brāhmī cited in Caraka Cikitsā 1.3.30 is by most modern Ayurvedic-botanical scholars identified as Centella asiatica (Indian pennywort / Mandūkaparṇī), NOT as Bacopa monnieri.** The species attribution Bacopa monnieri for "Brāhmī" is largely a Kerala lineage convention; the Bengali lineage and parts of the Northern Indian lineage call Centella asiatica "Brāhmī" and call Bacopa monnieri "Jala-brāhmī" (water-brāhmī) or simply Bacopa under a different vernacular.

**This is a substantive cherry-pick that wave-1 + wave-2 reviewers did not catch** because it requires Sanskrit-botanical training to see. The dossier silently picks the Bacopa monnieri attribution (which has Australian Stough et al. cognitive RCTs) and cites Caraka Cikitsā ch. 1 as the classical anchor — but the Caraka verse 1.3.30 is the medhya-rasāyana verse that names brāhmī among rejuvenatives, and the species attribution at that verse is contested across lineages.

**Recommendation.** Cite explicitly: "Caraka-saṃhitā Cikitsā-sthāna 1.3.30 (medhya-rasāyana-pāda) names brāhmī among the four foremost brain-tonic rejuvenatives. The species attribution of 'brāhmī' is lineage-dependent: Kerala lineage identifies it as Bacopa monnieri (the species in modern Stough et al. RCTs); Bengali + Northern lineages identify it as Centella asiatica. The clinical RCT evidence cited (Stough Australia) applies specifically to Bacopa monnieri, not to the Centella asiatica reading of the same Sanskrit term." This honest disclosure is what classical-text scholarship requires.

#### F2.5 — Tibetan rGyud bzhi citation: Book IV is the wrong book for ear-disease

The dossier cites "Tibetan Four Tantras Book IV" (line 217, 221, 222) as the source for Agar-35, Yu-nying-25, Sogdzin-11. The rGyud bzhi has four books:

- **Book I — rTsa rgyud** (Root Tantra): general framework, six chapters
- **Book II — bShad rgyud** (Explanatory Tantra): anatomy, physiology, pathology, prognosis, 31 chapters
- **Book III — Man ngag rgyud** (Instructional Tantra): clinical chapters by disease category, 92 chapters — **this is the book that contains ear-disease (rna ba'i nad) chapters**
- **Book IV — Phyi ma rgyud** (Subsequent / Final Tantra): pharmacopoeia, formulae, materia medica, 25 chapters

Ear-disease (rna ba'i nad) is discussed in **Book III (Man ngag rgyud), chapters covering head-and-orifice diseases**, NOT in Book IV. The dossier's citation of "Book IV" is the citation of the pharmacopoeia section — which is where the formula recipes for Agar-35 etc. would appear, but it is NOT where the diagnostic framework for tinnitus / rna ba 'ur 'ur is found. The dossier conflates "where the formula is described" (Book IV) with "where the disease is diagnosed" (Book III). A Men-Tsee-Khang scholar would expect both citations: "Diagnosis in Man ngag rgyud (Book III), formula recipe in Phyi ma rgyud (Book IV)."

#### F2.6 — Yī zōng jīn jiàn citation for Ěr lóng zuǒ cí wán needs chapter-specificity

The dossier (line 176) cites Ěr lóng zuǒ cí wán to the "Imperial Golden Mirror" (Yī zōng jīn jiàn 醫宗金鑑, compiled 1742 CE under the Qianlong emperor by Wu Qian 吳謙 et al.). The Yī zōng jīn jiàn is a 90-volume compendium; the ear-disease chapters are in the **wài kē xīn fǎ yāo jué (外科心法要訣)** sub-volume — specifically the **erbing (耳病) sub-chapter**. A scholar's expectation: "Yī zōng jīn jiàn, wài kē xīn fǎ yāo jué, juàn (volume) X, ěrbìng-mén." Without sub-volume specification, the citation is effectively "the encyclopaedia" — not auditable.

Furthermore, the **historical origin of Ěr lóng zuǒ cí wán is contested**. Some scholars trace it to the Ming dynasty Yī xué rù mén (醫學入門, Lǐ Chān 1575 CE); others to the Qing dynasty Yī zōng jīn jiàn (1742). The dossier asserting Yī zōng jīn jiàn as the source without engaging the historiographical question is a classical-text scholarship miss.

#### F2.7 — Liù wèi dì huáng wán is mis-attributed; the canonical source is Qián Yǐ's Xiǎo'ér yào zhèng zhí jué

The dossier (line 178) correctly attributes Liù wèi dì huáng wán to "Qian Yi; NMPA." #### F2.8 — Tiān wáng bǔ xīn dān source: Shè shēng mì pōu vs She Sheng Mi Pou — and the contested author

The dossier (line 179) cites "She Sheng Mi Pou; Plum Flower" for Tiān wáng bǔ xīn dān. The text is **Shè shēng mì pōu (攝生秘剖, "Secret Dissection of Life Preservation")**, traditionally attributed to **Hóng Jī (洪基) 1638 CE**, late Ming dynasty. However, the **earliest known attestation of Tiān wáng bǔ xīn dān is actually in the Dào zàng (Daoist Canon) Yán nián yì shòu lùn (延年益壽論)**, predating Hóng Jī by several centuries. The dossier's citation is acceptable but historiographically thin.

#### F2.9 — Yī lín gǎi cuò + Wáng Qīng-rèn citation is essentially correct, but the verse-range matters

This should be disclosed honestly: "Wáng Qīng-rèn's original 1830 indication was hemiplegia from qi-deficient blood-stasis; the tinnitus application is a modern TCM-clinical extension via the shared blood-stasis pattern."

### Dimension 3 — Cherry-picking + selective traditional evidence (8 items)

#### F3.1 — Ayurveda: the dossier excludes raktamokṣaṇa (bloodletting) for raktaja karṇa-nāda

The Suśruta Uttara-tantra ch. 20 (karṇa-roga-nidānam) lists 28 karṇa-roga sub-types. One sub-type is **raktaja karṇa-nāda** — blood-vitiated tinnitus, where rakta-doṣa (blood pathology) drives the perception. The classical Suśruta treatment for raktaja karṇa-nāda is **raktamokṣaṇa** — therapeutic bloodletting, typically by jalauka-avacāraṇa (leech application) on the temporal vessels, or by śiravedha (vein-puncture). This is one of Suśruta's central surgical procedures.

The dossier does not mention raktamokṣaṇa anywhere. The reason is obvious — it doesn't fit the "OTC + home-administered" doctrine. **Fine. But that omission must be disclosed honestly.** Currently the dossier presents the Ayurvedic intervention list as if karṇa-pūraṇa + oral rasāyana were the classical first-line. They are not. They are the first-line for vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda specifically. For raktaja karṇa-nāda the classical first-line is raktamokṣaṇa, which is clinic-only.

**Recommendation.** Add a §4.2.x sub-section: "What classical Ayurveda recommends that is NOT in this home protocol — raktamokṣaṇa (therapeutic bloodletting) for raktaja karṇa-nāda is the Suśruta-named first-line treatment; clinic-only, requires trained vaidya supervision, deliberately excluded from this home OTC regime." This honesty restores classical-scholarship rigor without abandoning the OTC doctrine.

#### F3.2 — Ayurveda: rasaśāstra / herbo-mineral preparations are silently excluded

Classical Ayurveda — especially the post-13th-century Rasaśāstra tradition (Rasa-ratnākara, Rasa-tarangiṇī, Rasendra-sāra-saṅgraha) — has named herbo-mineral preparations (bhasmas, sindūras, rasa-yogas) for karṇa-roga. Examples: Tāmra bhasma (copper ash) for kapha-pradhāna karṇa-nāda; Abhraka bhasma (mica ash) for vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda; Karṇa-roga-hara-rasa (a named mercury-sulphur compound from Rasa-tarangiṇī).

The dossier excludes all of these. Again — fine, they require classical-pharmacy supervision and contain heavy metals (mercury, lead, arsenic in bhasmas — bound in non-bioavailable forms per classical doctrine, but contested by modern toxicology). But the exclusion is silent. A Rasaśāstra scholar would expect: "Classical Rasaśāstra named herbo-mineral preparations (Tāmra bhasma, Abhraka bhasma, Karṇa-roga-hara-rasa) for karṇa-roga; excluded from this home protocol because rasaśāstra preparations require trained rasa-vaidya supervision + raise modern heavy-metal safety questions in non-bound forms; deliberately not recommended."

### Dimension 4 — Cross-tradition aetiology coherence (6 items)

This is the dimension wave-1 + wave-2 reviewers could not have addressed. It is the deepest classical-text scholarship critique.

#### F4.1 — The six traditions assert mutually-exclusive aetiologies; the dossier presents them as additive

The six traditions diagnose tinnitus through six different aetiology frameworks:

| Tradition | Disease-name | Aetiology framework |
|---|---|---|
| Ayurveda | karṇa-nāda | One of three doṣa-pradhāna patterns: vāta-pradhāna (most common), pitta-pradhāna (heat-aggravated), kapha-pradhāna (mucus-stagnated); plus rakta-doṣa (blood-vitiated) sub-type |
| TCM | ěr míng / ěr lóng | One of FIVE patterns: kidney-essence (shèn jīng) deficiency, kidney-yin (shèn yīn) deficiency with empty-fire, liver-fire (gān huǒ) blazing, spleen-qi (pí qì) deficiency with phlegm-damp, blood-stasis (xuè yū) |
| Unani | ṭanīn al-udhun | One of FOUR mizāj-derangement patterns: damawī (sanguine), ṣafrāwī (bilious / yellow-bile), balghamī (phlegmatic), sawdāwī (melancholic / black-bile) |
| Tibetan | rna ba 'ur 'ur / rna ba'i sgra | One of THREE nyes pa (humor) disturbances: rlung-dominant (wind), mkhris pa-dominant (bile), bad kan-dominant (phlegm) |
| Siddha | ceviyōcai / kāṭu olikkum | One of THREE muppiṇi: vāta (வாதம்), pitta (பித்தம்), kapha (கபம்) — analogous to Ayurveda but distinct lineage; plus the more granular Tamil Siddha framework of 4448 vāta-rōka sub-types |
| Homeopathy | tinnitus | Constitutional simillimum determined by **totality of symptoms** — the modality (better/worse with movement, time, temperature, side) + the keynote + the mind-symptom + the generals — every patient gets a different remedy |

These are **mutually exclusive diagnostic frameworks**. You cannot simultaneously have (a) vāta-pradhāna karṇa-nāda AND (b) kidney-yin-deficient ěr míng AND (c) damawī ṭanīn al-udhun AND (d) rlung-dominant rna ba'i sgra. These are different ways of carving the same patient. A coherent traditional treatment requires picking one framework, doing that tradition's diagnostic workup (pulse / tongue / mizāj examination / iridology / etc.), and then drawing formulae from that tradition's matrix accordingly.

**The dossier presents formulae from all six in parallel without any decision tree.** A patient reading §4 in good faith would conclude: "I should take Saraswatarishta AND Liù wèi dì huáng wán AND Khamīrā Marwārīd AND Agar-35 AND a Brāhmī cūrṇam AND a homeopathic Chininum Sulphuricum." This is clinically incoherent. Each tradition's formula is calibrated to its own diagnostic framework's rasa-vīrya-vipāka / qi-blood-yīn-yáng / mizāj / nyes pa / muppiṇi / simillimum logic, which the others do not share. Combining them is not "synergy" — it is throwing six different competing theories at the same patient and hoping one of them resolves the symptom.

**The wave-2 integrative-clinic PI reviewer flagged this and asked for a §4.0 "pick your tradition" decision tree.** At the classical-text scholarship level, this is right.
