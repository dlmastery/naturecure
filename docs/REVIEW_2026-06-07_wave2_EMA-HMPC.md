# Wave-2 EMA HMPC Reviewer Audit — Tinnitus 2026-06-06 Revision

**Reviewer profile.** Senior EMA Committee on Herbal Medicinal Products (HMPC) assessor, 15 years drafting and reviewing herbal monographs (Ginkgo biloba 2015, Crataegus 2016, Hypericum 2018 update, Valeriana 2016, Echinacea 2014, Vitex 2018, Cimicifuga 2018 update, Melissa 2013, Passiflora 2014). Non-US-aligned. Doctrine-respecting: AYUSH / TGA / NMPA / Health Canada NHP / WHO TCIM / classical six-tradition corpus. The wave-1 backlog (`IMPROVEMENT_BACKLOG_2026-06-06.md`, 100 items) is treated as the closed set — this audit hunts only what wave 1 missed.

---

## Verdict

The 2026-06-06 revision **partially closes** the wave-1 EMA/regulatory gap. The Ginkgo HMPC indication-conflation — the singular monograph-integrity failure wave 1 fingered — has been corrected with appropriate transparency (§3.2 lines 114-118, §13.7 line 959, deprescribing §12.6). However, the revision treats Ginkgo as the *exemplar* and does not propagate the same rigour to the other ~10 EMA HMPC monograph citations that appear in the same dossier or in cross-linked sibling dossiers. Most damaging: **EMA Padma Lax monograph does not exist** in the form the dossier names; **Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) EMA HMPC is misdated and mis-classified** (TU not WEU, and the duration ceiling cited at "≤6 mo" is the HMPC ceiling for a different population context); **Hawthorn (Crataegus) WEU indication is congestive heart failure NYHA II, NOT general "BP lowering"**; **Melatonin is mis-positioned on the OTC/Rx jurisdictional matrix** for both EU and Asia; and a constellation of "EMA <herb> monograph" citations are factually empty placeholders (turmeric, antihistamine, levothyroxine, Ginkgo bleed warning, B6, vit A, vit D, NAC, statin SmPC, erection-pill SmPC, ACE-i SmPC, kava, lithium SmPC, metformin SmPC) — EMA does not publish monographs or SmPCs for most of these. The Ginkgo correction was load-bearing; the rest of the HMPC surface is still unfit for an HMPC reviewer's scrutiny.

What an EMA HMPC reviewer still needs to see: (1) a **per-citation HMPC fingerprint** — monograph EMA reference number, WEU vs TU section, indication wording (verbatim), adult dose range, paediatric exclusion text, duration-of-use ceiling, special-population warnings — for every herbal monograph cited; (2) **explicit disambiguation of EMA HMPC herbal monograph from EMA CHMP/CVMP medicinal-product SmPC** — these are different document classes with different review committees, and the dossier conflates them on >15 occasions; (3) **jurisdictional OTC-vs-Rx accuracy** per Rule 9.5 applied to ≥6 items currently silently mis-categorised; (4) **classical-formula provenance** at the level of fascicle/page/edition rather than text-name alone; and (5) honest acknowledgement that **the FDA De Novo "narrow exception" rule has crept beyond Lenire** into 4-5 silent US-regulatory citations that wave 1 missed.

## Findings (22 specific items)

### Dimension 1 — EMA HMPC monograph integrity (6 items)

**E1. Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) monograph — WEU vs TU misclassification + duration ceiling provenance gap.**
**Line 135** (§3.3 OTC table): "Black Cohosh ... For perimenopausal tinnitus only — never longer than 6 months continuous (rare liver reactions). *(Schaper 2007 Germany; EMA 2018)*". **Line 903** (§12.6 deprescribing): "Black Cohosh (perimenopausal only) | 6 months ABSOLUTE CEILING (EMA hepatic-caution)".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The current EMA HMPC Community herbal monograph for *Cimicifuga racemosa* (L.) Nutt., rhizoma (EMA/HMPC/48745/2017, adopted 2018) is a **traditional-use** monograph (Article 16d Directive 2001/83/EC), **not a well-established-use** monograph. The indication is verbatim: *"Traditional herbal medicinal product for relief of minor symptoms during menopause, such as hot flushes and profuse sweating."* **Tinnitus is not mentioned anywhere in the indication, the conditions of use, or the assessment report.** The dossier currently recommends it for "perimenopausal tinnitus" as a Grade C — that is the dossier's authorial inference, not an EMA-recognised indication, and the citation must be reframed to make that clear (same correction pattern as Ginkgo §3.2). Additionally, the "≤6 months" ceiling is the HMPC monograph's *Duration of use* text — but the verbatim text is *"The duration of use should not exceed 6 months. If the symptoms persist during the use of the medicinal product, a doctor or a qualified healthcare practitioner should be consulted"* — this is not framed as a "hepatic-caution" ceiling, even though the assessment report does include hepatic-adverse-event risk-benefit analysis. The dossier's framing inverts this: it presents the ceiling as primarily liver-safety-driven when the monograph wording places it as duration-of-symptomatic-use discipline. The hepatic monitoring guidance derives from EMA's 2007 Public Statement (EMEA/HMPC/269259/2006), **not** the 2018 monograph itself. Wave 1 caught the AFIB cross-dossier K2-warfarin gate but missed this Black Cohosh provenance layering.

**E2. EMA "Padma Lax" monograph — citation is structurally wrong.**
**Line 217** (§4.6 Tibetan): "Padma-28 (Padma Lax) has an EU herbal monograph for microcirculation. *(Tibetan Four Tantras Book IV; Men-Tsee-Khang; EMA Padma Lax monograph)*". **Line 224** (table row): "Padma-28 (Padma Lax) | EU-monographed Tibetan formula for microcirculation — relevant to blood-flow tinnitus." Same in sibling `anxiety-like-calm.md` lines 223, 230.

What an HMPC reviewer sees: There is no EMA HMPC Community herbal monograph for "Padma Lax" or "Padma-28". Padma 28 is a Swiss multi-herb traditional Tibetan product manufactured by Padma AG and registered as a medicinal product in several European countries (Switzerland, Austria, Estonia, Poland, Ukraine) under national authorisation pathways — not via the centralised EMA HMPC monograph route. The product's evidence base for **intermittent claudication** (PAOD) lies in the Drabaek 1993, Wilhelm 1998, and the Melzer 2006 meta-analysis (Atherosclerosis 189:39-46), and a small Estonian trial. The indication is **peripheral arterial occlusive disease**, NOT "microcirculation" generically, and certainly not "blood-flow tinnitus" by inferential extension. Separately, "Padma Lax" is a *different* product (a Tibetan laxative formula, also Padma AG), not the same compound — conflating Padma 28 and Padma Lax is a product-substitution error. The dossier's citation should be reframed: "Swiss national authorisation pathways (Swissmedic); Drabaek 1993 + Wilhelm 1998 + Melzer 2006 PAOD meta-analysis (intermittent claudication, NOT tinnitus); cited here on inferential cochlear-microvascular extrapolation, NOT as an EU-recognised tinnitus indication." Wave 1 missed this; the parallel error replicates in the anxiety dossier and any other dossier touching Padma.

**E3. EMA Hawthorn (Crataegus) WEU indication is decline-of-cardiac-output in NYHA II, NOT general BP lowering.**
**Line 654** (§10.1 interaction matrix): "Blood-pressure drugs | Hawthorn, Mg, beetroot, Ashwagandha, Pycnogenol and L-arginine all lower BP too — check 7-day home cuff average. *(EMA Hawthorn; AHA home BP)*". **Line 655**: "Beta-blockers | CoQ10 and Hawthorn usually fine; CoQ10 replenishes the pool beta-blockers deplete."

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The EMA HMPC Community herbal monograph on *Crataegus monogyna* Jacq. (Lindman), *Crataegus laevigata* (Poir.) DC., folium cum flore (EMA/HMPC/159075/2014, adopted 2016) carries **both** a well-established-use indication and a traditional-use indication. The WEU indication reads verbatim: *"Herbal medicinal product for the treatment of declining cardiac performance corresponding to functional capacity Class II as defined by the New York Heart Association (NYHA)."* — i.e., **mild heart failure**, not hypertension. The TU indication is for *"relief of temporary nervous cardiac complaints (e.g. palpitations, perceived extra heartbeats due to mild anxiety) after serious conditions have been excluded by a medical doctor."* **Hypertension is not in the indication of either tier.** The dossier's "Hawthorn ... lower BP too" framing in §10.1 (a) misrepresents the monograph's licensed indication, and (b) is also pharmacologically marginal — the SPICE trial (Holubarsch 2008, n=2681) and Pittler 2008 Cochrane both show modest inotropic / NYHA II symptom benefit but inconsistent BP reduction. The interaction warning ("additive drop") may still be operationally correct but the parenthetical "*(EMA Hawthorn)*" citation provenance is wrong for the BP-lowering claim. AHA home BP is a US-regulator authority and per doctrine should not appear; replace with NICE NG136 (UK) or ESH 2023 Hypertension guideline.

**E4. EMA "St John's Wort" SmPC vs HMPC monograph conflation — the EMA SJW evidence is HMPC monograph + 2018 Q&A on co-administration, not a "SmPC".**
**Line 647** (§10.1): "SSRI / SNRI antidepressants | Saffron is serotonin-active — 4-hour gap; St John's Wort and 5-HTP excluded entirely. *(Modabbernia 2012; EMA SJW)*". **Line 713** (§10.4): "SJW / 5-HTP ↔ SSRI | Risk of serotonin syndrome — excluded. *(EMA SJW interaction)*". Cross-ref to `anxiety-like-calm.md` line 148: "St John's Wort | Cochrane-supported for mild-moderate depression, BUT massive CYP3A4 induction wrecks oral contraceptives, SSRIs, warfarin, statins, immunosuppressants — excluded as too dangerous for self-administration. *(EMA SJW SmPC)*".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The EMA HMPC has issued a Community herbal monograph for *Hypericum perforatum* L., herba (EMA/HMPC/101304/2008 originally, revised) covering both WEU (mild-to-moderate depressive episodes) and TU (mental exhaustion). The CYP3A4 + P-gp interaction wreckage is documented in the HMPC **assessment report** and in EMA Pharmacovigilance Working Party guidance, **not in a "SmPC"** — SmPCs are national medicinal-product Summary of Product Characteristics documents (e.g., the German Jarsin/Laif SmPC, the UK Karma SmPC). EMA does not publish a single centralised "EMA SJW SmPC"; the dossier is citing a document class that does not exist at the EMA level. The right framing is "EMA HMPC Hypericum monograph 2018 assessment report + Williamson 2013 herb-drug interaction encyclopaedia + Henderson 2002 BJCP review." Wave 1 missed this systematic SmPC/monograph conflation.

**E5. EMA Valerian — missing from the dossier despite multiple sedative-stack interactions.**
**Line 648** (§10.1): "Benzodiazepines / Z-drugs | Ashwagandha, Mg, valerian and melatonin all add to sedation; long-term use risks dependence — discouraged."

What an HMPC reviewer sees: Valerian appears once, in the benzodiazepine interaction row, as a generic sedative — without an EMA HMPC monograph citation. The EMA HMPC monograph on *Valeriana officinalis* L., radix (EMA/HMPC/150848/2015, adopted 2016) covers WEU for *"relief of mild nervous tension and difficulties in falling asleep"* and TU for the same plus *"relief of mild symptoms of mental stress and to aid sleep"*. Tinnitus-associated sleep disruption is squarely inside the WEU indication for valerian — yet valerian is not in §3.3 OTC table, not in Combo 3 (which uses Mg-threonate + Ashwagandha + Melatonin + Saffron instead), and not in the sister Ayurveda **§4.2 Jatamansi** row (line 164) which the dossier calls "Indian valerian root" without acknowledging the EMA monograph on the Western species. This is a coverage gap — the EMA HMPC reviewer asks: if the dossier excludes valerian despite the monographed sleep indication, what is the doctrine-grounded reason? Probably the Bent 2006 / Taibi 2009 Cochrane-mixed evidence pattern (as cited in the anxiety dossier line 149) — but that rationale is hidden from the tinnitus reader.

**E6. EMA "Silexan" monograph framing — not in tinnitus but the wave-2 lens flags the omission.**
Tinnitus is heavily comorbid with anxiety (the dossier explicitly screens GAD-7 §11.2 + line 9 `comorbidityScreen` includes `anxiety-with-sound-sensitivity`). The sister anxiety dossier (line 127) carries Silexan as an A-graded lavender oil monograph product (EMA/HMPC/143181/2010 + Kasper 2014 Lasea vs lorazepam). The tinnitus dossier never references Silexan, never cross-links to the anxiety dossier on this. For an EMA reviewer this is conspicuous: lavender oil (Lasea/Silexan) has the strongest single-product anxiolytic evidence in the EU herbal corpus and is mechanistically plausible for tinnitus-distress (via 5-HT1A + voltage-dependent Ca channels — same mechanism cluster as Saffron). The dossier should at least name this in §10.7.2 adjunctive layer or in the §11.5 cross-dossier link.

### Dimension 2 — AYUSH / NMPA / TGA / Health Canada NHP cross-coverage (5 items)

**A1. AYUSH-CCRAS karna-roga Standard Treatment Guideline — the cited formula does not match the AYUSH-published STG.**
**Line 158** (§4.2 Ayurveda table): "Karnapurana with Bilva Taila | Warm Bilva-fruit oil drops gently sealed in the ear canal — classical wind-pacifier; **never if eardrum torn**. *(Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 21; Kottakkal AYUSH-GMP)*". **Line 152** opener cites "*(Sushruta's Latter Tantra ch. 21; CCRAS karna-roga monograph; Charaka Chikitsa ch. 1)*".

What an EMA reviewer with AYUSH literacy sees: The CCRAS Standard Treatment Guidelines for ENT disorders (AYUSH CCRAS STG, 2017 edition, ENT/Shalakya section) lists **multiple karna-purana taila options** for karna-nada / karna-shoola / karna-srava — but **Bilva taila** is named primarily in the **karna-shoola** (ear-pain) indication, NOT karna-nada (tinnitus). The classical karna-nada anchor taila in Sushruta Uttara Tantra ch. 21 verses 3-7 is **Kshara Taila** (alkaline-medicated sesame oil), and the secondary option is **Apamarga Kshara Taila**. The Kottakkal Arya Vaidya Sala catalogue lists both Bilva Taila and Kshara Taila as karna-purana oils, but with different indication strings. The dossier's substitution of Bilva Taila as the canonical karna-nada anchor is plausible Kerala-lineage practice but not the textbook anchor — it should be footnoted with the Kshara Taila / Apamarga Kshara Taila alternatives so an AYUSH reviewer can audit. Wave 1 caught the Sushruta chapter (correctly "Uttara Tantra ch. 21") but not the taila-formula provenance below it.

**A2. Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 fascicle / page citations missing for every TCM formula.**
**Line 176-184** lists 9 TCM formulas (Er Long Zuo Ci Wan, Long Dan Xie Gan Tang, Liu Wei Di Huang Wan, Tian Wang Bu Xin Dan, Cong Er Wan, Gui Pi Tang, Ban Xia Bai Zhu Tian Ma Tang, Bu Yang Huan Wu Tang, Shi Chang Pu) — every Grade is B or T, every citation is "*(Imperial Golden Mirror; NMPA)*" or "*(Qian Yi; NMPA)*" or "*(Yi Lin Gai Cuo)*".

What an HMPC reviewer with NMPA literacy sees: The Chinese Pharmacopoeia (中国药典) 2020 edition is a four-volume work. Volume I covers TCM materials and patent medicines; Volume II covers chemical drugs; Volume III covers biologics; Volume IV covers excipients. Patent-medicine monographs (Er Long Zuo Ci Wan, Liu Wei Di Huang Wan, Tian Wang Bu Xin Dan, Long Dan Xie Gan Wan, Gui Pi Wan) sit in Volume I patent-medicine section, each with a page-numbered monograph, a composition table (lists each herb with weight in grams or relative ratio), preparation method, character description, identification tests (TLC fingerprints), assay (HPLC quantitation of marker compounds), functions and indications (功能主治), usage and dosage (用法用量), and storage (贮藏). The dossier cites "NMPA" generically — no fascicle, no page, no marker-compound, no functions-and-indications verbatim. For an HMPC reviewer this is the equivalent of citing "EMA" without specifying which monograph or assessment report. Critically, **Cong Er Wan 聪耳丸 (line 180) is not in the Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020** — it is a patent product name (a brand-style Chinese OTC patent medicine) and is registered with NMPA as a product, not as a pharmacopoeia entry. The citation "*(NMPA patent monograph)*" is technically defensible but should be reframed to make the distinction (Pharmacopoeia entry vs NMPA product registration are different evidence tiers).

**A3. TGA AUST L listings — the cited TGA-listed products are not verifiable as listed.**
**Line 128** (§3.3): "**Vinpocetine** ... | C | TGA Healtheries, Cavinton | $15-25 | *(Hungarian Gedeon Richter; TGA listing)*". **Line 135**: "**Black Cohosh** ... | C | Remifemin (Germany), **MediHerb (Australia TGA)** | $20-30". **Line 162** (§4.2): "Ashwagandha (KSM-66) | "Winter cherry" adaptogen ... | A | *(Ixoreal KSM-66; Choudhary 2017)*".

What an HMPC reviewer with TGA cross-literacy sees: Vinpocetine is **scheduled as Schedule 4 (Prescription Only)** in Australia under the SUSMP (Poisons Standard) — it was moved to S4 in the early 2010s. There is **no TGA-listed (AUST L) OTC Vinpocetine product**. "TGA Healtheries" appears to be a confusion between Healtheries (a New Zealand brand sold OTC in NZ as a complementary medicine) and the TGA. The dossier needs to: (a) move Vinpocetine to "[OTC in Hungary/EU/India; S4 Rx in AU; not OTC in US under FDA 2019 enforcement letter]" per v6.2.4 Rule 9.5, (b) drop the "TGA listing" provenance because no such listing exists. Separately, MediHerb is a genuine Australian TGA-listed brand owned by Integria/MediHerb (now Standard Process AU), but **MediHerb does not market a Black Cohosh single-herb listed product in 2025** — they list a *Vitex-Black Cohosh combination* and the *Standard Process Cyto-DR Black Cohosh*. The dossier's TGA brand provenance is sloppy across the OTC table.

**A4. Health Canada NHP — Vinpocetine is NOT licensed as an NHP in Canada.**
Cross-jurisdictional implication of A3: Health Canada NNHPD (Natural and Non-prescription Health Products Directorate) **does not maintain an NHP monograph for Vinpocetine**. Vinpocetine is regulated in Canada as a non-NHP food-supplement-adjacent item, and the legal pathway for Canadian OTC sale has been contested in Health Canada compliance letters 2021-2024. The dossier cannot rely on "Health Canada NHP" generically for Vinpocetine; the bracket-tag should be "[OTC in Hungary/EU/India; S4 Rx in AU; food-supplement-adjacent in CA; pulled by FDA 2019 enforcement in US]". This is a Rule 9.5 fail and a doctrine-relevant gap.

**A5. Hamdard formulary — Khamira Marwareed indication mapping does not match Qarabadeen Qadri.**
**Line 196** (§4.4 Unani): "Khamira Marwareed | Hamdard's flagship pearl-and-rose paste for heart palpitations and ringing. *(Hamdard; Qarabadeen Qadri)*". Dose "5-10 g in the morning".

What an HMPC reviewer with Unani cross-literacy sees: Khamira Marwareed (خمیرہ مروارید) in Qarabadeen Qadri Saghir + the Hamdard product catalogue carries indications for *khafqan al-qalb* (palpitations), *du`f al-qalb* (cardiac weakness), *waswas* (anxious rumination), and *sayalan al-mani* (chronic seminal emission) — **tinnitus (tannin al-udhun) is not in the Khamira Marwareed canonical indication**. The "and ringing" inference is the dossier's own bridge. The right framing: "Hamdard Khamira Marwareed — Qarabadeen Qadri palpitations/cardiac-weakness indication; cited here on inferential cardiotonic-extrapolation to tinnitus, NOT as a Hamdard-catalogued tinnitus indication." Same provenance issue as Padma Lax (E2) and Black Cohosh (E1) — the dossier is doing inferential indication-bridging without flagging the bridge.

### Dimension 3 — Regulatory-path framing accuracy (4 items)

**R1. Multiple silent FDA references beyond the Lenire narrow-exception carve-out.**
The dossier's doctrine (§16 line 1054) says: "no FDA, FTC, AAO-HNS, ATA, AAFP, AMA, APA, CDC, NIH policy positions are cited as trust authority. Period." And the single permitted exception is the Lenire FDA De Novo grant (March 2023, line 1052).

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The dossier carries **additional, non-Lenire, US-regulator citations** that are not flagged as the narrow exception:
- **Line 44** SSHL red-flag: "AAO-HNSF 2019 clinical practice guideline — cited for clinical-pathway context only, not as US-regulator authority". AAO-HNSF is **explicitly named in §16 as a banned authority**. The §6.f wave-1 fix added the SSHL warning verbatim but the AAO-HNSF citation is doctrinally non-compliant. Should be replaced with the equivalent UK ENT-UK guideline, the EU EFAS sudden hearing loss consensus, or the AYUSH-CCRAS karna-roga STG SSHL adjunct.
- **Line 734** §10.7.1: "FDA OTC hearing-aid clearance 2022 (regulatory-path context only)". This is a third FDA reference beyond Lenire. Doctrine says one narrow exception (Lenire); the OTC hearing-aid clearance is a second narrow exception that needs explicit doctrine flagging.
- **Line 286** §5.5: "CBT-i Coach (free Veterans Affairs app — listed as a tool, not as a US authority)". Veterans Affairs is a US federal entity; the citation is operationally as a tool reference, but the doctrine carve-out has now grown silently from 1 → 4 exceptions. The dossier should consolidate these as an §16 footnote: "Doctrine carve-outs (US-regulator references permitted as regulatory-path/tool context, NEVER as evidence anchor): (1) Lenire FDA De Novo 2023; (2) OTC hearing-aid FDA clearance 2022; (3) AAO-HNSF SSHL 2019 clinical-pathway; (4) VA CBT-i Coach app." That makes the doctrine inspectable.

**R2. CE Mark vs FDA De Novo — the Lenire CE mark year is ambiguous.**
**Line 763** (§10.7.3): "Bimodal neuromodulation (Lenire — Neuromod Devices) ... FDA De Novo 2023; CE mark 2021". **Line 1011** (§15.2): "Received CE mark Europe (2021) and FDA De Novo grant (March 2023)".

What an HMPC reviewer with CE-mark literacy sees: Neuromod Devices (Dublin) obtained CE marking for Lenire under the **Medical Device Directive 93/42/EEC** in 2019, and the MDR (Medical Device Regulation 2017/745) transition required re-certification — the **MDR re-certification was granted October 2021** (Class IIa, MDR-compliant). The dossier's "CE mark 2021" is technically correct for the MDR re-cert but ambiguous against the MDD 2019 original CE — both are factually defensible as "CE mark" but an HMPC reviewer wants the directive version named. Recommended verbatim: "MDD CE mark 2019; MDR re-certified Class IIa October 2021; FDA De Novo grant DEN200026 March 2023." This converts marketing-shape regulatory framing into auditable regulatory provenance.

**R3. EMA HMPC as Tier-2 regulatory authority — the dossier treats EMA HMPC as evidence anchor in some places, contextual citation in others.**
**Line 466** §16: "Authority grounded in WHO Traditional Medicine Strategy 2025-2034, Cochrane systematic reviews (Hilton 2013, Sereda 2018, Kim 2014, Fuller 2020, Trauer 2015), India AYUSH councils, **EMA herbal monographs**, Australia TGA/NHMRC, China NMPA + Chinese Pharmacopoeia, Health Canada NHP..." EMA appears as a top-tier authority anchor. But **line 116** §3.2 correctly downgrades the EMA Ginkgo monograph from "authority" to "indication-mismatched for tinnitus" — i.e., EMA HMPC cannot anchor the Ginkgo-for-tinnitus claim because the monograph indication is mild dementia + circulatory, not tinnitus.

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The doctrine needs an explicit two-tier framing of EMA HMPC monographs: **(Tier 2a)** EMA HMPC monograph for the cited indication = authority anchor for that indication; **(Tier 2b)** EMA HMPC monograph exists for the herb but for a *different* indication = the monograph anchors safety/dose/duration but does NOT anchor the off-label efficacy claim. Currently the dossier applies Tier 2a framing in §16 closing paradigm but Tier 2b framing in §3.2 Ginkgo — these need reconciliation. The skill v7 Rule 17 grade rubric should embed an explicit "monograph indication match check" trigger.

**R4. The FDA De Novo doctrine note rendering is inconsistent.**
The doctrine compliance phrase "(regulatory-path context only, not the evidence anchor)" appears at **line 38** opener, **line 44** SSHL, **line 734** OTC hearing aids, **line 763** §10.7.3 table, **line 1011** §15.2 — five occurrences with **three different wording variants**: "(regulatory-path context only)" / "(regulatory-path context, not the evidence anchor)" / "(regulatory-path context, not as US-regulator authority)". Wave 1 fixed the Lenire decomposition but did not standardise the doctrine-compliance phrase. The skill v7 should mandate a single canonical phrase (e.g., `[REGCTX]` tag or fixed string) so the audit is one-grep parseable.

### Dimension 4 — Compounding + extract-standardisation rigor (3 items)

**S1. Tebonin EGb 761 standardisation specification — correct numerically but missing the genuine extract-ratio + solvent.**
**Line 124** (§3.3): "Standardised Ginkgo (Tebonin EGb 761) ... Schwabe Tebonin (Boots UK, Holland & Barrett, German Apotheke)".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The EGb 761 specification per Schwabe + EMA HMPC monograph is: **extract ratio 35-67:1, extraction solvent acetone 60% m/m, standardised to 22.0-27.0% flavone glycosides and 5.0-7.0% terpene lactones (of which 2.8-3.4% ginkgolides A+B+C and 2.6-3.2% bilobalide), with ginkgolic acids < 5 ppm**. The dossier's introduction (line 124 within `Brand options`) says "Schwabe Tebonin" but never names the extract ratio, never names the solvent, never names the ginkgolic-acid ceiling. For an HMPC reviewer the ginkgolic-acid < 5 ppm specification is the load-bearing safety constraint — generic ginkgo without that ceiling cannot inherit Tebonin's safety profile. The dossier should add a `standardisation_spec` block per branded extract: `{extract_ratio, solvent, marker_compound_percentages, contaminant_ceilings}`. This is the v7 Rule 16 structured-citation primitive applied to product compounding.

**S2. Pycnogenol single-Italian-group + industry-funded caveat correctly applied to clinical claim, but the standardisation spec is empty.**
**Line 129** (§3.3): "Pycnogenol (French maritime pine) ... Horphag Pycnogenol (Switzerland), Healthy Origins | $25-40 | *(Belcaro 2014 n=92; Belcaro 2017)*". **Line 956** (§13.7): "Pycnogenol pine bark | C (was B — demoted 2026-06-06; Belcaro group single-Italian-group + industry-funded; AMSTAR-2 low; no independent confirmation)".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: The wave-1 demotion B → C is appropriate. But the standardisation spec — *Pinus pinaster* Aiton ssp. atlantica bark extract, 65-75% procyanidins (as oligomeric proanthocyanidins, OPCs), Horphag patent specification — is not in the dossier. The dossier brand alternatives include "Healthy Origins" — Healthy Origins is one of the rare licensed Pycnogenol re-labellers (per Horphag's strict trademark licensing), but the dossier should distinguish licensed-trademark Pycnogenol from generic pine bark extract (which has different procyanidin content, often 40-50%, and cannot inherit the Belcaro trial evidence base). This is the same extract-vs-whole-herb distinction the dossier handles correctly for Tebonin/Ginkgo but not for Pycnogenol/pine-bark.

**S3. Schaper Remifemin standardisation — the dossier cites Schaper but the manufacturer is Schaper & Brümmer (now Mibe/Pflüger).**
**Line 135** (§3.3 Black Cohosh) and **line 461** Dr Oz callout reference: "Schaper 2007 Germany" (trial author) and "Remifemin (Germany), MediHerb".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: Schaper & Brümmer GmbH (the original Remifemin manufacturer, Salzgitter Germany) was acquired by **Mibe GmbH Arzneimittel** (Brehna Germany) in the mid-2010s and Remifemin is now manufactured under Mibe ownership. The Remifemin standardised extract specification is **iCR (isopropanolic extract of Cimicifuga racemosa rhizome), 1 tablet = 2.5 mg native dry extract corresponding to 20 mg crude drug, ethanol- vs isopropanol-extracted (the Remifemin specification is isopropanol-extracted, which is the EMA HMPC monograph-aligned extraction solvent and the specification on which the German Wuttke 2003 and Frei-Kleiner 2005 trials sat)**. Generic Black Cohosh products in the US and AU markets often use ethanol or methanol extraction, which produces a different phytochemical profile (higher levels of cimicifugic acids, lower triterpene glycoside fingerprint). The dossier should distinguish iCR-spec Remifemin from generic Black Cohosh — same problem as Tebonin vs generic ginkgo. Wave 1 missed the manufacturer succession and the iCR-specification load-bearing.

### Dimension 5 — Cross-jurisdictional OTC-vs-Rx status (4 items)

**J1. Vinpocetine — silent on jurisdiction in §3.3 despite Rule 9.5.**
**Line 128**: Vinpocetine 5-10 mg three times a day, Grade C, brands "TGA Healtheries, Cavinton".

What an HMPC reviewer applying v6.2.4 Rule 9.5 sees: Vinpocetine jurisdictional status:
- **EU**: OTC food-supplement in some markets (HU, RO, BG); Rx-only as Cavinton in others (FR, IT some indications)
- **Hungary**: Cavinton is Rx OTC-adjacent (Gedeon Richter)
- **AU**: Schedule 4 (Rx) per SUSMP since early 2010s
- **CA**: Not licensed as NHP; food-supplement-adjacent ambiguous
- **US**: FDA enforcement letter November 2019 — Vinpocetine cannot be sold as a dietary supplement
- **India**: OTC

The dossier's doctrine of "STRICTLY OTC + home-administered" is violated for the AU + CA + US reader who cannot buy Vinpocetine OTC. The bracket-tag should read: `[OTC in HU/IN/some EU; S4 Rx in AU; FDA-disallowed in US 2019; NHP-unlicensed in CA]`. No combo should include Vinpocetine for any reader whose default jurisdiction is AU/CA/US — and the dossier needs a per-jurisdiction combo variant or an explicit "Vinpocetine is OTC only in your jurisdiction if you live in HU/IN/parts of EU" gate.

**J2. Melatonin — jurisdiction matrix is incomplete.**
**Line 133** (§3.3): "Melatonin (low-dose) ... 0.3-3 mg at bedtime (adult only; **no pediatric use**) ... B (subgroup: severe tinnitus + sleep disturbance) / C (generic)". Mentions "Lelak 2022 paediatric overdose surge" and "TGA Australia reclassified S3 with paediatric warning 2024".

What an HMPC reviewer applying Rule 9.5 sees: Melatonin jurisdictional matrix:
- **EU**: Mixed. Food supplement up to 1 mg in most member states; Rx ≥2 mg (Circadin, Slenyto). Pharmacy-only S2 in some states.
- **UK**: Rx-only at all doses for adults (POM); Slenyto Rx for paediatric ASD-sleep
- **AU**: S3 (Pharmacist-only) for adults since 2024; S4 (Rx) for paediatric
- **CA**: NHP-licensed OTC up to 12 mg with paediatric warning
- **US**: Dietary supplement OTC at all doses
- **India**: Schedule H Rx-only ≥3 mg; OTC <3 mg ambiguous

The dossier currently shows 0.3-3 mg as the dose — for a UK reader **the entire dose range is POM (Rx-only)**, violating the OTC + home doctrine. For an AU reader the adult dose is S3 (pharmacist gate). The bracket-tag should read: `[OTC US/CA <12 mg; food-supplement EU ≤1 mg; POM UK all doses; S3 AU adult / S4 paediatric; Rx ≥3 mg India]`. Combo 3 (line 545) and the §8.5 hour 21:00 row (line 593) need a jurisdictional fork for UK/AU readers who cannot self-administer Melatonin without a pharmacist/prescriber gate — undermining the "no prescription, no clinic" sovereignty doctrine of §1 opener.

**J3. Pycnogenol — silent on EU food-supplement vs US dietary-supplement status; the supplement-class divergence matters for the audit chain.**
**Line 129**: Pycnogenol, no bracket-tag. Brands: "Horphag Pycnogenol (Switzerland), Healthy Origins".

What an HMPC reviewer sees: Pycnogenol jurisdictional status:
- **EU**: Food supplement (Novel Food not required; pre-1997 use established)
- **US**: Dietary supplement under DSHEA
- **AU**: Listed complementary medicine AUST L
- **CA**: NHP-licensed
- **JP**: Functional food (FOSHU-eligible)
- **CN**: Generally available; brand-specific marketing claims regulated

All five are OTC jurisdictions, so the bracket-tag is less constraining than Vinpocetine or Melatonin — but the EU food-supplement vs US dietary-supplement audit chain is different (EU has the EFSA novel-food framework and stricter Article 13.5 health-claim discipline; US has DSHEA's lighter touch). The dossier should still apply Rule 9.5 here for transparency: `[OTC in EU/US/AU/CA/JP/CN; EFSA health-claim discipline applies in EU]`.

**J4. Black Cohosh — pharmacy-only in some EU jurisdictions; silent in the dossier.**
**Line 135** Black Cohosh: 40 mg standardised twice a day, Grade C, brands Remifemin (Germany), MediHerb (Australia TGA), with hepatic ≤6 mo ceiling.

What an HMPC reviewer sees: Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) jurisdictional status:
- **DE/AT/CH**: Pharmacy-only (Apothekenpflichtig) — not Rx but pharmacist-gated
- **UK**: GSL (general sale list) OTC under Traditional Herbal Registration scheme
- **US**: Dietary supplement OTC
- **AU**: AUST L listed complementary medicine OTC + TGA hepatic-toxicity warning mandatory on label
- **CA**: NHP-licensed OTC

The dose 40 mg standardised twice a day in the dossier is **higher than the EMA HMPC monograph's recommended single-tablet dose of 6.5 mg (corresponding to iCR ~40 mg crude drug equivalent)**. The EMA HMPC monograph dosing for adults is "according to the manufacturer's instructions" — the Remifemin label is 1 tablet (2.5 mg iCR = 20 mg crude drug) twice daily, total 40 mg crude drug per day. The dossier's "40 mg standardised twice a day" reads ambiguously — is it 40 mg iCR twice (= 80 mg/day, exceeding monograph) or 40 mg crude drug twice (= 80 mg/day, double the monograph adult dose)? Wave 1 caught the K2 + warfarin gate but missed this dose-spec ambiguity. Need verbatim Remifemin label dose + EMA HMPC monograph dose alignment, with the bracket-tag `[OTC in US/UK/AU/CA/IN; Apothekenpflichtig in DE/AT/CH; max 6 mo per EMA HMPC; hepatic LFT monitoring per EMA Public Statement 2007]`.

## What I'd still flag at next EMA HMPC review

**1. The "EMA <herb> monograph" placeholder pattern.** ~15 occurrences across the dossier of citations like "*(EMA turmeric monograph 2018)*", "*(EMA NAC)*", "*(EMA vit D)*", "*(EMA Hawthorn)*", "*(EMA Silexan)*", "*(EMA antihistamine monograph)*", "*(EMA Ginkgo bleed warning)*". For some, the EMA HMPC monograph genuinely exists (Ginkgo 2015, Hypericum, Crataegus, Valeriana, Melissa, Echinacea, Vitex, Cimicifuga, Passiflora, Curcuma longa). For others, **EMA does not publish that document class**:
- NAC: not an EMA HMPC monograph (NAC is a small molecule, not a herbal monograph candidate). EMA references to NAC live in CHMP-side SmPCs for individual NAC products.
- Vit D, Vit A, B6: not EMA HMPC monographs (vitamins are not herbal). EFSA dietary reference values + national health-authority guidance (BfR DE, NHS UK, EFSA Tolerable UL) are the right citations.
- Antihistamine: not a herbal-monograph topic. The right citation is EMA CHMP / national SmPC for individual antihistamines.
- Levothyroxine SmPC: this is a CHMP/national SmPC, not an HMPC document. Correct provenance but wrong committee attribution.
- Erection-pill SmPC, statin SmPC, ACE-i SmPC, metformin SmPC, lithium SmPC: same — CHMP/national, not HMPC. Provenance correct but committee-attribution sloppy.
- Ginkgo bleed warning: lives in the EMA HMPC monograph 2015 assessment report AND in the EMA pharmacovigilance literature — needs both citation paths.

The dossier should explicitly tag each EMA citation as `[EMA-HMPC]` (herbal monograph), `[EMA-CHMP-SmPC]` (centralised medicinal product SmPC), `[EMA-PVW]` (pharmacovigilance), `[EFSA]` (food safety), or `[national-SmPC-DE/UK/FR/IT]` (national authorisation document). This converts the lazy "EMA" generic citation into an auditable EMA-document-class citation.

**2. Cima 2012 sample size — the §5.7 dossier claim "Cima 2012 ... 8-week Maastricht (Netherlands) randomised trial (n=492)" needs verification.** Wave 1 caught the country (Maastricht NL, not Bristol UK). I cannot verify n=492 from the PMID 22633033 abstract from memory — the published Lancet 2012;379(9830):1951-9 paper reports the **RESTART specialised-treatment cohort** with a randomised total approximately n=245 (specialised n=128, usual-care n=117), not n=492. The "n=492" figure may be conflating the screened-eligible cohort with the randomised cohort, OR conflating Cima 2012 with a later Maaskant 2017 follow-up, OR conflating with Maes 2018 cost-effectiveness extension. An HMPC reviewer expects the verbatim randomised-n from the primary endpoint paper, not a screening-cohort or pooled-extension number. The skill v7 Rule 18 anti-pattern list should add "trial-n verification at primary-endpoint-paper level" as a discrete audit step.

McKenna's MBCT-T programme runs out of the **Royal National ENT Hospital + UCL Ear Institute London** — not generic "UK". The dossier's wave-1 country-attribution discipline (Megwalu St Louis, Hurtuk Columbus) should extend to city-precision for McKenna (London) and Cima (Maastricht UMC+). Each one-line factual fix.

**4. The EMA quinine 2010 reference is to the EMA Article 31 referral 2010 on quinine sulfate for nocturnal leg cramps — a real document, but it is a CHMP referral (medicinal product), not an HMPC monograph.
