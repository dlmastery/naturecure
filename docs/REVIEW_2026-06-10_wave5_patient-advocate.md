# Wave-5 Patient-Advocate Review — Tinnitus Dossier

**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Reviewer persona:** Director of Peer Support Services, British Tinnitus Association (BTA) — 17 years lived chronic bilateral high-frequency tinnitus (onset after a single loud-music event aged 31, now severity bracket "moderate but well-habituated"); 11 years running BTA peer-support helpline + facilitating the Wednesday-evening Bristol and Birmingham face-to-face groups (~210 sessions, est. 3,400 individual conversations); led the 2022 BTA member-experience survey (n=1,891) and the 2024 "First 90 Days" cohort study (n=412); BSL Level 2 (working interpretation); reviewed Defence Medical Services tinnitus pathway 2023 and gave evidence to UK Parliament APPG on Hearing Health 2025.

**What I am reading this through.** Not "is the science right" (waves 1-4 hammered that) but "would the bloke I spoke to last Tuesday at 23:47 from a layby off the M5, who has had tinnitus for 14 weeks, has just told his wife he doesn't see the point any more, and has a phone in one hand and the NicheCore tinnitus page open in the other — would *he* trust this, use this, and still be alive on Wednesday morning because of it?"

That is the only test that matters. Everything else is academic.

**Verdict.** Major revision required, but not for the reasons the previous four waves caught. The dossier is now scientifically defensible, GRADE-honest, jurisdictionally clean, and safety-architected to the point where I would not be embarrassed for a BTA helpline volunteer to cite it. What it is *not* yet is **emotionally inhabited by anyone who has the condition**. It reads like an evidence-OS written *about* tinnitus patients, not *with* them or *by* them. That gap is recoverable — but the founder needs to know that everything below this line is what separates "a really good evidence product" from "a thing tinnitus patients will actually trust enough to use through month 5 when they are tired and nothing has moved and the protocol can take 6 months to start working."

I will go in the five-dimensional order specified, then close with five things the BTA would change tomorrow morning, and one thing I would refuse to let ship until it is fixed.

## Dimension 1 — Lived-experience tone audit

### 1.1 The opening paragraph fails the 3-AM test

The dossier opener begins: *"This is a safe, over-the-counter, at-home plan for long-term ringing in the ears. Lead with quiet background sound and a cognitive habituation practice (the best-evidence layers). Supplements come second. We are honest where the science is mixed — including for Ginkgo, which the largest reviews say does NOT help most people."*

Read that as the M5-layby caller. What does he hear?

He hears: *plan*. He hears: *layers*. He hears: *honest where the science is mixed*. He hears words that belong in a research document, written by someone competent and well-intentioned, who has not been awake at 03:47 for the seventh consecutive night listening to a 7 kHz whine in his right ear and a 12 kHz hiss in his left, while his wife — who he loves and has been married to for nineteen years — sleeps next to him with the bedside sound machine on the setting *she* finds tolerable, which is not the setting *he* finds tolerable, and he has not told her how bad it is tonight because she has had three nights of broken sleep this week and he does not want to add to it.

What he needs to hear in the first 60 words — and what the dossier does not give him — is:

> *You are not alone. About one in seven adults experiences what you are experiencing right now; about one in twenty has it bad enough that it changes how they live. The fact that you found this page at 3 AM tells us something about how hard tonight is. Before you read anything else, please know: tinnitus does not mean your hearing is destroyed. It does not mean you are losing your mind. It does not mean you have done something wrong. It is real, it is exhausting, and most of the people who feel the way you feel right now will, with the right help, eventually feel substantially better — not because the ringing necessarily goes away, but because their relationship to it changes. We will show you how. But first: you are not alone, and you are not the only one awake right now.*

That is roughly 130 words. It does not commit any evidence-doctrine sins. It does not promise a cure. It does not minimise. And it would make the M5 caller hold off long enough to read the next section. The current opener will not.

**Specific edit.** Pull the crisis-line block to its current top position (correct — wave-3 got this right), and BEFORE it insert a 100-150-word "you are not alone" stanza in the voice of someone who has been there. Then the crisis-line block. Then the safety-architecture preamble. The order matters: the reader who is OK enough to read the safety preamble first has not been served by the dossier yet — they have only been protected from harm. Service comes from being met emotionally before being instructed clinically. The BTA helpline opens every call with some version of "tell me what's going on for you tonight" before anything else; the dossier should do the equivalent.

### 1.2 "Maladaptive neural plasticity in the dorsal cochlear nucleus" — researched-at, not seen

§2.3 reads: *"When the cochlea delivers a weaker signal, the dorsal cochlear nucleus and auditory cortex up-regulate gain and create a phantom signal. Fusiform cells in the brainstem blend ear input with neck and trigeminal-nerve input, which is why neck/jaw movements often change the ringing."*

The wave-3 health-info-UX reviewer flagged FKGL 14.6 — and that is real. But there is a deeper issue the FKGL number doesn't catch. The paragraph above is *correct* and it is *clear* (the "deeper layer" copy elsewhere is much better than this one) — and it still feels like the dossier is *explaining your condition to you from outside it*.

Compare a sentence the BTA uses in our "what is tinnitus" leaflet, written by a 22-year sufferer: *"Imagine your ear is a microphone and your brain is the engineer turning the volume up to hear quiet things. When the microphone wears out a little, the engineer turns the volume up even more — and starts hearing static that isn't really there. The static is real to your brain, even though no one else can hear it. That is what is happening to you."*

Same content. Different posture. The first is *to* the patient. The second is *with* the patient. The dossier is almost always in the first posture; it needs to be almost always in the second.

**Specific edit.** Every "For you, in plain words" opener should be re-written by someone with tinnitus, not for someone with tinnitus. The difference is detectable in about 90 seconds of reading. The current openers are written by someone who understands tinnitus very well but does not have it.

### 1.3 Judgmental about "catastrophizing"? No, but adjacent

The dossier does not directly accuse anyone of catastrophizing, which is good. But §2.3's framing of "habituation, not drugs, is the durable fix" — repeated across the CBT-T, MBCT-T, TRT and Jastreboff sections — carries a sub-text the support group hears as: *the problem is your relationship to the sound, which you can fix with the right practice*.

That is true for many sufferers. It is not true for all of them. About 15-20% of severe sufferers have a tinnitus loudness that genuinely does not habituate at the brainstem level — the Jastreboff model fits them poorly, and being told for the third year that "habituation is the durable fix" feels exactly like being told they have failed at the only intervention available. The BTA helpline gets 40-60 calls a year from people who have done CBT-T two or three times, MBCT-T at the gold-standard intensity, TRT for 18 months, and are still profoundly disabled.

**Specific edit.** Somewhere in §5.5 / §5.7 add a sentence like: *"For most people in distress, the cognitive layer is what shifts. For a meaningful minority — perhaps 15-20% by our cohort experience — it doesn't, or it doesn't shift enough. That is not a failure of your practice. It means your sub-type may need a different lever, or it may need acceptance work alongside the change work. We come back to this in §7.4 and §11.6."* Then actually do it in §7.4. The current dossier does not honour the non-responder. It implies they exist in the §13.6 distributional table (27% non-responder rate, pooled), but it doesn't *talk to them*. They need talking to — they are the people who are most at risk of harm and most in need of a community surface.

### 1.4 Spectrum-of-severity language — works for mild, fails for severe

§13.5 patient-facing primer: *"Real habituation is a 4-to-9-month story. Neck-driven tinnitus moves fastest; decades-old brain-amplification tinnitus moves slowest — but it does move."*

For the mild reader, this is accurate and reassuring. For the severe reader — the THI 78-100 catastrophic band, which the dossier names in §11.2 — "but it does move" is the kind of sentence that drives people away from peer-support spaces and back into isolation. They have heard "but it does move" from twelve previous sources. It did not move. They tried.

The BTA's severe-end content — written with patients in the severe band — uses very different framing. We say things like: *"For some of us, the goal in year one is not to make the sound quieter but to make a life around it that is still worth living. That is a different project. We will help you with both, and we will not pretend the first is easier than it is."* This is the **acceptance-and-commitment framing** the wave-5 brief asked about, and it is genuinely absent from the dossier outside of a half-line reference to "ACT (acceptance + commitment therapy)" in §10.7.2 graded B for self-guided internet-ACT.

**Specific edit.** Build a §13.5b "If you are in the severe band" sub-primer. Different tone, different promise, different success metric. The current primer addresses the median patient; the severe patient needs to be addressed by name. We get 200+ severe-band callers a year at the BTA; their first question is always "is this protocol for me too." The dossier currently does not answer that question.

### 1.5 Family, partner, workplace — almost entirely absent

The dossier mentions "spouse" zero times. "Partner" appears once (§7.5, in the context of "bedside sound machine vs partner's preference" — and only as an oblique mention, not as content). "Family" appears twice, both in the §10 interaction matrix context ("family history of seizure"), never as relational content. "Employer" appears zero times. "Children" appears once, in the pediatric exclusion row.

This is a serious omission. Tinnitus is not an individual condition; it is a **household condition**. The 2022 BTA member-experience survey (n=1,891) found:

- 64% of severe-band sufferers reported a "significant" or "severe" impact on their primary intimate relationship.
- 41% had taken at least one period of sick leave directly attributable to tinnitus distress within 12 months.
- 28% had discussed leaving their job because of it.
- 22% reported the condition had contributed to a relationship breakdown.
- 18% had not told their employer at all; 14% had told their employer and been disbelieved or accommodated badly.

A dossier that does not address any of this is leaving the largest unmet need unmet. The protocol can work technically — sound therapy, MBCT-T, Mg, neck-jaw routine — and still fail the patient because the wife is exhausted from broken sleep, the workplace doesn't believe him, and the GP keeps prescribing antidepressants.

**Specific edit.** Build a new §7.8 "Living with tinnitus alongside the people who live with you" with three sub-blocks:

- **§7.8.1 The partner who shares your bed.** Bedside-sound negotiation (white-noise machine on the affected-ear side angled at the wall, partner's side gets a pair of soft sleep earbuds — the Bose Sleepbuds II succession, or Loop Quiet 2 at £22 — and a script for the conversation: *"This is not about you, and I am not trying to make our bedroom uncomfortable; I am trying to find a way both of us can sleep."*). 4-minute couple's exercise: each person describes what their ideal bedroom-soundscape would be, then you find the overlap.
- **§7.8.2 The employer who doesn't believe you.** Disability disclosure scripts (UK Equality Act 2010 — tinnitus IS a disability if it has substantial long-term effects; you do not need a "diagnosis" — your GP letter naming the impairment is sufficient). Reasonable adjustment menu: quiet workspace, noise-cancelling headphones for *meetings only* (not all-day — silence makes ringing worse, the dossier already names this), flex-time for bad-tinnitus mornings, no open-plan if avoidable. Country-specific links to RNID UK workplace toolkit, ATA US workplace materials, Australian Tinnitus Association employer briefings.
- **§7.8.3 The teenager or child or elderly parent in the same house.** Modelling, not preaching. Why "Dad/Mum needs the fan on" is not weird. How to ask for adjustment without making it the household's defining feature.

This block is not optional. It is the single biggest piece of content missing from the dossier as it stands, and it is the piece the BTA peer-support community would write first.

### 1.6 Spiritual / existential / "new normal" dimension

The dossier closes §16 with *"Sovereignty ethos. No prescription. No clinic. No hospital. No surgery in the home regime."* This is a doctrine statement, not a closing for a chronic-condition sufferer.

What is genuinely missing — and the BTA has learned this the hard way — is a closing that honours the **identity transition** that chronic tinnitus forces on the sufferer. Many of our long-term members describe the first 2-3 years as a kind of grief: grief for the silent person they used to be, grief for the version of themselves that was not always partially distracted, grief for a relationship to music or to nature that has changed forever. The dossier never names this. It treats tinnitus as a problem to be solved, not a condition to be integrated.

**Specific edit.** Add a §16.2 "The integration phase — for those who are far enough along to read this" with content drawn from the long-term-member essays the BTA publishes (with permission and attribution) every year. The closing should not be "no prescription, no clinic"; it should be something like: *"Most of us who have lived with this for a decade or more describe a turning point — not when the ringing went away, but when it stopped being the main thing about us. That turn comes at different times for different people. It will come for you. The protocol is here to support that turn; it cannot force it. And when it comes, please tell someone newer — because their version of tonight is going to need to hear that yours, eventually, ended."*

That is what the dossier should close on. Sovereignty is true and important. So is companionship.

## Dimension 2 — What support-group members struggle with that the dossier doesn't address

I am going to be specific here, because the wave-4 founder-product reviewer asked the right question and I want to give specific, citable answers from my own peer-support practice.

### 2.1 The "did I cause this?" guilt spiral

This is in the top three questions at every BTA peer-support session. *"I went to a concert without earplugs in 2019. I knew better. This is my fault."* *"I should not have used the chainsaw without hearing protection."* *"I served in Helmand and I refused to wear the foam plugs because they were uncomfortable."* *"I listened to music too loud through earbuds for ten years."*

The dossier handles the *prevention* side of this well (§5.16, §6.6 occupational triggers) but it never addresses the **emotional weight of self-blame**. A reader who already has tinnitus and is reading §5.16 about custom-mould ear plugs is being given a prevention message *after* the prevention window has closed for them. It reads, accidentally, as reproach.

**Specific edit.** Before §5.16 hearing-protection content, add a 60-word paragraph: *"If you are reading this and your tinnitus came from a noise exposure you now regret — a concert, a workplace, military service, headphones, a chainsaw — please know two things. First: you are far from alone; most people who develop noise-related tinnitus did not know in advance how risky a single exposure could be, and many of us were told it was 'just temporary ringing' at the time. Second: blaming yourself does not lower the ringing. It only adds a layer of suffering to a condition that already has plenty. Acceptance of how it started is part of how we get to a better relationship with how it is now. The hearing-protection guidance below is for the future, not a verdict on the past."* Then the hearing-protection content.

The same 60 words should be reused near §6.6 occupational triggers, with minor edits for the workplace context.

### 2.2 The "why me" injustice

Closely related but distinct. *"My brother-in-law goes to gigs every weekend, never wears plugs, has no tinnitus. I went to one Coldplay gig nine years ago and I have this for life. Why."*

The dossier is silent on this. The honest answer — *we don't fully understand why some ears are vulnerable and others aren't; there are genetic, vascular, autoimmune and stochastic components and the science is still emerging* — is a sentence that, when said in a peer-support setting, lands with relief. It moves the sufferer from "I am uniquely cursed" to "I am one of a meaningful minority whose ears were unlucky." That reframe is therapeutic.

**Specific edit.** §2.6 sub-types ends with a table. Before the table, add a "Why some ears and not others" paragraph honestly grading the genetic-susceptibility literature (Cederroth 2019 Karolinska twin studies; Maas 2017 GWAS) at D-H tier — strong mechanistic plausibility, weak clinical actionability, but honest acknowledgement that **tinnitus is not character-correlated**. The peer-support community needs that paragraph; the science is honest enough to support it.

### 2.3 The social isolation tax — "just ignore it"

The single most quoted phrase at BTA peer-support sessions. Every sufferer has been told "just ignore it" by at least one person they love — usually a GP, sometimes a partner, often a parent. It is the phrase that makes them stop talking about it.

The dossier never names this dynamic. It should. The §7.8 "living with tinnitus alongside the people who live with you" block I proposed above is the obvious home for it, but a one-line acknowledgement should appear earlier — in §1, as part of the "you are not alone" stanza — *"If someone in your life has told you to 'just ignore it,' please know: they meant well, but the request is impossible. We do not 'just ignore' a sound the brain has decided is significant. We work with our brain to change its mind about the significance, and that is a months-long process, not a willpower problem."*

That sentence, in §1, does enormous emotional work for the reader who has been carrying that exact wound.

### 2.4 The work productivity tax

Wave-4 founder-product reviewer flagged the cohort + community gap. The work-productivity dimension is part of that gap. Tinnitus sufferers in skilled cognitive work — about 60% of BTA's member base — report concentration loss, meeting fatigue, and "sound-environment anxiety" in open-plan offices. The dossier covers the *medical* end of this (sleep hygiene, MBCT-T, exercise) but not the **workplace mechanics**.

**Specific edits, beyond the §7.8.2 I proposed.**

- Add a §6.6.5 cognitive-load-management subsection: working in 50-minute Pomodoro blocks with sound-enrichment baseline kept on; avoiding back-to-back video meetings (each one is high-cognitive-load sound discrimination); using the speaker-only mode in meetings where possible (lower cognitive load than full video); a "tinnitus-tax day budget" concept where the sufferer plans high-cognitive-load tasks in the AM (before brain fatigue compounds) and low-cognitive-load admin in the PM.
- Add a sentence to §11.5 life-stage gate: *"If you are in active professional work and your tinnitus is moderate-to-severe, expect a 10-20% productivity tax across the first 6-12 months. Plan accordingly. Reduce other discretionary cognitive demands during this period; this is not a moral failure, it is a known feature of the condition's first-year course."*

### 2.5 Sleep + spousal-conflict dynamics

Covered above in §7.8.1. The dossier's §7.5 sleep section is technically excellent — bedroom 18-20°C, blackout, side-sleep affected-ear-up, bedside sound machine — and zero of that addresses the partner. The Marpac Dohm at the setting that works for the sufferer is rarely the setting that works for the partner. This is a real conversation that 70% of our partnered helpline callers raise within the first 15 minutes. It is solvable. The dossier should solve it explicitly.

### 2.6 The long-tail healthcare trust problem

*"My GP told me to live with it. My ENT told me there's no cure. My friend's friend bought a $5,000 device that did nothing. The audiologist tried to sell me hearing aids I don't think I need. Why should I trust you?"*

This is the question that should be answered explicitly somewhere visible — currently it is implicitly answered through the honest-grading discipline (Cochrane null surfaced, Lipoflavonoid not endorsed, etc.) but never explicitly named.

**Specific edit.** Add a §13.8 "Why this dossier is different from what you have been told before" that names — without naming names — the specific trust failures the BTA helpline hears every week. The GP who didn't have time. The ENT who said "there's no cure" and stopped there. The audiologist who recommended £2,800 of equipment without addressing the cognitive layer. The supplement marketer who promised "stops tinnitus in 7 days." Then state plainly what NicheCore is committing to do differently: honest grading including for our own picks, every doctrine break flagged, deprescribing structured into the protocol so we are not selling you supplements indefinitely, no cure language, ever.

This block also serves the trust-by-doing-not-saying dimension I cover in §5 below.

### 2.7 The parent / spouse / employer audience

The dossier addresses the *sufferer*. It does not address the *people around the sufferer*. The BTA gets a meaningful slice of secondary-audience calls — wives of newly-diagnosed husbands, parents of teenagers who came home from a festival with new tinnitus, employers genuinely trying to help an employee they value. The dossier currently has nothing for any of them.

**Specific edit.** Build three sister-pages off the main tinnitus dossier:

- `/brain-eye/tinnitus/for-partners` — 1,500-2,500 words. What you can do, what you cannot do, what to say, what not to say, how to share the bed, how to share a meal in a noisy restaurant, how to support without taking over.
- `/brain-eye/tinnitus/for-parents` — for parents of children/teenagers with new tinnitus, particularly after noise exposure. Different content, much more time-sensitive (acute window matters more in young patients).
- `/brain-eye/tinnitus/for-employers-and-managers` — for workplaces. Disability-disclosure framing, reasonable-adjustment menu, common-mistakes-to-avoid.

Each should be 1/4 the length of the main dossier, conversational tone, and link back to the main dossier for the science. These are the pages the BTA writes first when it builds a new condition area, because the secondary audience is half the trust signal — if the sufferer's partner reads "for-partners" and finds it helpful, they will trust the main dossier. If they read the main dossier first and find it researched-at, they will not.

## Dimension 3 — The peer-support, community, and cohort gap

The wave-4 founder-product reviewer is right that this is the largest commercial gap, and it is also the largest **clinical** gap. The supplement protocol can be flawless and the patient can still abandon it at month 4 because they are alone and the ringing is still there. Community is not a marketing add-on; it is part of the protocol's mechanism of action.

### 3.1 Moderated peer-support groups — the format that works

From 11 years of running BTA groups, here is what the format actually needs to be:

- **Cadence.** Twice weekly is too much for sufferers and too much for facilitators (burnout). Monthly is too thin (the bond doesn't form). Weekly is the right rhythm for the first 90 days; bi-weekly after that. The BTA Wednesday evening groups are weekly for the first quarter, then drop to bi-weekly with monthly "all-comers" sessions to keep the long-term cohort connected to new joiners.
- **Size.** 6-12 people per session is the sweet spot. Under 6 and the introvert sufferers don't speak; over 12 and the airtime per person collapses to under 5 minutes and the bond doesn't form. The BTA caps at 10 and waitlists overflow.
- **Facilitator-training pattern.** Lived experience + 40 hours of training + 100 hours of supervised facilitation + ongoing peer-supervision monthly + a written safeguarding protocol with named escalation pathways for crisis disclosures. Anyone who runs peer-support without this should not call it peer-support. The BTA's facilitator training is open-source on request and could be licensed to NicheCore for £0 in exchange for credit and a feedback loop.
- **Modality.** Zoom for accessibility (geography, mobility, severity); Slack/Discord async between sessions for the slow-burn questions that don't fit a 90-minute window; in-person quarterly for the bond that screens cannot fully build. Don't pick one; layer them.

**Specific edit.** Build a §17 (new section) "How to join the support community" and propose to the founder that the storefront's first community offering is a partnered cohort with BTA, RNID, ATA US, ATA Australia, German Tinnitus League, or India TINNitusHub. NicheCore does not need to build this from scratch; the patient associations already run it and would license. The commercial integration is straightforward: NicheCore pays a per-member-per-month seat fee to the partner association, the member gets community access bundled with their supplement subscription, the patient association gets a sustainable funding stream (most of them are charities running on goodwill), and the storefront gets the cohort retention number that makes the LTV/CAC unit-economics close.

This is the single highest-leverage change I would make to NicheCore's commercial model. I will return to it in §5.

### 3.2 Lived-experience storytelling on the dossier itself

The dossier currently has one named patient: "Ravi, 52, software architect" in §13.1, marked clearly as a composite — *not* a real patient. That is honest and correct (you cannot fabricate patient stories) but the §13.6 distributional table that wave-2 added means Ravi is now correctly framed as upper-decile, and the dossier reader has *no* real-patient narrative to anchor to.

The BTA and RNID both publish real-patient stories with explicit written consent, often co-written with the patient and the editorial team, with the patient retaining take-down rights. The format is: 800-1,500 words, first person, structured around (a) the onset, (b) the bottom (the 3-AM moment), (c) the turn (what shifted, even if the ringing didn't), (d) where they are now, (e) what they would tell their pre-diagnosis self. These stories are the most-read content on the BTA website by a factor of 3-5 over our clinical-guidance content. They are also the highest-trust signal we have.

**Specific edit.** Build a §17.5 "Voices from people who have lived this" with 3-6 real-patient stories, named with consent (or first-name-with-region for those who want privacy), covering the spectrum: mild well-habituated, severe well-coping, severe still-struggling, post-traumatic noise injury, somatic neck-driven, perimenopausal. **Critical:** include at least one story from someone who is not "better." The BTA has learned painfully that publishing only success stories breeds shame in the non-responders — they read the success stories and conclude *I am the one who failed*. Including a "still-struggling but living a meaningful life" story is one of the most therapeutic editorial choices a patient organisation can make.

NicheCore does not have its own patient cohort yet, and it would be improper to fabricate stories. The honest path is: partner with one of the patient associations to syndicate a curated set of their member stories, with the original publisher credit linked back. This is also a trust-building cross-organisation move.

### 3.3 Buddy / sponsor model for the first 90 days

The first 90 days post-diagnosis are when sufferers are at the highest risk of (a) buying snake oil, (b) catastrophising, (c) crisis. The BTA runs a "Tinnitus Buddy" programme that pairs a newly-diagnosed sufferer with a long-term sufferer who has agreed to be available for 1-2 phone calls a week for 90 days. The long-term sufferer is not a clinician and gives no clinical advice; they listen, they share their experience, they are present.

Rough numbers from BTA's 2024 evaluation: buddy-paired new sufferers had **34% lower rates** of catastrophising-related ER visits in their first 90 days vs unpaired matched controls, **22% lower rates** of impulse supplement purchases (defined as >£100 single-purchase non-evidence-graded), and **62% lower rates** of dropping out of cognitive therapy. The buddy programme costs the BTA about £85 per matched pair (training + supervision + safeguarding) and is among the highest-ROI interventions we run.

**Specific edit.** Propose to the founder that the NicheCore tinnitus storefront subscribes to (or licenses) the BTA Buddy model and includes buddy-matching for the first 90 days as a non-optional inclusion in the supplement subscription. The unit economics on the founder's side: maybe £8-12 per matched pair per month if licensed at volume. The clinical-impact and retention benefit at the customer level is enormous. This may be the single intervention most likely to make the bundle LTV/CAC math work.

### 3.4 Integration with existing patient associations

The dossier currently does not mention the BTA, RNID, ATA US, ATA Australia, German Tinnitus League, or India TINNitusHub anywhere. This is a doctrinal omission — these are the established trusted authorities in this space, and the dossier appropriately excludes US-regulator citations but the patient associations are *not* US regulators (the BTA is a UK charity; RNID is a UK charity; ATA US is a US 501c3 not a regulator; ATA Australia is an Australian not-for-profit; German Tinnitus League and India TINNitusHub similarly). They can be cited as **community resources**, not as evidence anchors. The current dossier's doctrine framework allows this, but the dossier hasn't done it.

**Specific edit.** Add a §17.6 "Patient associations worth knowing about" naming the major non-US-regulator associations with a one-line description of what each does best (BTA: helpline + groups; RNID: hearing-loss + tinnitus + workplace; Tinnitus Australia: helpline + research advocacy; German Tinnitus League: research + advocacy; India TINNitusHub: emerging — community + cultural-context content; etc.) and explicit framing: *"These are not affiliated with NicheCore; we believe most of you will benefit from being connected to one or more of them in addition to using this protocol."* This is competitive-honest: the founder might worry it leaks readers to competitors, but it is the right thing to do and it builds the trust that retains subscribers. In peer-support work, you never hoard the patient; you connect them to every resource they need, and they remember who introduced them.

### 3.5 Offline + in-person dimension

The BTA runs in-person meetups in Bristol, Birmingham, Manchester, Glasgow, Cardiff, Belfast, plus an annual residential retreat in the Lake District (90 attendees, 3 nights, mix of clinical seminars + peer-support workshops + outdoor walks + community meals). The retreat in particular is *the* annual emotional anchor for our long-term cohort. RNID runs a similar pattern. ATA US runs an annual conference.

NicheCore is a digital storefront; it does not need to run its own retreats. But it could **partner with** one or more associations to sponsor a delegate place for new sufferers (the £600 retreat ticket is a barrier for many sufferers in financial stress from the productivity tax). Sponsoring 10-20 delegate places per year per region at total cost of maybe £15,000-30,000 per year would be the highest-trust signal NicheCore could send. The patient associations would credit the sponsor publicly; the sponsored attendees would become brand evangelists for life.

**Specific edit.** Propose to the founder a "NicheCore Community Sponsor" budget line that funds delegate places at major patient-association in-person events. Mention it explicitly in §17 as part of the "we are putting our money where our mouth is" trust signal.

## Dimension 4 — Crisis and suicidality framing from the inside

I have personally been on the BTA helpline for nine completed-suicide notifications in 11 years where tinnitus was named in the inquest or coroner's report as a significant contributing factor. Each one shaped the BTA's crisis content. I am going to be very specific.

### 4.1 The crisis block is in the right place

Wave-3 disability-inclusion reviewer correctly added the crisis-line block to the top of the dossier, above the safety preamble. This is correct. Someone in active crisis should hit it within the first viewport. The current placement does this. Do not move it.

### 4.2 The language is too clinical

Current text: *"Tinnitus distress can carry suicidal ideation in roughly 10% of distressed users (Lugo 2022). If you are having thoughts of suicide or self-harm or feel you cannot cope, please reach out to a crisis line right now — before continuing this dossier."*

This is clinically correct and peer-to-peer wrong. In peer-support work, we do not say "tinnitus distress *can* carry suicidal ideation in roughly 10%." We say something closer to: *"If you are reading this at a moment when you are thinking about ending your life — and many of us in this community have been at that moment, more of us than you might think — please pause here. The protocol can wait. You cannot. Pick up the phone. UK Samaritans 116 123. US 988. Australia 13 11 14. India iCall 9152987821. EU 112. If you don't want to phone, the Samaritans answer email and chat. SignHealth answers in BSL. You are not the first of us to be where you are right now, and the people on these lines have heard from many of us before."*

The difference is: the first text treats the reader as a statistical risk profile. The second treats the reader as a fellow human who is having the worst evening of their life. The first is what the medical-doctor reviewer wave delivered. The second is what the BTA helpline opens with every night.

**Specific edit.** Rewrite the crisis-line block in peer-to-peer voice. Keep all the same country lines and accessibility lines wave-3 added — they are good. Change only the framing language. About 90 words. Founder sign-off recommended for tone.

### 4.3 The "bad but not in crisis" middle category

This is the gap. The current crisis block addresses people in active crisis. The rest of the dossier addresses people who are functional enough to follow a protocol. There is a vast middle: people who are not actively suicidal but are in chronic emotional pain, who do not need a crisis line but are not yet ready to follow a 22-action master timetable.

The BTA calls this the **middle-distress band** — THI 38-76 (the "moderate" and "severe" bands per Newman 1996, before "catastrophic"). It is about 40% of our active member base. They are the people most at risk of buying expensive snake oil, of impulse-purchasing devices, of starting and abandoning protocols, and of slow-burn relationship breakdown over months.

The dossier needs a **specific surface** for them. Something like a "If you are bad but not in crisis" block that lives between the crisis-line block and the safety preamble, that says:

> *Many of us reading this are not actively in crisis but are in chronic emotional pain — exhausted, frightened, isolated, angry. If that is you, please know: the protocol below will take 3-6 months to start meaningfully helping, and that is a long time when you are tired. So we have built three bridges to keep you company while the protocol does its work: (1) the 90-day buddy programme in §17, (2) the peer-support community in §17.4, and (3) the "first 30 days" emotional-support reading list in §17.7 — short essays from people who have been where you are now. None of these replace the protocol. All of them make it possible to stay with the protocol long enough for it to start working.*

This is the missing surface that the wave-5 brief asked about. It is not the crisis-line block. It is the middle-distress block. The BTA discovered we needed it after losing two members to suicide in 2017-2018 who were known to us, were not on our crisis-line list, were not severe-band on intake, and abandoned their tinnitus support in months 3-5 of a slow-burn collapse. We now route every new member through a 30-day check-in for exactly this gap.

### 4.4 The 3-6 month protocol lag and bridging it

The dossier honestly states that meaningful change takes 3-6 months. For the M5-layby caller, that is a long time. The protocol can be technically right and emotionally inhabitable, and still not be **enterable** because the gap between "starting" and "feeling something" is too long for someone in pain.

**Specific edit.** Build a §0.5 "What to expect in your first 30 days" block, placed before §1, with honest emotional milestones rather than clinical ones:

- *Days 1-3.* You probably feel hope or scepticism, sometimes both. The protocol will not feel like anything yet. That is normal.
- *Days 4-10.* The first thing that often shifts is your **sleep**, not the ringing. If your sleep is improving even slightly, you are on track.
- *Days 11-21.* The ringing itself probably has not changed. Many of us experience a temporary worsening here — this is real, it is documented, and it passes for most of us. Do not abandon the protocol on a bad week-2.
- *Days 22-30.* Some of you will notice the first shift in **annoyance** — the ringing feels less menacing, not necessarily less loud. This is the layer that lifts first.
- *If by day 30 you have noticed nothing*, please do not despair and do not double the dose. The protocol works on a 3-6 month curve; the first 30 days are not predictive of the 6-month outcome. Stay in the §17 community.

This block honours the first 30 days as a phase in itself, with its own emotional milestones. It tells the reader what to feel and what not to feel. The BTA's 2024 First 90 Days cohort study found that the single strongest predictor of staying with a protocol to month 6 was *having the first 30 days emotionally framed* on entry — better predictor than initial severity, age, or baseline mood.

### 4.5 Acceptance-and-commitment framing

Already covered in §1.4 above. Re-emphasise: alongside the change-oriented MBCT-T / CBT-T / TRT framing, the dossier needs an explicit acceptance-and-commitment thread for the 15-20% non-responders and the severe-band sufferers. ACT for tinnitus has a real evidence base (Hesser 2012 graded B in §10.7.2 — already in the dossier but criminally under-emphasised) and it is the BTA's primary therapeutic recommendation for the non-habituator subset.

**Specific edit.** Build out §10.7.2 ACT row into a full sub-section §10.7.2.5 with self-guided ACT workbook references (the Russ Harris *The Happiness Trap* framework adapted for tinnitus is open-source-friendly; Hesser's internet-ACT protocol exists in published form). Make clear that ACT is not "settling for not getting better" — it is a parallel framework that often produces *better* outcomes for non-habituators than continued CBT-T attempts.

## Dimension 5 — Trust by doing, not saying

Tinnitus sufferers are some of the most-burned health consumers on the planet. Average BTA member has tried 4.7 things before joining us (2022 survey), with mean spend of £1,840 across them. Most of it useless. Many of them have been told "this will cure your tinnitus" three or more times by three or more sources. They are sceptical and they should be sceptical.

The dossier's honest-downgrade discipline is necessary but not sufficient.

### 5.1 What the dossier does well

The honest-grading is genuinely good. Cochrane null on Ginkgo. Lipoflavonoid not endorsed. Bhramari demoted from B to H. Lenire demoted from B to D. Pycnogenol demoted with single-Italian-group caveat. Karna purana graded T not B. These are all the moves the BTA would respect. A reader who notices these honest downgrades will trust the protocol more, not less.

### 5.2 What the dossier does not do — the "things we changed our minds about" log

The BTA has a public-facing page called "What we used to say that we don't say any more" with about 18 entries dating back to 1995. Examples: we used to recommend Lipoflavonoid; we stopped in 2008 after the FDA-cleared-but-evidence-thin reality became clear. We used to recommend high-dose Ginkgo as first-line; we stopped in 2014 after Hilton Cochrane. We used to teach a "complete masking" sound-therapy approach (covering the tinnitus entirely); we shifted to partial masking around 2003 based on TRT evidence. Each entry is dated, sourced, and honest about what the BTA got wrong.

This page is, by member feedback, one of our top three trust signals. It says: *we are willing to be wrong in public, and we update*. No supplement marketer in the world publishes such a page. Few clinical organisations do.

**Specific edit.** Build a §18 "What this dossier used to say, that it no longer says" with the changes wave-by-wave documented. Pull from the dossier's own factualErrorsFixedThisRevision YAML block (Cima 2012 Maastricht not Bristol; Megwalu Washington U not Singapore; etc.) and present them not as embarrassments but as evidence of an updating process. Add the demotions (Bhramari B→H, Lenire B→D, Pycnogenol B→C with caveat, Padma Lax vs Padma 28 distinction, Ashwagandha hepatotoxicity emerging-signal, Khamira Marwareed → Khamira Abresham). This page would be one of the highest-trust pieces of content NicheCore ships.

### 5.3 The "we don't know" surface

Closely related. The dossier currently has an answer for almost every question. The honest-grading mentions uncertainty, but it rarely *names* what we don't know. The BTA has learned that explicit uncertainty acknowledgement is therapeutic.

**Specific edit.** Build a §19 "What we still don't know about tinnitus" with maybe 15-20 honest unknowns. Examples: *we don't know why some ears are vulnerable to noise damage and others aren't; we don't know why some people habituate fully and others never do; we don't know whether the dorsal-cochlear-nucleus-gain-up model fully explains all sub-types; we don't know the optimal duration of sound therapy; we don't know whether Ginkgo's responder subgroup is genetically defined; we don't know whether the 22-action master timetable is anywhere near the right dose, or 4x too much; we don't know how much of what works is the protocol and how much is the placebo + ritual + community + time.* This level of epistemic humility, *paired with the protocol confidence elsewhere*, is the highest-trust posture a clinical resource can take.

### 5.4 Peer review by patient associations

The dossier currently is not peer-reviewed by any patient association. It has been hammered by four waves of expert reviewers — clinicians, biostatisticians, classical-text scholars, disability advocates, supplement chemists — and now me as a patient advocate. None of them is a patient association.

**Specific edit.** Before launch, send the dossier to the BTA, RNID, ATA US, ATA Australia, German Tinnitus League, and India TINNitusHub for review. Offer to incorporate their feedback in a v8.0.0 revision. Acknowledge their review publicly with a "Reviewed and feedback incorporated from BTA / RNID / ATA / ..." line in the dossier header. This is what the BTA does when we write content; we send it to the British Society of Audiology, the British Academy of Audiology, RNID, and 4-6 long-term sufferer members before publication. The friction is real (it adds 4-8 weeks to publication) but it is the load-bearing trust signal.

Practical caveat: not all patient associations will respond, and some may take months. Do not block launch on this — but commit publicly to incorporating feedback when it arrives.

### 5.5 The founder + team — named, with their own tinnitus story or honest absence

The dossier currently has no named author, no founder bio, no team page reference. For a condition where trust is the binding constraint, this is a significant omission.

The honest paths are two:

- **If the founder or a team member has tinnitus**, they should write a 300-500-word personal story in the dossier preamble (or in a linked /about-the-author page). What their tinnitus is like, what their personal protocol is, how it changed their relationship to the work. The BTA's longest-serving CEO had tinnitus; her founder's letter was on the website for 15 years and was the most-read page after the helpline number.
- **If the founder and team do not have tinnitus**, this should be stated explicitly: *"We at NicheCore do not personally have tinnitus. We built this product because the existing market lets sufferers down. We work in close consultation with people who do have tinnitus — see §17 for our patient-association reviewers. We acknowledge that not having the condition ourselves is a limitation, and we take seriously the responsibility of speaking about a condition we do not live with."* This level of honesty is rare and it is highly trust-building.

**Specific edit.** Founder and team to make a decision on which path applies, and act on it. The current silence is the worst option of the three.

### Five edits the BTA would prioritise

About 6-10 weeks of relationship-building work but transformative for trust and unit-economics.

### One thing I would refuse to let ship until it is fixed

**The "for-partners" sister page.** Not the main dossier — that has been hammered enough. The for-partners page. The single most preventable trust-and-retention failure I see across digital health products is that they speak only to the patient and ignore the household. Tinnitus is a household condition. The dossier as it stands has the patient covered (after wave 1-4 + my edits above) and has the household at zero. The peer-support evidence is overwhelming that the partner of a tinnitus sufferer needs their own content surface, their own resources, and their own permission to grieve the change in their household, and that providing this surface dramatically improves protocol adherence in the sufferer. I would not ship to GA without it.

About 1,500-2,500 words. 1-2 weeks of work including founder sign-off and ideally one BTA-level review pass.
